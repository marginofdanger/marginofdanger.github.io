1. Business description, model & unit economics

Zillow operates the most-used set of U.S. residential real-estate portals — Zillow, Trulia, StreetEasy, HotPads — and monetizes the consumer transaction intent that traffic represents by selling agents, property managers, lenders, and brand advertisers access to it. The end consumer (buyer, seller, renter) never pays; the consumer is the inventory. Roughly 235 million average monthly unique users and 9.6 billion visits in FY2025 are sold to the supply side of the market (10-K FY2025). The company estimates that about 80% of U.S. residential transactions involve an agent who uses at least one Zillow product❝ (10-K FY2025, Business). It reports a single operating segment evaluated on consolidated net income, with four disaggregated revenue categories.

The economics differ enough by category that each is built separately below.

The demand-driver tree — The core's ultimate exposure is U.S. existing-home transaction volume (near multi-decade lows, ~4.1M sales versus a ~6M "normalized" level) times the dollars Zillow extracts per transaction, not the "$3.5T housing market," the 235M-user audience, or the raw ~9.6B visit count the IR deck leads with. The crux: visits and transactions are both flat-to-down, so essentially all recent For Sale growth is monetization/share, while the genuine growth engine — Rentals — is penetration-led and transaction-cycle-independent.

1.1 Residential ($1,704M, 66%) — monetization of a flat audience

Zillow discloses one driver pair that ties to the dollar exactly: Residential revenue = visits × revenue per visit. That identity exposes the constraint the business now runs into.

2023 $1,452M ~9.0B $0.162 −5% — −5%
2024 $1,594M 9.31B $0.171 +10% +6% +4%
2025 $1,704M 9.59B $0.178 +7% +4% +3%
Q1'26 $450M 2.28B $0.198 +8% +12% −3%

Check: $0.178 × 9,593M = $1,708M ≈ reported $1,704M (10-K FY2025 MD&A). With visits flat to declining — up only 3% in FY2025 and down 3% year-over-year in Q1 2026 — essentially all Residential growth now comes from extracting more dollars per visit, a finite monetization lever rather than a traffic-compounding one❝ (10-Q 2026-Q1 MD&A). The lever pushing revenue per visit is conversion: the share of connections delivered through the higher-conversion "Enhanced Markets" experience rose from 21% (Q4 2024) to 44% (Q4 2025) to 49% (Q1 2026), against a stated target above 75% (Q4 2025 / Q1 2026 calls). Showcase rich-media listings rose from 1.7% to 3.7% to 4.3% of new listings over the same window, toward a 5–10% intermediate target.

Residential bundles five streams under one line: Premier Agent (agent advertising, the majority), Zillow Showcase (premium listing media), New Construction (homebuilder advertising), StreetEasy for-sale (NYC), and software (Follow Up Boss CRM, dotloop transaction management, ShowingTime scheduling). Premier Agent runs on two pricing models. Market-based pricing is a share-of-voice auction: an agent prepays a monthly amount in a ZIP code and receives connections in proportion to its share of total prepaid spend, recognized over the billing period (10-K FY2025, Note 2). Zillow Preferred (renamed from Flex in Q4 2025) is pay-when-you-close: the agent pays nothing up front and a performance fee on a closed transaction, generally within two years, recognized as variable consideration. Management states the two are "a bit of a wash" on realized take rate; Preferred is an alignment mechanism, not a price increase, and the real economic driver of Enhanced Markets is the attached Zillow Home Loans origination❝ (Goldman conference, Sept 2025).

The genuine unit economics below the visit are not externally verifiable. Zillow stopped disclosing Premier Agent advertiser counts, average revenue per advertiser, lead counts, and price per lead at the FY2023 single-segment recast. No supplier or customer filing constrains a per-lead price (agents are private individuals), and a "connection" is a data record with no capacity ceiling, so no Tier-2 triangulation is possible. The published revenue-per-visit residual fuses price and volume of an undisclosed paid unit, so an outsider cannot tell whether monetization gains are fewer agents paying more or more agents paying. The cost shape is high-margin software/advertising: the database of ~173 million homes, the Zestimate models, the apps, and the engineering organization are built once and serve all visits, so the marginal cost of an additional Residential dollar is small. Residential's own gross margin is almost certainly well above the consolidated 74.1%, because the FY2025 gross-margin step from 76% to 74% was driven by a Rentals cost, not a Residential one (see §1.2). The distinctive working-capital item is the contract-asset build from Zillow Preferred: contract assets rose to $230M at year-end 2025 from $157M, a 46% increase, because Zillow now recognizes revenue before cash collection at the agent's transaction close (10-K Note 15). The Flex-to-Preferred transition therefore flips Residential working capital from cash-positive (prepaid deferred revenue) to cash-negative (contract asset), a structural drag on cash conversion that scales with Preferred adoption.❝

1.2 Rentals ($630M, 24%) — penetration-led, the growth engine

Rentals is a two-sided advertising marketplace: Zillow aggregates rental listings, draws a renter audience of about 36 million average monthly visitors, and sells that audience to the supply side — multifamily property managers (the engine), longtail landlords, and renters who pay a flat application fee. Zillow's own decomposition reconciles to the dollar: 33M visitors × $4.77 quarterly revenue per visitor × 4 = $630M (10-K line 2304).

Rentals growth is monetization-led, not traffic-led: revenue per visitor rose 31% in FY2025 while visitors rose only 6%❝ (10-K FY2025) — the supply side is paying more per renter while the renter audience is roughly fixed. The two compounding levers are multifamily building count (about 76,000 of an estimated 140,000–150,000 buildings, roughly half-penetrated) and revenue per building (package-tier upgrades).❝ Multifamily generates an estimated $5,500–7,900 per property per year, roughly $460–660 a month [estimate: multifamily revenue $400–480M solved from the disclosed 58% multifamily growth and 39% total growth, divided by ~61,000 average property count] — a few dollars per unit per month for a 25-plus-unit building, which is why the package-upgrade motion has headroom.

The cost side is what makes Rentals the company's current margin drag rather than a high-margin flywheel. The FY2025 gross-margin decline from 76% to 74% was driven "primarily due to increased lead acquisition costs associated with our Redfin rentals syndication agreement," a Rentals cost of $87M and the single largest cost increase in the company❝ (10-K line 2519, 2522). In February 2025 Zillow paid Redfin $100M upfront, capitalized as a customer-relationship intangible amortized over nine years at about $11M a year, to become the exclusive multifamily-listings provider on Redfin's network, plus an ongoing per-lead fee for five years with two two-year extensions, which is the $87M in cost of revenue. Rentals revenue is high-incremental-margin at the platform level because an extra ad impression costs near nothing, but Zillow is deliberately buying incremental renter demand and that purchased traffic comes with a real per-lead cost, so the current margin reflects a demand purchase that has not yet been harvested. Management guides this variable cost to "neutral to EBITDA margins" by year-end 2026 as the Redfin comp laps and the dedicated rental sales force matures.

1.3 Mortgages ($199M, 8%) — the fastest-inflecting line

Zillow Home Loans (ZHL) is a gain-on-sale originator licensed in 49 states plus D.C.: it originates purchase loans, then sells substantially all loans and the associated servicing rights to GSEs and whole-loan investors. Revenue ≈ origination volume × blended gain-on-sale-plus-fee rate. Refinance is about 0.7% of volume, so this is effectively a purchase-money business tied to home transactions, not the refi cycle that whipsawed the legacy business in 2020–2022.

In Q1 2026 ZHL grew purchase origination volume 96% year-over-year while the industry purchase-mortgage market declined about 1%, so essentially all of the growth is share captured from Zillow's own funnel rather than a market tailwind❝ (Q1 2026 call). 1.4 Other ($50M, 2%) — harvested display

Other is display advertising: graphical web and mobile brand ads sold on a CPM/CPC basis to home-and-shelter advertisers, recognized as impressions are delivered with no contract, backlog, or escalator. The five-year record went nowhere, rising to $50M in FY2021, declining to a $40M trough in FY2023, and returning to $50M in FY2025, flat year-over-year at $11M in Q1 2026, a 0% four-year CAGR. Management has not discussed display on a single earnings call from FY2023 through Q1 2026, and the Feb-2026 deck gives it a single residual cell with no revenue-driver description, unlike every other category [deck: 2026-02-10, slide 10]. It is high-margin, near-100% incremental because the inventory rides traffic Zillow runs anyway, but it is immaterial to growth and management neither grows nor harvests it.

Consolidated operating leverage

The realized incremental margin is the operating-leverage test. From FY2024 to FY2025 operating income improved by $164M, from a $198M loss to a $34M loss, on $347M of additional revenue, a 47% incremental operating margin that is the company's best and reflects a high-fixed-cost, capital-light structure.❝ On Adjusted EBITDA the incremental margin was 36% ($124M on $347M), the gap reflecting falling SBC and absolute declines in general and administrative cost as 2022 equity-award actions rolled off. This 47% is consolidated, flattered by high-margin Residential mix and dragged by Rentals' Redfin cost; a clean Residential incremental margin is undisclosed. Management frames a mid-cycle target of $5B revenue at 45% Adjusted EBITDA margin, up from 24.1% in FY2025, which would require sustained incremental EBITDA margins above 50%, well above the 36% FY2025 actual, and is conditioned on a housing-market normalization to roughly 6 million existing-home sales that has not arrived❝ (see §14, §17).

2. Key technologies & technical differentiators

Zillow is best understood as a data-and-distribution company that uses AI/ML as a feature, not as a deep-technology company. The 224 issued patents and the Zestimate are real but are not what stops a competitor: CoStar outspent Zillow on technology and still lost the for-sale monetization battle.❝ What stops a competitor is the audience, around 80% of it direct traffic, and the data that compounds from it. Five technology assets matter, ranked below by how much excess return each actually defends.

The living database and Zestimate. A "living database of approximately 173 million U.S. homes," fed by property/transaction/listing data plus user updates to more than 46 million records, is the substrate for every model (10-K FY2025). The Zestimate is a patented automated valuation model with a median error of 1.8% on listed homes and 7.2% off-market (10-K FY2025). The model itself is commoditized — every large portal, the GSEs, CoreLogic, and ICE run AVMs, and Redfin historically claimed a lower on-market error. What is hard to replicate is the data feedstock and the rich-media content that increasingly feeds it. The Zestimate is free, so it shows up financially as audience rather than as a price premium: it is why "more people search for 'Zillow' than 'real estate'" (10-K, 2025 Google Trends). It also creates standing liability, since a risk factor warns that disputes over Zestimate accuracy "may harm our business," and FY2025 adds the risk of proposed legislation requiring disclosure of proprietary algorithms.

Zillow Showcase / rich-media content. Premium listing media — 3D tours, interactive floor plans, virtual staging, SkyTour drone video — that does two jobs: a better shopping experience and proprietary data capture that "makes our Zestimates better" (AI Summit, 2026-03-24). Adoption reached "nearly 10% of new for-sale listings" with proprietary rich media and more than doubled year-over-year; Showcase-specific penetration is 4.3% of new listings in Q1 2026 (broad rich-media penetration of ~10% is the wider figure). Showcase is sold, so unlike the Zestimate it supports a realized price (ASP undisclosed), and management cites it helping homes sell for about 2% more and agents win 30% more listings [deck: 2025-02-11, slide 18]. The camera technology is buildable in a year; getting it onto a meaningful share of listings through the agent channel is a multi-year distribution problem.

The transaction-rails software stack. Acquired-and-integrated workflow software that runs the back office of the transaction. ShowingTime facilitates 90% of U.S. in-person tours; 100,000 agents use Follow Up Boss daily; dotloop is involved in roughly half of all U.S. transactions❝ (AI Summit, 2026-03-24) — near-utility positions in the agent workflow that a new entrant would have to displace tool-by-tool against entrenched habit. None is technically deep, and the two most important (ShowingTime 2021, Follow Up Boss 2023) were bought. The defensibility is market position and integration, and the strongest observable evidence is conversion lift: real-time-touring customers convert at 3x the rate of normal customers and ZHL pre-approved customers at 5x, which feeds Premier Agent monetization.❝ Switching costs remain low formally, because agents run multiple CRMs and portals, so the moat holds only while Zillow delivers the best ROI.

The AI/LLM layer. AI Mode (a conversational LLM search experience) is live for about 5% of the audience as of Q1 2026; Zillow was among the first real-estate apps integrated into ChatGPT (October 2025); Follow Up Boss has AI features. Management's thesis is that a vertically integrated real-estate AI beats a horizontal LLM because only Zillow has the content (173M homes plus rich media), the context (saved homes, tours, budgets, agent messages), and the integration (the transaction rails) — and that generic LLMs cannot answer fiduciary questions ("how much is my home worth exactly? which offer should I accept?") without a brokerage license, which Zillow holds in all 50 states. The weakness is that every component of "content + context + integration" is a pre-existing data/distribution asset, the partner LLM does the heavy model lifting, and there is no disclosed monetization or retention uplift from AI yet, so the case rests on the underlying data moat rather than on the AI layer itself.

Substitution threat. The same horizontal LLMs Zillow partners with are the primary substitution risk: if ChatGPT/Gemini-class agents become the front door to home search, Zillow's roughly 80%-direct traffic — a habit, not a contract — could be intermediated and its lead-generation monetization compressed.❝ Roadmap exposure is high: the 2026-plus growth narrative leans on early-stage products (Zillow Pro, in beta by Q1 2026; Zillow Preview pre-market listings, from 5 to more than 60 broker partners in two months, jointly with Realtor.com; AI Mode at 5%), none with a disclosed revenue contribution. Against the cautionary iBuying precedent, the post-2021 record of shipping the integration roadmap on schedule, plus "40% more code shipped per engineer 2023→2025" [deck: 2026-03-24, slide 21], is the counterweight.

3. Competitive advantages & moat assessment

Zillow's advantage differs by category, and the single-segment reporting means none of it can be confirmed in a segment margin or ROIC, so the moat is assessed from share, monetization, and unit economics rather than from a returns premium. One fact governs this whole section: Zillow has not earned its cost of capital on a GAAP basis over the decade.❝ Operating income was negative every continuing year except the Homes-distorted 2020–2021 and only reached −1.3% of revenue in FY2025, and GAAP ROIC was negative or near zero throughout. The respectable ~15–17% return appears only on an Adjusted-EBITDA-over-invested-capital basis, which adds back $390–451M of annual stock-based compensation, 15–23% of revenue, as if it were free and treats $2.8B of acquisition goodwill as sunk. The moat case below therefore rests on unit economics and an inflecting margin trajectory, not on a demonstrated multi-year returns premium.

Residential — real and currently durable, with a contested foundation

Zillow's Residential business holds a real and presently widening competitive advantage — a consumer-attention aggregator network effect — that is strengthening on monetization but facing a genuine, unresolved threat to its demand foundation; it is real but contestable, not a structural fortress.❝ The advantage is that Zillow owns the largest, highest-intent, lowest-acquisition-cost for-sale audience in U.S. residential real estate, and converts that attention into agent advertising dollars no rival matches. The pricing power is visible and intensifying: with visits flat to down, revenue per visit rose from $0.162 (FY2023) to $0.198 (Q1 2026), up 22% cumulatively. The decisive comparison is monetization rather than traffic: Zillow's roughly $1.7B Residential revenue is about 3x Realtor.com/Move's entire ~$0.55B and roughly 17x Homes.com's ~$100M run-rate, and that gap has not closed despite CoStar spending well over $1B over three years to attack it.❝

Three things keep it short of the structural tier and one threatens to pull it down a tier. First, the moat is on consumer attention, but the inputs that produce it, listings and search traffic, are contestable: for-sale listings flow through the same 500-plus MLSs to every portal, NAR weakened Clear Cooperation in 2025, and Zillow had to relax its own Listing Access Standards in March 2026 to settle a Compass antitrust suit. Second, the largest threat sits one layer up the stack, in AI search disintermediation. The FY2025 10-K newly names AI search as a risk that could "reduce traffic to traditional online real estate platforms," and the stock declined roughly 53% from its 2025 high into early 2026 partly on this concern. Third, returns are unproven: there is no Residential margin, and consolidated returns only just crossed break-even. Management frames the integrated-transaction depth as an AI-proof moat, while the external view and the 2026 stock action say the search-traffic foundation is now exposed. The evidence sides with the external view on where the risk sits, at the discovery layer upstream, while agreeing that the workflow embedding is a real and deepening defense for the agent relationship that a top-of-funnel AI answer does not replicate.

Rentals — real, widening on share, not yet visible in margin

Zillow Rentals has a real advantage that is widening on the dimension that matters, renter audience and share, but is not yet structural and not yet visible in margin; it sits between the contestable and structural tiers and is moving toward the structural end.❝ The advantage is a demand-side two-sided-marketplace network effect anchored on the renter audience (about 36 million monthly visitors, more than 2x the daily active app users of the nearest competitor, ~80% direct traffic). Rentals grew 39% in FY2025 and 42% in Q1 2026, roughly 2.5x the growth of CoStar's rentals business, and multifamily listing share rose from 54% (2024) to 63% (2025).❝ Three things keep it short of fully structural. The advantage is on demand rather than supply, and supply is contestable, since there is no MLS in rentals and Zillow had to buy breadth with the $100M Redfin payment; there is no segment margin, and the rentals push currently compresses consolidated gross margin; and the same Redfin exclusivity that widened the moat is the subject of the FTC/state-AG antitrust suit. The most powerful comparison is the competitor's economics: CoStar runs its entire Residential Real Estate segment at a negative Adjusted EBITDA of $(230)M in FY2025 (improved from $(361)M in FY2024) because Homes.com losses swamp Apartments.com's profits❝ (CoStar 10-K FY2025), while Zillow funds its rentals push out of a profitable, cash-generative base. Zillow is the structurally healthier combatant.

Mortgages — real but contestable, distribution not product

ZHL has a real but contestable advantage that comes from distribution rather than product: it acquires a mortgage customer almost free because the borrower is already inside Zillow's funnel, searching homes, sized by BuyAbility (4.3M enrolled users by Q1 2026), and matched to a Zillow agent.❝ The CFO is explicit that "in a more commoditized product like mortgage, brand and distribution tend to do quite well" (Q4 2025 call). Four things keep it out of the structural tier. A conforming mortgage is a GSE-standardized commodity sold on rate and fees; ZHL sells the servicing rights away and keeps no recurring annuity, where Rocket retains servicing on ~92% of loans; there is no margin evidence and the segment is sub-scale and probably unprofitable today; and the integrated-funnel thesis is directly contested by a much bigger rival, since Rocket assembled the exact stack in 2025 by acquiring Redfin (portal plus brokerage) and Mr. Cooper (the largest U.S. servicer).❝ The advantage is real as a future prospect, not yet a demonstrated returns engine.

Other — no durable advantage

Other/display has no durable competitive advantage of its own. The display product does not differentiate — sold on CPM/CPC with no contract, no switching cost, no pricing power against fungible open-market inventory (Google, Meta, programmatic).❝ What returns it earns are a byproduct of Zillow's audience moat, not a defended position in the display market; it is a price-taker.

4. Competitive landscape & market sizing

Residential competes in two arenas with different structures: the consumer-search/portal arena (eyeballs — concentrated, network-effect-driven, Zillow #1) and the agent-monetization arena (where the dollars are — fragmented, because an agent's marketing budget has many substitutes: rival portals, Google/Meta ads, brokerage-supplied leads, yard signs, referrals).❝ Zillow is dominant in arena one but arena one is a means to arena two; its defensibility rests on translating attention dominance into agent-spend share faster than rivals can offer a cheaper lead. The downstream industry is among the most fragmented in the U.S. economy: about 4.8 million homes sold in 2025 served by over 300,000 brokerages and over 70,000 lenders across more than 500 MLSs❝ (10-K FY2025). No agent or brokerage has buyer power over Zillow; conversely, 300,000 fragmented brokerages cannot collectively bargain its pricing down. Zillow sets price via the share-of-voice auction; the agent is the price-taker.

Market sizing, checked

Zillow's TAM slide totals $202B, of which the $108B "residential real estate transaction fees" component is the largest. That figure is total agent commissions, the money Zillow's customers earn rather than a market Zillow sells into, derived from NAR TTV times internal commission rates (10-K, footnote 13). The honest Residential denominator is agent lead-generation spend. A bottom-up triangulation of roughly 1.5 million NAR members, a minority actively buying leads, spending $1,000–3,000 a month across all channels with about a third to portals, gives a $4–8B for-sale lead-gen pool [estimate: marketing-pool, medium confidence; active-agent count and portal allocation are the soft inputs]. Against that, Zillow Residential ($1.7B) already captures roughly 28% of the midpoint. Residential growth must therefore come from monetization penetration, with revenue per TTV rising from 8.5 bps, rather than from a vast untapped market, a tighter ceiling than the $108B slide implies.❝ The genuinely under-penetrated pools are Rentals (multifamily building count and package upgrades) and mortgage attach.

5. Value chain & profit pools

Zillow sits at the intersection of three distinct chains: for-sale (MLS → portal → agent → buyer/seller), mortgage (warehouse lender → ZHL → GSE), and rentals (property manager → portal → renter, no MLS). It is a toll on transaction intent, not a principal in the transaction — it captures only 8.5 basis points of the $2.2 trillion of value transacting across its platform❝ (10-K FY2025).

The profit pool is split in two, and Zillow sits on the profitable side. Take one ~$400K home sale generating roughly $19,600 of commission at the ~4.9% blended rate Zillow's own TAM math implies:

The brokerage layer grosses the giant commission dollar but earns near-zero-to-negative net margin: Compass grossed $267B of gross transaction value and $6.96B of revenue but ran a net loss of $(58.5)M and passes 81.6% of revenue straight to independent-contractor agents (Compass 10-K FY2025); Zillow captures a tiny fraction of the transaction value at a ~74% blended gross margin and an inflecting operating margin.❝ The toll-taker keeps far more of its smaller take than the principal keeps of its larger one. Margin is migrating toward the asset-light portal layer (Zillow's first GAAP profit, +47% incremental operating margin) and away from the asset-heavy brokerage layer (Compass net loss; the power inside that layer sits with the individual agent, not the brokerage).

In the mortgage chain Zillow is sandwiched between two counterparties with structural power over it: upstream, warehouse lenders fund the loan on mostly-uncommitted lines (they can decline to advance in a stress, the binding constraint on ZHL growth); downstream, the GSEs are the exit, and the entire revenue model depends on the secondary market staying open.❝ In the rentals chain Zillow is unusually buying upstream demand: the $87M per-lead payment to Redfin is the single largest cost increase in the company and the sole driver of the 76%-to-74% gross-margin compression, a vertical-integration-by-contract move that trades current margin to lock up renter demand and remove the #3 ILS competitor.

What actually constrains the chain. The for-sale chain's binding constraint is access to MLS listings, and it is live: NAR weakened its Clear Cooperation rule in 2025, Zillow had to relax its own Listing Access Standards to settle the Compass suit, and the private/pocket-listing trend is growing — any of which can throttle the flow of listings that drives portal traffic. But listing access constrains only the for-sale portal, not the whole company. Two broader ceilings bind harder today: the housing cycle itself — existing-home sales of roughly 4 million are near multi-decade lows and cap For Sale and Mortgages revenue no matter how much share Zillow gains — and the capacity of Zillow Home Loans' warehouse credit lines, which sets how fast ZHL can originate.

Almost nothing in the chain is circular. No supplier or customer locks Zillow in with prepayments, and Zillow's own purchase commitments are small (~$260M, mostly cloud hosting, data and content licensing, and the Redfin deal). The one quasi-circular flow is the Redfin partnership: Zillow pays Redfin for renter demand that it then monetizes — a contract, not an equity stake, and the subject of the FTC suit. The only balance-sheet financing inside the model is internal and self-directed. Under Zillow Preferred (pay-on-close), Zillow fronts the agent's marketing cost and collects up to two years later; that builds a growing contract asset and flips Residential working capital cash-negative as Preferred scales. It is Zillow financing its own customers, not an external dependency.

6. Secular & industry trends

Almost all of Zillow's recent growth is share and monetization gain into a depressed transaction market rather than a market tailwind. Over three years (2022→2025) For Sale revenue grew 16% while existing-home sales declined 19% — roughly 35 points of cycle-independent outperformance — so essentially 100% of recent growth is secular, with the cyclical recovery sitting as unexercised upside management says it is not counting on❝ (Q4 2025 call). The cleanest single number showing the growth is share rather than market is For Sale revenue per TTV, which rose to 8.5 bps in FY2025 from a recast 7.9 bps; the FY2024 recast cut the prior-year figure about 22%, so the direction is like-for-like but pre-FY2025 absolute levels are not comparable. Independent competitor financials corroborate the share story rather than only management's math: Residential is ~3x Move and ~17x Homes.com, and Zillow multifamily grew 57–63% per quarter against Apartments.com at about 11%.

The three end-markets have different secular slopes. For-sale transactions are cyclical and at a trough (4.06 million existing-home sales in 2024, slowest since 1995; ~4.1 million in 2025, roughly a third below the 6-million "normalized" level management cites).❝ Zillow's audience penetration is near a ceiling (~70–80% of buyers/sellers use Zillow), so the for-sale growth lever is monetization per transaction and attach, not more eyeballs. Rentals is the cleanest secular story because it is penetration-led, not transaction-cycle-led — about half the multifamily building universe is still un-onboarded, and revenue per building is rising — and it is structurally less cyclical (renting is counter-cyclical to buying; multifamily ad demand actually rose during the for-sale freeze on higher vacancy).❝ Mortgage origination is a derivative of for-sale volume plus a rising attach rate, currently an attach story (purchase volume +53% FY2025 against a flat purchase market), not a market story.

The cyclical option is large and unexercised. Management sizes a normalization to 6 million existing-home sales at roughly $1.3B of incremental For Sale revenue, and states FY2025 EBITDA margin "could have been mid- to high-30%" at a normal 6-million environment versus the 24% reported.❝ The mid-cycle $5B/45% target capitalizes that recovery; the near-year consensus does not, and the street has been removing recovery slope (CY2028 EPS cut ~10% over a year — see §14).

The slope can shift in either direction. AI search is the genuine two-sided risk, because the FY2025 10-K newly names it as potentially "reducing traffic to traditional online real estate platforms," yet the leading KPIs had not broken at the annual level (FY2025 visits +3%, users +6%), though both inflected to −3% YoY in Q1 2026 (see §18). Listing access (Clear Cooperation erosion, private listings) is the binding regulatory slope risk to the portal model. The NAR commission settlement is a slope risk to agent marketing budgets that has not bitten yet. The FTC/state-AG challenge to the Redfin partnership is a Rentals-specific slope risk. The KPIs that would flag a secular break first, in order: visits and visits-per-user (the AI-search alarm); For Sale revenue per TTV stalling; Enhanced Markets connection share stalling below 75%; Showcase adoption stalling below 5–10%; Rentals listing share / multifamily count plateauing.

7. Critical points

  1. The business is at a GAAP operating-profitability inflection. Operating loss narrowed from $(270)M (FY2023) to $(34)M (FY2025) and turned positive at +$36M in Q1 2026; net income of $46M in that one quarter exceeded all of FY2025's $23M. The exit run-rate is positive and accelerating, so a forward model should not anchor on the FY2025 −1.3% operating margin. FY2025's full-year GAAP profit was investment-income-assisted ($77M of interest on the cash pile against a $34M operating loss); the operating line crossed zero only in Q1 2026.

  2. Gross margin is in a structural decline that runs opposite to operating margin. Consolidated gross margin declined from 85% (FY2021) to 74.1% (FY2025) to 73.3% (Q1 2026), driven by the growth mix, namely Rentals' Redfin lead-acquisition cost ($87M, the single largest cost increase) and Mortgages mix. This cuts against the "high-margin internet portal" framing, because the very categories driving growth dilute gross margin even as opex leverage lifts operating margin, and that tension is what any model has to reconcile.

  3. Stock-based compensation is the dominant cost and the real mechanism of the GAAP inflection. SBC ran $390–451M a year, 15–23% of revenue, larger than the entire FY2025 operating loss and about 17x FY2025 GAAP net income. The GAAP turn owes more to SBC declining as a share of revenue (23.2% in 2023 to 15.1% in 2025) than to revenue growth.❝ Counting SBC as the economic cost it is, the company has not earned its cost of capital over the decade (see §3).

  4. All Residential growth now comes from monetizing a flat-to-shrinking audience. Visits were +3% in FY2025 and −3% YoY in Q1 2026, so the entire +8% Residential growth in Q1 2026 came from revenue per visit (+12%), driven by Enhanced Markets conversion heading toward a hard 75% connection-share ceiling. This is a finite lever applied to a funnel that is no longer growing, the durability question the AI-search risk sharpens (see §8).

  5. Rentals is the growth engine but the current margin drag. Rentals grew 39% (FY2025) and 42% (Q1 2026), the entire growth story alongside flat Residential, and is ~50% penetrated of the multifamily building universe. But it incurs the Redfin cost that compressed gross margin, and the $100M exclusivity that fuels its share gains is the subject of the FTC/state-AG suit (motion to dismiss denied May 7, 2026; trial set August 2026). It is sensitivity-driver #1 in the model and cannot be double-counted with a gross-margin recovery.

  6. Mortgages is the fastest-inflecting line off a small base. Q1 2026 revenue grew 56% and purchase volume 96% against an industry purchase market that declined about 1%, pure share gain off Zillow's funnel. But the blended take rate is compressing (4.65% to 4.19%), revenue lags volume, the segment is sub-scale and probably unprofitable, and the integrated-funnel thesis is directly contested by a far larger Rocket.

  7. Capital structure flipped from convert-funded to cash-rich and debt-free. The entire convertible-note stack (peak ~$1.6B) was retired by May 2025, leaving zero corporate funded debt, ~$1.3B net cash, and an undrawn $500M revolver. Buybacks turned genuinely price-sensitive in Q1 2026 ($626M at ~$46, 2.5x the prior-year pace at ~35% lower price; authorization raised to $4.8B). The balance sheet does not add to the stress case (see §8 for the buyback-price variant).

  8. Litigation moved from a bystander risk to a named-defendant stack. In 2025 Zillow became a named defendant in suits by the FTC, five state AGs, Compass, CoStar, and consumer plaintiffs, the single largest year-over-year risk escalation in the filings. The FTC/Redfin trial is a dated August-2026 catalyst on the fastest-growing 24% of revenue, and legal cost already cut Q2 2026 EBITDA guidance below the street (see §18).

8. Variant perceptions

Each point below cuts against a consensus or management framing, with the qualification stated as substance.

  1. "Zillow is a high-margin internet portal." Consolidated gross margin declined 11 points in four years (85% to 74%), and the FY2025 decline from 76% to 74% is entirely the Rentals-related Redfin lead-acquisition cost ($87M), with Mortgages mix adding to the pressure. The $87M and the 76%-to-74% decline are in the 10-K, and the 47% incremental operating margin holds. The part that overstates the case is a high Residential standalone margin, which is undisclosed under single-segment reporting and only inferred from the cost shape, so this dossier states Residential's own margin as "almost certainly above the 74% blend" rather than as a number.

  2. "The integrated-transaction depth makes Zillow AI-proof." The moat's foundation is consumer search traffic, which is exactly what AI answer-engines threaten to disintermediate, upstream of all the transaction depth. Visits are already flat-to-declining (−3% YoY in Q1 2026), so the top of funnel is not growing even before AI bites; the FY2025 10-K added AI search as a traffic risk; the market repriced the stock ~53% lower on this in 2026. The transaction depth is a real defense for the agent relationship, but it does not protect the consumer entry point where the value originates.

  3. "Homes.com / CoStar is closing in on Zillow's for-sale position." After more than $1B and three years, Homes.com revenue is ~$100M versus Zillow Residential's $1.7B — a ~17x gap that has not closed; CoStar's reported "Residential" +32% in Q1 2026 bundles Apartments.com (rentals) and Australian Domain with U.S. Homes.com, and an activist is pressing CoStar to exit residential. The for-sale monetization threat is far weaker than the headline CoStar segment growth implies.

  4. "The $108–202B TAM gives Zillow a huge runway." The $108B is agent commissions — money paid to the downstream agent, not an addressable pool Zillow can capture. The real Residential pool is agent lead-gen spend, triangulated at ~$4–8B, of which Zillow already captures ~28%. With visits flat, growth must come from revenue-per-transaction penetration (8.5 bps and rising), a tighter ceiling than the slide implies [estimate: marketing-pool, medium confidence].

  5. "Buybacks are routine return of capital." The repurchasing in Q1 2026 was genuinely price-sensitive and countercyclical, $626M at ~$46, 2.5x the prior-year dollar pace at ~35% lower price, with the authorization raised to $4.8B as the stock declined. The 2025 tranche, though, was pro-cyclical: $670M deployed at ~$70–73 near the post-iBuying high with the stock now ~$32, a paper loss of roughly $300–390M on that year's buys [estimate: 9.0M shares × ($70.5 − $32), bounds $300–390M]. The program is therefore partly disciplined, not uniformly opportunistic.

  6. "FY2025 GAAP profitability proves the model works." The per-share quality is thin: SBC of $390M (15% of revenue) is ~17x the $23M of net income, ~10.6 million shares a year are still issued to employees, and the profit exists only because investment income offsets a $34M operating loss. The profit is real but driven by the cash hoard and convert-interest relief, not yet by operating leverage clearing the SBC drag — the operating line crossed zero only in Q1 2026.

  7. "The mortgage business adds balance-sheet risk." The warehouse is non-recourse to Zillow Group, over-collateralized, and self-liquidating in days, so it cannot reach equity holders. The genuine risk is growth-capping: the "primarily uncommitted" capacity can shrink in a dislocation, throttling ZHL's origination growth.

10. Management, incentives & capital allocation

Leadership and incentives

Zillow is founder-controlled with a three-headed top structure: CEO Jeremy Wacksman (a 15-year insider promoted from COO in August 2024) plus two co-founder Co-Executive Chairmen, Rich Barton (CEO 2004–10 and 2019–24) and Lloyd Frink. The bench is deeply internal and low-turnover (CTO Beitel since 2005). The board is collegial and founder-aligned — the compensation committee that sets founder pay is chaired by a 20-year investor-director (Jay Hoag of TCV) and includes a former founder-era executive, with a disclosed web of cross-investments among directors.

Zillow pays executives with salary plus equity only: no cash bonus, no formal short-term incentive plan, and no performance-conditioned equity. All NEO equity is time-vested options and RSUs on four-year quarterly vesting, granted in non-voting Class C so management pay does not dilute founder voting control. The proxy states plainly that "neither the Board nor the compensation committee considers any specific financial measures in making compensation decisions for our NEOs." This aligns pay structurally to per-share value, since there is no revenue, EBITDA, or size metric to game and the only payoff is the share price plus staying employed, with two soft spots: nothing connects pay to operating results or relative TSR, and a triennial say-on-pay vote last passed with a soft ~78% in a founder-controlled company.

Execution was genuinely countercyclical in 2022, spending $947M near the $29–40 lows, and in Q1 2026, adding $1.25B to the authorization and buying $626M at ~$46 as the stock broke down, but pro-cyclical in 2025, $670M at ~$70–73 at the post-iBuying high with the stock now ~$32, so "opportunistic" overstates the record. The deeper per-share point is that despite $2.6B of buybacks over 2021–2025, basic shares rose from ~234M to ~242M because repurchases were mostly mopping up more than $400M a year of SBC, and the share count began declining only in late 2025 and Q1 2026 once SBC declined. Per-share FCF was therefore negative-to-negligible for most of 2014–2023, and a real per-share-FCF engine appeared only in 2024–2026. The company's own Adjusted FCF metric (first reported Q3 2025, $420M FY2025) flatters this by adding back net warehouse borrowings and the $100M Redfin payment, where a stricter OCF-minus-all-capex FCF was ~$101M in FY2025. The stated framework of investing in growth, holding a capital reserve, keeping M&A flexibility, buying back opportunistically, and paying no dividend matches the revealed record everywhere except the 2025 buyback price.

11. M&A, divestitures & deal track record

Zillow is a serial acquirer with a split record. Its bolt-on software/marketplace M&A (StreetEasy, dotloop, ShowingTime, Follow Up Boss) has been integrated, is still in the product portfolio, and has produced no goodwill impairments — a competent bolt-on record. But the two largest capital commitments in company history scaled and then reversed: the ~$2.0–2.5B Trulia stock merger (2015), whose flagship brand was written down $69M in 2018 and never delivered the consolidation economics promised, and Zillow Offers/the Homes segment, an internally built iBuying business that scaled to $6.0B of revenue in 2021 then was wound down with $408M of inventory write-downs and a 25% workforce cut. Goodwill was never impaired across the period ($1,931M in 2017 to $2,823M in 2025), but that partly reflects how the loss landed: the Homes segment held zero goodwill, so the largest value destruction in company history bypassed the goodwill line entirely. The honest grade is a disciplined, well-integrated bolt-on acquirer wrapped around a company that twice made enormous, ultimately reversed strategic bets, and the open question is whether the appetite for transformational bets is retired or merely paused.

18. Risks

Severity-ranked; mechanism, sized exposure, and leading indicator each.

1. AI / search disintermediation of the traffic funnel. Mechanism: Zillow monetizes transaction intent captured as traffic, so if Google inserts native for-sale listings or horizontal LLMs answer listing and affordability questions without sending the user to Zillow, the impressions and connections Zillow can monetize shrink. Exposure: about 90% of revenue is traffic-monetization. Leading indicator, already moving: visits and average monthly unique users both inflected to −3% YoY in Q1 2026 (220M against 227M users; 2,276M against 2,354M visits), a sign flip from FY2025's +6%/+3% even as revenue still grew 18%, which means Zillow is monetizing a shrinking audience harder, the pattern early disintermediation produces. The Dec-15-2025 Google-listings-test headline dropped the stock ~8–11% intraday [web: GeekWire/CNBC]. Mitigation: ~80% direct traffic, the content/context/integration thesis, ~5% SEM dependence.

2. FTC + 5 state-AG antitrust suit over the Redfin rentals partnership (dated catalyst). Mechanism: the FTC and AGs of VA, AZ, CT, NY, and WA sued over the Feb 2025 exclusivity ($100M payment); the motion to dismiss was denied May 7, 2026, the trial is set for August 2026, and plaintiffs seek injunctive relief that could unwind the exclusive feed driving multifamily growth (+57% YoY). Exposure: the fastest-growing 24% of revenue, with legal cost already cutting Q2 2026 EBITDA guidance below the street. Leading indicator: the August trial, and multifamily property count and revenue-per-visitor if the feed is restricted. The dollar exposure of an adverse remedy is bounded only qualitatively, with no number on the share of Rentals reach that depends on the Redfin syndication. [web]

3. MLS / listing-data access (the structural binding constraint). Mechanism: listings are the content that drives traffic, access is governed by 500+ MLS rulebooks terminable with limited notice, and competitors can withhold listings on private networks. The fight runs on two fronts, Zillow's Listing Access Standards and the Compass/MRED feed dispute where Zillow is suing to keep feeds open, with Compass and CoStar in turn suing over the Standards. Exposure: near-total ("loss or degradation of this listings data could … severely harm our business"). Leading indicator: MLS feed-cutoff actions, private-listing-network adoption, and the share of new listings on Zillow within one day.

4. IP / antitrust suits from Compass and CoStar (named defendant). Mechanism: suits alleging antitrust, IP, consumer-protection, and RESPA claims attack the Listing Access Standards and Preview. Exposure: legal cost plus injunctive relief; these suits do not yet appear in the Legal Proceedings footnote, which signals management does not view them as reasonably-possible-material yet. Leading indicator: promotion of these suits into the footnote, any injunction, and the legal-expense opex line.

5. Housing-cycle / rate exposure (cyclical, diminishing). Mechanism: For Sale and Mortgages volume is capped by existing-home transactions, throttled by rates, inventory, and affordability. Exposure: For Sale is 74% of revenue, but the company has decoupled, with revenue growing 18% in Q1 2026 against a flat market via share gains, so the risk is real but diminishing. Leading indicator: existing-home sales and TTV, and For Sale revenue per TTV.

6. Mortgage warehouse-funding + rate sensitivity. Mechanism: ZHL funds on $600M of mostly-uncommitted warehouse lines with margin-call provisions, so a capacity squeeze caps origination growth. Exposure: 8% of revenue but the fastest-growing, and non-recourse to the parent, so the risk caps growth rather than threatening solvency. Leading indicator: facility capacity and utilization, gain-on-sale margin swings, and FHA/HUD standing.

7. Zillow Preferred variable-consideration / earnings-quality risk. Mechanism: pay-when-you-close revenue is estimated on conversion and "may be reversed," so a growing share of the largest revenue line rests on estimates that can revise. Exposure: contract assets $230M (from $157M), with a ~5-month recognition lag. Leading indicator: contract-asset growth against revenue, and any disclosed estimate revisions. This is also the source of the cash-conversion drag flagged in §17, so projected FCF inherits the uncertainty.

8. Legacy Zillow Offers securities litigation (tail, contained). The Federal Securities Suit proceeds, with class certification affirmed and en-banc rehearing denied Jan 2026, loss "not estimable" and no material accrual, and iBuying itself fully wound down, so the exposure is litigation-only.

9. Governance: dual-class founder control rising via buybacks. Founder voting power ~59.7% and rising mechanically as buybacks shrink the denominator (Barton 31.7% → 36.2%, 2023→2026), limiting external governance pressure if capital allocation or AI strategy disappoints.

These risks fire together in the bad-quarter case: a frozen-housing quarter coincides with the August 2026 FTC trial and an adverse MLS/private-listing development while unique users keep declining, and the traffic decline is what makes every monetization assumption (Preferred conversion, Rentals revenue-per-visitor, mortgage attach) harder to hit at once. The convertible-notes removal and ~$1.3B liquidity mean the balance sheet does not add to the fire, so this is a revenue and competitive-position stress rather than a solvency stress.

20. What we still don't know / monitoring KPIs

Named, unresolved gaps that bear on the conclusions above:

  1. Per-segment profitability is structurally undisclosable. Zillow reports a single operating segment, so there is no Residential, Rentals, Mortgages, or Other gross margin, operating margin, or ROIC anywhere. Every per-category moat conclusion in §3 rests on consolidated returns plus unit KPIs, so the Rentals and Residential moats are not margin-backed.
  2. The outside-view base-rate column in §14 is empty. No reference-class test was built for how often companies sustain ~19% Rentals or ~27% Mortgages growth for five years or reach a 45% EBITDA margin from 24%, so the model's drivers are calibrated to management, street, and own-history only.
  3. GAAP cost-of-capital fact. Zillow has not earned its cost of capital on a GAAP basis over the decade, with GAAP ROIC negative every continuing year, and the ~15–17% return appears only by adding SBC back at 15–23% of revenue. §3 states this so the adjusted-EBITDA framing does not obscure it.
  4. The FTC-remedy dollar exposure is unsized. No number bounds the share of Rentals' renter-side reach and revenue that reverses if the Redfin multifamily exclusivity is unwound — the highest-severity Rentals risk.
  5. The cash-conversion / contract-asset drag from Zillow Preferred is unresolved. Revenue is recognized up to ~2 years before cash, contract assets rose from $157M to $230M, and working capital flips from cash-positive prepaid to cash-negative pay-at-close, which makes OCF and FCF the weakest part of the model, so every projected FCF number is an estimate rather than a forecast.
  6. Core Residential unit economics are not externally verifiable. Zillow stopped disclosing Premier Agent advertiser counts, ARPA, lead counts, and price per lead at the FY2023 recast, so an outsider cannot tell whether monetization gains are price or volume for 66% of revenue.
  7. The Q1 2026 −3% unique-user decline is unattributed — AI/Google effect, weather/rate one-off (management blamed both), or the 2024 internal-measurement methodology switch. The Q2/Q3 2026 prints separate trend from noise.
  8. Period and source residuals. Pre-2015-08 price history and per-fiscal-year EV/EBITDA are not built; per-quarter consensus surprise % is not recoverable for a December-FYE name; FY2026–28 revenue/EBITDA dollar consensus is [web]/derived, not Bloomberg-grade.

Monitoring KPIs that would resolve the open questions, in order: visits and visits-per-unique-user (the AI-search alarm); For Sale revenue per TTV (the monetization engine); Enhanced Markets connection share toward 75%; Showcase penetration toward 5–10%; multifamily property count and listing share toward the ~145–150k ceiling; the August 2026 FTC trial outcome and any forced Redfin unwind; % of new listings appearing on Zillow within one day (the listing-access alarm); the contract-asset balance vs revenue (the cash-conversion drag); and the diluted-share-count and SBC/revenue trend (the per-share-value lever).
