Claims by section
business-model
  • about 80% of U.S. residential transactions involve an agent who uses at least one Zillow product
  • Premier Agent revenue declined from 66% of the total in 2022 to 52% in 2025 as Rentals rose from 14% to 24%
  • With visits flat to declining — up only 3% in FY2025 and down 3% year-over-year in Q1 2026 — essentially all Residential growth now comes from extracting more dollars per visit, a finite monetization lever rather than a traffic-compounding one
  • Management states the two are "a bit of a wash" on realized take rate; Preferred is an alignment mechanism, not a price increase, and the real economic driver of Enhanced Markets is the attached Zillow Home Loans origination
  • The Flex-to-Preferred transition therefore flips Residential working capital from cash-positive (prepaid deferred revenue) to cash-negative (contract asset), a structural drag on cash conversion that scales with Preferred adoption.
  • Rentals growth is monetization-led, not traffic-led: revenue per visitor rose 31% in FY2025 while visitors rose only 6%
  • The two compounding levers are multifamily building count (about 76,000 of an estimated 140,000–150,000 buildings, roughly half-penetrated) and revenue per building (package-tier upgrades).
  • The FY2025 gross-margin decline from 76% to 74% was driven "primarily due to increased lead acquisition costs associated with our Redfin rentals syndication agreement," a Rentals cost of $87M and the single largest cost increase in the company
  • In Q1 2026 ZHL grew purchase origination volume 96% year-over-year while the industry purchase-mortgage market declined about 1%, so essentially all of the growth is share captured from Zillow's own funnel rather than a market tailwind
  • The blended take rate is compressing (4.65% to 4.19% for the year; 5.2% to 4.08% in Q1) because ZHL is trading gain-on-sale margin for volume to win share, so revenue structurally lags volume.
  • The dominant cost of originating a loan, the loan officer, sits in sales and marketing rather than in cost of revenue (only about $4M of incremental mortgage-processing cost hit COGS in FY2025), so any COGS-based gross margin (~95%) substantially overstates real Mortgages profitability, and management calls the business "not yet at scale" with thin-to-negative true operating economics today.
  • From FY2024 to FY2025 operating income improved by $164M, from a $198M loss to a $34M loss, on $347M of additional revenue, a 47% incremental operating margin that is the company's best and reflects a high-fixed-cost, capital-light structure.
  • Management frames a mid-cycle target of $5B revenue at 45% Adjusted EBITDA margin, up from 24.1% in FY2025, which would require sustained incremental EBITDA margins above 50%, well above the 36% FY2025 actual, and is conditioned on a housing-market normalization to roughly 6 million existing-home sales that has not arrived
  • SBC ran $390–451M a year, 15–23% of revenue, larger than the entire FY2025 operating loss and about 17x FY2025 GAAP net income. The GAAP turn owes more to SBC declining as a share of revenue (23.2% in 2023 to 15.1% in 2025) than to revenue growth.
competitive-advantage
  • Zillow has not earned its cost of capital on a GAAP basis over the decade.
  • Zillow's Residential business holds a real and presently widening competitive advantage, a consumer-attention aggregator network effect. It is strengthening on monetization, but its demand foundation, consumer search traffic, is open to erosion.
  • Zillow's roughly $1.7B Residential revenue is about 3x Realtor.com/Move's entire ~$0.55B and roughly 17x Homes.com's ~$100M run-rate, and that gap has not closed despite CoStar spending well over $1B over three years to attack it.
  • Zillow Rentals has a real advantage that is widening on renter audience and listing share, but it is not yet visible in margin.
  • Rentals grew 39% in FY2025 and 42% in Q1 2026, roughly 2.5x the growth of CoStar's rentals business, and multifamily listing share rose from 54% (2024) to 63% (2025).
  • CoStar runs its entire Residential Real Estate segment at a negative Adjusted EBITDA of $(230)M in FY2025 (improved from $(361)M in FY2024) because Homes.com losses swamp Apartments.com's profits
  • ZHL has a real but contestable advantage that comes from distribution rather than product: it acquires a mortgage customer almost free because the borrower is already inside Zillow's funnel, searching homes, sized by BuyAbility (4.3M enrolled users by Q1 2026), and matched to a Zillow agent.
  • the integrated-funnel thesis is directly contested by a much bigger rival, since Rocket assembled the exact stack in 2025 by acquiring Redfin (portal plus brokerage) and Mr. Cooper (the largest U.S. servicer).
  • Other/display has no durable competitive advantage of its own. The display product does not differentiate — sold on CPM/CPC with no contract, no switching cost, no pricing power against fungible open-market inventory (Google, Meta, programmatic).
competitive-landscape
  • Residential competes in two arenas with different structures: the consumer-search/portal arena (eyeballs — concentrated, network-effect-driven, Zillow #1) and the agent-monetization arena (where the dollars are — fragmented, because an agent's marketing budget has many substitutes: rival portals, Google/Meta ads, brokerage-supplied leads, yard signs, referrals).
  • The downstream industry is among the most fragmented in the U.S. economy: about 4.8 million homes sold in 2025 (existing plus new; ~4.1M existing) served by over 300,000 brokerages and over 70,000 lenders across more than 500 MLSs
  • Zillow Residential ($1.7B) already captures roughly 28% of the midpoint. Residential growth must therefore come from monetization penetration, with revenue per TTV rising from 8.5 bps, rather than from a vast untapped market, a tighter ceiling than the $108B slide implies.
  • CoStar narrowed an unaided-brand-awareness gap from 64 points to 24 points yet converted it into only ~$100M of revenue, buying reach without buying the agent-spend share that is the prize.
  • In Rentals, the structure consolidated to a Zillow-vs-CoStar duopoly after the Redfin exit. The FTC complaint frames multifamily (25-plus-unit) ILS advertising as its own market where Zillow and CoStar held more than 80%; Zillow's internal estimate of its rental-listing share rose to 63% (2025) from 54% (2024).
  • Zillow's multifamily revenue grew about 46% on average year-over-year (Q1'23 33% to Q4'25 63%) against rental-advertising industry demand averaging about 14% and decelerating to +4% in the latest quarter, so the growth is share taken from a softening market
  • listing access — NAR's weakened Clear Cooperation and the private/pocket-listing trend are the binding structural battle, with Zillow simultaneously suing to keep MLS feeds open (Zillow v. Compass/MRED, May 2026) and being sued by Compass and CoStar over its own Listing Access Standards
expectations
  • the real business is the ~$2.0–2.6B continuing-operations company growing about 5%/year blended but ~24%/year in Rentals and roughly flat in Residential 2021→2025.
  • Gross margin is in a clear structural decline (85% → 74% → 73% Q1'26) driven by the growth mix — Rentals lead-acquisition cost including Redfin and Mortgages mix — which cuts against the high-margin-portal framing
  • The $5B/45% numbers are "unchanged," but the commitment is materially weaker: a hard 2025 deadline became an open-ended "whenever existing-home sales return to ~6M," which has not happened. Treat the $5B/45% as an aspirational, macro-contingent figure with no committed date.
secular-trends
  • Over three years (2022→2025) For Sale revenue grew 16% while existing-home sales declined 19%, roughly 35 points of cycle-independent outperformance, so essentially 100% of recent growth is share and monetization gain rather than a market trend, with the cyclical recovery sitting as unexercised upside management says it is not counting on
  • For-sale transactions are cyclical and at a trough (4.06 million existing-home sales in 2024, slowest since 1995; ~4.1 million in 2025, roughly a third below the 6-million "normalized" level management cites).
  • Rentals is the cleanest secular story because it is penetration-led, not transaction-cycle-led — about half the multifamily building universe is still un-onboarded, and revenue per building is rising — and it is structurally less cyclical (renting is counter-cyclical to buying; multifamily ad demand actually rose during the for-sale freeze on higher vacancy).
  • Management sizes a normalization to 6 million existing-home sales at roughly $1.3B of incremental For Sale revenue, and states FY2025 EBITDA margin "could have been mid- to high-30%" at a normal 6-million environment versus the 24% reported.
technology
  • Zillow's technology is applied ML sitting on a data and distribution asset. The 224 issued patents and the Zestimate are real but are not what stops a competitor: CoStar outspent Zillow on technology and still lost the for-sale monetization battle.
  • ShowingTime facilitates 90% of U.S. in-person tours; 100,000 agents use Follow Up Boss daily; dotloop is involved in roughly half of all U.S. transactions
  • The defensibility is market position and integration, and the strongest observable evidence is conversion lift: real-time-touring customers convert at 3x the rate of normal customers and ZHL pre-approved customers at 5x, which feeds Premier Agent monetization.
  • The same horizontal LLMs Zillow partners with are the primary substitution risk: if ChatGPT/Gemini-class agents become the front door to home search, Zillow's roughly 80%-direct traffic — a habit, not a contract — could be intermediated and its lead-generation monetization compressed.
value-chain
  • It charges for transaction intent without taking title: it captures 8.5 basis points of the $2.2 trillion transacting across its platform
  • The brokerage layer grosses the giant commission dollar but earns near-zero-to-negative net margin: Compass grossed $267B of gross transaction value and $6.96B of revenue but ran a net loss of $(58.5)M and passes 81.6% of revenue straight to independent-contractor agents (Compass 10-K FY2025); Zillow captures a tiny fraction of the transaction value at a ~74% blended gross margin and an inflecting operating margin.
  • In the mortgage chain Zillow is sandwiched between two counterparties with structural power over it: upstream, warehouse lenders fund the loan on mostly-uncommitted lines (they can decline to advance in a stress, the binding constraint on ZHL growth); downstream, the GSEs are the exit, and the entire revenue model depends on the secondary market staying open.
