1. Business description, model & unit economics

Cboe Global Markets operates exchanges and the data businesses that sit on top of them. It runs four U.S. options exchanges, four U.S. equities exchanges, a U.S. futures exchange, pan-European equities trading and clearing, an institutional FX platform, and the cross-cutting Cboe Data Vantage market-data and access business. The company reports on two axes that reconcile to the same totals: five operating segments (Options, North American Equities, Europe and Asia Pacific, Futures, Global FX) and three income-statement revenue captions (Cash and spot markets, Cboe Data Vantage, Derivatives markets). ~half of gross revenue is a pass-through Cboe never keeps, so net revenue ($2,429M FY2025) is the real top line, and ~63% of it, plus the bulk of operating income, sits in one segment, Options, behind one exclusive license.▪

What drives price, volume, and cost

The barbell inside Options. The Options segment runs two businesses with opposite economics on the same platform. Index (proprietary) options — SPX, VIX options, RUT, XSP, sector indices — are exclusive to Cboe under its S&P license and earn a net revenue per contract (RPC) of $0.924 in FY2025. Multi-listed (equity/ETP) options trade the identical contract on 18 U.S. exchanges and earn $0.066 — about a 14x lower RPC for the same matching engine▪. From roughly 27% of the segment's contract volume (index ADV 4.9M of 18.4M Cboe-touched), index options generate about 84% of the segment's net transaction fees▪ (FY2025: index ~4.9M × $0.924 × 250 days ≈ $1,132M versus multi-listed ~13.5M × $0.066 × 250 ≈ $223M) [estimate: ADV × RPC × trading days; reconciles within ~1% to the disclosed total-RPC check] (business-model--Options, FY2025 10-K). The investment question for the company is almost entirely a question about the SPX/VIX franchise.

What sets price. On index options Cboe is a price-setter because no rival venue can list SPX; the only discipline is the substitute (SPY options, prediction-market binaries) and keeping the liquidity ecosystem intact, not a competing exchange.▪ Index RPC rises every year — $0.832 (FY2021), $0.879 (FY2022), $0.902 (FY2024), $0.924 (FY2025), $0.940 (Q1 2026) — a durable +2% to +6% annual climb on the exclusive product while the input cost (the S&P/FTSE royalty, ~18% of the net fee) does not erode it. On multi-listed options and U.S. equities Cboe is a price-taker: fees and rebates are set by the marginal competing venue, net capture is competed down, and Cboe's published net RPC ($0.066 multi-listed) or net capture ($0.015 per 100 touched U.S. equity shares) is a thin sliver after rebates. (business-model--Options §3; business-model--North-American-Equities §5.)

What drives volume. Options volume growth comes from the multi-year rise of short-dated index trading. Zero-days-to-expiry (0-DTE) SPX options went from ~43% of SPX volume in Q4 2022 to over 61% in Q4 2025; index contract ADV rose from 1.9M (2019) to 4.9M (2025) and 6.1M in Q1 2026, a ~17% compound rate over 2019–2025 on a 43-year-old product.▪ The volume is diffuse — 34 retail broker platforms (largest ~30%), 11 floor-broker groups (largest ~23%), 20 market-making groups — which is what a structural behavior change looks like rather than a single-customer concentration (Q1 2026 transcript, lines 99–101, 253–259). The smaller segments are volatility-and-volume cyclical: Futures (VIX) ADV tracks the volatility cycle around a flat decade-long baseline; Global FX ADNV and European equity value traded surge with macro volatility.

The demand-driver tree — Strip the IR labels ("the vol franchise," "secular options growth," "record volumes") and one line is the company: index-option contract volume, driven by 0-DTE penetration amplified by the level of the VIX, times a rising exclusive-license fee-per-contract. About 74% of net revenue reprices with contract volume; the ~26% Cboe Data Vantage recurring annuity is the only leg that does not.

What drives the cost curve. The platform (Cboe Titanium) is in-house, runs on commodity hardware, and is largely fixed, so an incremental index contract arrives with almost no variable cost (only the S&P royalty) and runs over a built platform. That produces extreme operating leverage on index-driven growth: the Options segment converted 86% of incremental net revenue to operating income FY2024→FY2025 and 88% in Q1 2026 (versus a soft 30% in the flat-volume FY2023→FY2024 year).▪ The floor is high (segment operating margin ~73%) and the incremental margin in volatile years is ~85–88%, but the leverage runs both ways — a flat-volume year compresses margin because the opex base is fixed. (business-model--Options §5.)

Customer and clearing concentration. One customer was ~10% of total company revenue in FY2025 ($480.0M, up from $403.1M in FY2024 and $389.4M in FY2023 — concentration rising in absolute dollars), a large market-maker/order-flow relationship spread across all five segments, with no contractual lock-in▪ ("No customer is contractually or otherwise obligated to continue to use the Company's services"). Separately, three clearing members clear ~71% of the market-maker sides at all of Cboe's U.S. options exchanges, and ~69% of net transaction-and-clearing fees route through OCC▪, the sole U.S. options clearer Cboe co-owns at a token $0.3M carrying value (FY2025 10-K Item 1A, lines 4172, 4176, 8257; value-chain §0). Cboe does not clear its own flagship product.

2. Key technologies & technical differentiators

CBOE is not a deep-technology company, and its own 10-K says so: the matching engines run on "readily available hardware," and low latency and high uptime are table stakes every credible exchange clears. The hard-to-replicate assets are regulatory and contractual, not technical — the exclusive S&P 500 / SPX index license and the proprietary VIX methodology. The clearest proof is that the same Cboe Titanium engine produces a 14x-higher RPC on the exclusive product than on the commodity product.▪ Anyone constructing a "technology moat" story for CBOE is mislabeling a regulatory and network moat. (technology, FY2025 10-K lines 3633, 3625, 3909.)

Exclusive SPX license + VIX methodology (the real moat; regulatory/contractual). SPX options are cash-settled options on the S&P 500, listed exclusively on Cboe under a license from S&P Dow Jones Indices that runs through December 31, 2033, with exclusive rights to trade S&P 500 index options through December 31, 2032.▪ No other U.S. exchange may list an option on the S&P 500 index itself. The VIX Index is computed from the mid-points of real-time SPX option quotes and settles via a Special Opening Quotation; it is the reference for VIX options and VIX futures. So the two franchises are linked — VIX is literally built on SPX option prices that only Cboe has at exclusive scale. What is hard to replicate is not the math (the VIX formula is academically public, and Cboe's own risk factor concedes competitors "could create substantially similar volatility indices") but (a) the bilateral S&P license, and (b) the 30-plus-year liquidity network that has accreted around SPX/VIX. The differentiation shows up as pricing power that compounds: index RPC rose every year while multi-listed RPC stayed flat. The substitution threats are SPY/multi-listed cash-settled index options siphoning retail flow, event/prediction-market binaries capturing retail directional demand, and — the binding constraint — non-renewal or loss of exclusivity in 2032–2033. (technology §1; FY2025 10-K lines 3528, 3534–3535, 3914.)

Cboe Titanium (efficiency/scale, real but contestable). Titanium is the in-house matching platform that runs options, futures, and equities on one codebase with locally configured market models. It is the rebrand of the Bats-origin engine Cboe acquired in 2017 and migrated every venue onto. The valuable thing is not raw speed but the single multi-asset, multi-region codebase, which makes integration and new-product launch cheap — management says incremental revenue delivered onto the platform "approaches nearly 100%" margin, and Cboe integrated nine acquisitions onto it in five years with 100% uptime through 2020–2021.▪ But this is operational muscle, not a structural entry barrier: 18 U.S. options exchanges now compete (the FY2025 10-K basis; two more are expected in H1 2026), and the 10-K attributes that proliferation to rivals "opening new exchanges that offer different markets and pricing models on existing technology." The platform lowers Cboe's cost to defend share; it does not stop entry. (technology §2; Investor Day 2021 transcript line 629.)

Index calculation engine / Cboe Global Indices (data moat, modest). Cboe calculates and licenses proprietary indices (volatility indices, BuyWrite/PutWrite benchmarks, the Magnificent 10 Index) built from its own exchange-generated data and distributed via the Cboe Global Indices Feed. The defensible piece is the proprietary history of exchange-generated data, which is downstream of the SPX exclusivity, not an independent moat. Data Vantage net revenue grew 19% organically year-over-year in Q1 2026, but ~85–90% of that growth came from new units and new sales rather than price increases▪, and the products are "under competitive threat from ATSs and trading venues that offer similar products." (technology §3; Q1 2026 transcript line 125.)

Cboe Global Cloud / dedicated cores (table-stakes-plus). Cloud data distribution and low-latency access products monetize the already-exclusive data at high margin and extend reach (85% of Global Cloud growth came from outside the U.S.). Differentiation rests on what flows through the pipe (exclusive SPX/VIX and global-exchange data), not the pipe itself. Hybrid open-outcry floor (legacy/niche, but it holds the highest-value flow). Cboe Options is the only one of Cboe's four U.S. options markets with a physical Chicago floor; 58% of SPX notional value still trades on the floor (versus 16% of contracts) because complex multi-leg institutional risk "can't be replicated electronically."▪ AI/blockchain/tokenization are used internally for productivity (code, infosec) and remain roadmap, not a customer-facing edge; the forward case leans on SPX/0-DTE growth, Data Vantage, and prediction markets, not on unshipped AI. (technology §4–§6; Q1 2026 transcript lines 105, 255.)

3. Competitive advantages & moat assessment

The advantage is real and, for now, durable, but it is anchored to a dated third-party license, which keeps it below the very top tier. Cboe earns its excess returns from one thing: the exclusive S&P 500 / SPX index-options franchise and the proprietary VIX complex built on it. That franchise earns a ~73% segment operating margin and a 14x revenue-per-contract premium over the commodity multi-listed book on the same matching engine, and the premium is widening, not eroding — index RPC rose every year from $0.832 (2021) to $0.940 (Q1 2026) while index ADV compounded ~17% a year from 2019 to 2025.▪ This is a real liquidity-network moat with a contractual cliff: the crown-jewel exclusivity runs only through 2032, and rivals are already attacking the benchmark.▪ The advantage is stronger than an edge that is already compressing and weaker than a self-owned network like Visa or Google search, because the most valuable asset is leased from S&P, the contract has a date on it, and a competitor (MIAX's Bloomberg 500 index options) is building a substitute benchmark now. The advantage is best described as two businesses: a wide-moat index franchise that holds essentially all the economic rent, wrapped around a no-moat multi-listed utility that is a price-taker losing share.▪

Management claims: the SPX/VIX complex is the franchise, and management says so plainly — ~68% of net revenue comes from the Options and Futures segments, "the majority of which was generated by products based on exclusively licensed indices (e.g., SPX options) and products based on our proprietary VIX methodology … our revenues less cost of revenues are dependent in large part on the exclusive licenses we hold" (FY2025 10-K line 3909). The mechanism management points to is not the license but the ecosystem on top of it: a balanced base of 34 retail platforms, 11 floor-broker groups, and 20 market-making groups, none dominant, plus the cross-product SPX-to-VIX plumbing and the 0-DTE expiration grid — "an entire ecosystem with balanced flow, balanced risk, balanced participation … This is what we set out to build" (Q1 2026 transcript, lines 253–263). The 2021 Investor Day framed it as a "virtuous cycle": more products to more customers to more data [deck: Investor Day 2021-11-16, slide 3].

External sources: with no local sell-side primer, the external view comes from the competitive reality. It corroborates the index franchise as the asset and confirms the license risk is now an active analyst and competitor focus, not theoretical. MIAX signed a 10-year exclusive license with Bloomberg in July 2024 to list cash-settled options on the Bloomberg 500 Index, a deliberate S&P-500-proxy competitor (the SEC approved cash-settled B500 options in May 2025)▪ [web: MIAX licensing materials / Federal Register, accessed 2026-06-24]. A BofA analyst on the Q1 2026 call asked directly about "other exchanges looking to compete for the SPX contract in 2032" (transcript line 247). And prediction-market binary S&P contracts (Kalshi-style, plus Cboe's own Cboe Predicts) are an adjacent substitute for retail directional S&P exposure.

Ground-up view: from first principles the segment splits cleanly. Multi-listed options have no moat — the identical contract is fungible across 18 venues, minimum efficient scale is low (Cboe runs four venues on one stack precisely because opening another is cheap), switching costs are zero, and the financials show what that predicts: RPC $0.066, ~7.5% volume CAGR, share drifting down (24.5% to 22.3% over five quarters). Index options have a wide moat built on three reinforcing barriers: an exclusive license (absolute but time-boxed), a liquidity network effect (all SPX liquidity pools on one venue; depth attracts flow attracts market-makers — the same economics as a futures pit, and CME defended its E-mini S&P futures for decades against many would-be competitors), and cross-product complementarity (a competitor with a substitute benchmark gets none of the VIX/0-DTE/settlement plumbing on day one). The structural soft spot is the supplier: S&P DJI owns the index, collects a rising automatic royalty (royalty cost grew $204.9M to $256.3M over two years, ~12%/yr, only $14.6M of it a fixed floor), runs a 69%-operating-margin business on that royalty stream, and holds the 2032/2033 renewal lever. CME's analogous S&P license is architecturally stronger because it is tied to CME's equity ownership of the S&P DJI joint venture and stays exclusive as long as CME owns at least 5% of the JV — effectively perpetual — whereas Cboe's is a fixed-term lease.▪ (competitive-advantage--Options; value-chain §4.)

Returns tested three ways

Standalone. Consolidated GAAP operating margin (on net revenue) rose from 52.8% (FY2020) to 60.4% (FY2025), with the FY2022 trough at 28.1% entirely a $460.9M Digital impairment. ROIC rose from ~10–13% (FY2018–2021, weighed down by Bats goodwill in invested capital) to 22.4% in FY2025, durably above any reasonable 8–9% cost of capital.▪ The Options segment itself earns ~73% operating margin, and ROIIC over FY2022 to FY2025 is high (about $730M of incremental NOPAT on roughly flat invested capital as cash built faster than capital was deployed). On a tangible-capital basis the return is far higher still, because Cboe needs almost no tangible capital (capex 2.3–3.8% of net revenue across the decade). The level is high and the trend is stable-to-rising.

ROIC definition. NOPAT = operating income × (1 − effective tax rate); invested capital = total debt at face + stockholders' equity − cash & equivalents, averaged over the year. ROIC is depressed post-2017 by ~$3.2bn of Bats goodwill + intangibles in invested capital; on a pre-goodwill/tangible basis the index franchise's return is far higher. This is a business-quality test, not a stock-valuation judgment.

4. Competitive landscape & market sizing

Cboe's competitive position differs sharply by business line, so the structure is examined line by line. The summary: Cboe is the uncontested scale leader where it owns the product (index options, VIX futures, pan-European equity clearing), an incumbent losing share where the product is a commodity (U.S. multi-listed options, U.S. cash equities), and a mid-pack growth player in FX.▪ Where it is gaining (European clearing) and where it is losing (U.S. equities, multi-listed options) both matter less to profit than the one place it is uncontested and getting richer (index options).

U.S. listed options — monopoly in index, oligopoly losing share in multi-listed

There are 18 U.S. options exchanges (Cboe runs four), with two more expected in H1 2026. The market splits into two sub-markets with different concentration: proprietary index options (an effective monopoly — Cboe held ~98.7% index-options share in FY2021 and ~100% since, because SPX is single-listed)▪ and multi-listed options, where 18 venues list the identical contract and share is fragmenting. The total U.S. options market grew ADV +25% to 60.8M contracts/day in FY2025 (~15.2bn contracts/year), the sixth consecutive record year. Cboe's total options share slipped to 30.3% (FY2025) and 29.1% (Q1 2026) even as its own volume grew, because the market grew faster and Cboe ceded multi-listed share.

U.S. cash equities — the weakest position; revenue rises only because industry volume does

Yet because industry ADV exploded (11.0bn to 17.6bn shares, FY2023–FY2025), Cboe's net equities revenue rose every year and its operating margin climbed from 32.3% (FY2023) to 46.2% (FY2025) to 59.8% (Q1 2026). Its share falls every year while industry volume grows, so revenue still rises. (competitive-landscape--North-American-Equities.)

VIX futures — monopoly in a tiny segment; the real competition is internal and prediction markets

Within listed futures Cboe is a rounding error — 227k contracts/day is ~0.8% of CME's 28.1M and ~0.6% of the CME-plus-ICE complex — but in equity-volatility futures it is an uncontested monopolist, because no licensed competitor exists and CME's own CVOL volatility suite explicitly excludes S&P 500 equity volatility.▪ Cboe is not losing VIX share because there is no rival to lose it to. The real competitive pressure is, first, substitution toward Cboe's own higher-value VIX/SPX options (the dollar stays inside Cboe but moves to the Options segment — VIX options ran a record 862k contracts/day in 2025), and second, the rise of event/prediction markets (Kalshi, Polymarket, CME-FanDuel), which scaled from under $5bn/month (Sep 2025) to ~$24bn/month (Apr 2026) and which Cboe is entering rather than defending against. (competitive-landscape--Futures.)

European equities & clearing — the one clean scale win, and it is widening

Cboe is the structural scale leader in two of the three EAP businesses. Cboe Europe is the single largest pan-European equities venue at ~25.0% share (FY2025), gaining (24.0% to 25.0% over 2023–2025) while net capture rose (0.226bps to 0.269bps) — rising price and rising share, the opposite of commodity fee compression.▪ Cboe Clear Europe is the largest pan-European cash-equity CCP at ~39.4% cleared share, rising (34.9% to 39.4% over 2023–2025, then 40.2% in Q1 2026), and it clears a larger share of the market than it trades because its interoperable model lets it clear for venues it does not own — a structural edge the vertical-silo incumbents (Euronext Clearing, Eurex Clearing) cannot copy without cannibalizing their captive venues.▪ The threat here is regulatory (an EU push toward a single CCP, or EMIR 3.0 / MiFIR transparency changes), not a peer taking share. The third leg (Asia Pacific) is sub-scale and being exited: Japan wound down 2025, Canada and Australia sold to TMX under a definitive April 2026 agreement. A new additive pool, central clearing of securities-financing transactions, exceeded €1bn of notional outstanding by January 2026. (competitive-landscape--Europe-and-Asia-Pacific.)

Global FX — mid-pack on size, top-quartile on growth

Cboe FX is a mid-pack electronic spot-FX venue (~$50bn/day spot ADV, ~4th–5th among public-data venues, behind LSEG ~$106bn, FXSpotStream ~$77bn, and CME's EBS ~$64bn). It is sub-scale relative to the ~$2.1tn/day global spot-FX market it sells into (~2.4% share). But it grew spot ADV faster than the two scale leaders in 2025 (+9.5% vs LSEG +6.1% and EBS +7.4%)▪, leads in disclosed/"Firm" ECN liquidity, and is taking relative share from a stalling EBS (whose FX transaction fees were flat at ~$132M across 2023–2025). Cboe withdrew its FX market-share KPI from the FY2025 10-K the year after it ticked down (20.0% to 19.6% in FY2024), so the internal share series stops at 2024. (competitive-landscape--Global-FX.)

The one upstream interface that matters: S&P. This is the only place a counterparty captures a margin-rich slice of Cboe's best business and can raise the price. For every $1 of net derivatives revenue Cboe keeps, it pays S&P/index licensors about $0.15 in royalty ($201.8M Derivatives royalty against $1,341M Derivatives net revenue). S&P Dow Jones Indices runs a 69% operating margin on that royalty stream, above Cboe's own.▪ Cboe does the matching, listing, distribution, regulation, and product engineering; S&P owns the trademark and collects a high-margin toll. Only $14.6M/year of Cboe's licensing cost is a fixed floor; the rest is variable and rises automatically with volume, so S&P's capture grows as the SPX franchise grows (SPX ADV grew 34% YoY in Q1 2026). In November 2025 S&P hired Cboe's own former Head of Derivatives to run S&P Dow Jones Indices. (value-chain §1, §4.)

The binding constraint is contractual and regulatory, not capacity. Unlike a chip or aerospace chain there is no physical bottleneck — Cboe Titanium runs on commodity hardware and matching capacity scales cheaply. The binding constraints are (1) the S&P license window (exclusive SPX rights through Dec 31, 2032; full license through 2033), which the 10-K calls out as the single most material dependency; (2) VIX methodology exclusivity, which depends on continued access to SPX option data, which depends on (1); (3) sole-clearer dependence at OCC for all U.S. options and VIX futures; and (4) clearing-member concentration▪ — three members clear ~71% of the market-maker sides at all Cboe options exchanges, so a withdrawal "could create significant disruption to the U.S. options markets, including Cboe's." There is essentially no input-side lock-in: license minimums total $14.6M/year and a cloud-services minimum of $5.8–6.9M/year, against $4.7bn of revenue. (value-chain §2.)

Customer-side economics. For an exchange the customer-return test is whether downstream demand is durable, because that is what makes net capture sustainable. The 0-DTE retail/broker cohort is volume-driven and, on the evidence so far, regime-robust: when the macro shifted in March 2026, 0-DTE growth slowed to +6% but non-0-DTE rose 26%, so the toolkit captured flow in both regimes. The caveat is that 0-DTE is a post-2022 phenomenon never tested through a sustained low-volatility year; if retail options engagement reverses, the $1.7bn rebate cost is largely variable and falls with volume, so margin holds on the way down, but net revenue would decline with it. (value-chain §3.)

Bargaining-power trajectory. Power is strongest and improving in the proprietary index book (index RPC ~14x multi-listed; SPX exclusive so no venue can bid the fee down; index ADV +29% with RPC also up 19% in Q1 2026 — Cboe taking both price and volume) and weak/eroding in the commodity equities and multi-listed books (liquidity payments rose 29% in 2025 to $1,709.7M as Cboe competes for fungible flow). The one relationship moving against Cboe, and against its best business, is the S&P license — a supplier with a 69%-margin business, a renewal lever, and a rising automatic toll. The chain is notably free of vendor-financing circularity: Cboe's minority stakes are small and strategic, not demand-supporting (the largest, the 7Ridge fund that owned Trading Technologies, was largely realized in 2025 for a $90.8M gain). The one contingent exposure is clearing-member default risk at Cboe Clear Europe and Cboe Clear U.S., a tail risk applying only to the European and U.S.-futures clearing Cboe owns — not to the SPX/VIX options that OCC clears. (value-chain §4–§5.)

6. Secular & industry trends

Cboe's growth is pulled by one dominant, structural force — the multi-year rise of short-dated index-options trading (0-DTE) in its exclusive SPX/VIX complex — amplified by two smaller secular legs (globalization of access to U.S. index options, and the high-margin Data Vantage recurring annuity), and overlaid by a large cyclical layer (volume scales with volatility). The 2024–2026 results combine both: a real secular step-change in 0-DTE penetration and an unusually active, tariff/geopolitics-charged macro window. The single most important secular risk is not demand erosion but timing — the exclusive S&P license is contractually exclusive only through 2032–2033.

Short-dated (0-DTE) index trading, the largest source of growth: same-day SPX trading went from over 43% of SPX volume in Q4 2022 to over 61% in Q4 2025; index contract ADV rose from 1.9M (2019) to 4.9M (2025) and 6.1M in Q1 2026. The inflection point was clearly 2022 (+44% index ADV), the year Cboe added Tuesday/Thursday and ultimately full Monday–Friday SPX expirations.▪ This is a structural change in how participants use options (intraday risk transfer and income), not a one-time volatility event, and the evidence for durability is that it grew through both calm and stressed quarters and that participation is diffuse (no broker over 30%, no floor group over 23%). The second leg, globalization of access, compounds it: global-trading-hours SPX volume grew over 30%, 7 of 10 identified Korean local brokers now offer SPX options (versus 0 online two years ago), and Robinhood onboarded.▪ The third leg, Data Vantage, is a ~98%-gross-margin recurring annuity growing high-single to low-double-digit, ~85–90% from new units rather than price — but roughly half of Q1 2026 Data Vantage growth was access/connectivity revenue pulled by the same options-volume boom, so it is partly derivative of the 0-DTE trend rather than fully independent. (secular-trends §2.)

Share math: the reconciliation that matters for the consensus framing is that Cboe's options revenue is up far more than its share suggests, because the growth is mix-driven toward the exclusive high-RPC index line, where the "market" is effectively its own. The U.S. options market grew ADV +25% (FY2025) and +18% (Q1 2026), faster than Cboe's volume in the multi-listed line, so total options share declined (30.8% to 30.3% to 29.1%) and multi-listed share declined harder (24.5% to 22.3%). Cboe is a modest share loser in the contestable multi-listed pool, not a share winner in options broadly — the revenue growth is a product-mix shift toward a proprietary line, not competitive share capture.▪ The picture is mixed across lines: gaining in European equities (23.6% to 25.0%) and clearing (37.4% to 39.4%), losing in the two most commoditized lines (U.S. multi-listed options and U.S. on-exchange equities, where the market is itself migrating off-exchange). (secular-trends §3.)

Cyclical versus secular: the reported FY2025 growth (net revenue +17%, +28% in Q4, +29% in Q1 2026) overstates the trend rate because it overlaps a secular step-change with an unusually active macro window. Futures ADV tracks volatility most directly, and it swings: −5% in FY2025 (227.2k versus 238.6k), then +14% in Q1 2026.▪ A reasonable split of recent consolidated growth: the bulk is secular (0-DTE penetration, index mix, international, recurring data), but a material slice — the part that moves with the VIX and industry ADV — is cyclical and will not persist if volatility normalizes to a low-vol regime. The durable floor is high (even in a calm year, 0-DTE income strategies, international onboarding, and recurring data keep compounding); the ceiling risk is the 2032 license, not demand. The first signs that the secular story is breaking would be: 0-DTE share of SPX rolling over from ~60%, index ADV decelerating below mid-teens in a non-low-vol quarter, GTH/international growth slowing from 30%+, and any S&P/SPX renewal news. (secular-trends §4, §6.)

Technology and regulatory shifts that could change the slope. Upside: 23x5 U.S. equities trading (targeted late November 2026), securities-based event/prediction contracts (Cboe Predicts on XSP, launched 2026-06-23, with a stated regulatory tailwind from SEC/CFTC drawing clearer securities-versus-swaps lines), single-name 0-DTE (management argues additive, not cannibalistic), and concentration-hedging demand (Russell 2000, "Mag 10"). Downside: the 2032–2033 license horizon (the dominant slope risk — if exclusivity ends, the highest-margin line's RPC could compress toward the $0.066 multi-listed level), the SEC equity access-fee cap reduction (a headwind to the thin on-exchange equities line, effective Nov 2026), off-exchange migration (off-exchange touched ADV +96% FY2025, +175% Q1 2026), and continued multi-listed competitive entry. The emerging optionality leg — event/prediction markets and tokenization — is management's stated new growth bet but is un-sized and currently scored by the market as a multiple threat rather than a revenue opportunity. (secular-trends §5.)

7. Critical points

  1. One exclusive license is the company. About 84% of Options net transaction fees, ~76% of the company's pre-corporate segment operating income, and the bulk of consolidated profit ride the SPX/index franchise behind a single S&P license that is exclusive only through 2032. The 10-K states it directly: ~68% of net revenue comes from the Options and Futures segments, "the majority of which" is exclusively-licensed or proprietary-index product. Everything else — four equities exchanges, FX, the European footprint — is either thin-net or a smaller annuity. The investment case for the business is, at root, the durability of this one franchise.

  2. The 14x revenue-per-contract premium proves the moat is contractual, not technological. The same Cboe Titanium engine yields $0.924 per index contract and $0.066 per multi-listed contract. The difference is entirely the exclusive license plus the liquidity network that has accreted around SPX/VIX for 30-plus years.▪ Index RPC has risen every year ($0.832 in 2021 to $0.940 in Q1 2026) with no rival venue to compete it down; multi-listed RPC has stayed flat while Cboe loses share. The premium is widening today; the question is 2032, not now.

  3. Q1 2026 is a sharp positive inflection the FY2025 figures understate. Net revenue $728.9M was +29% YoY and ~20% above the FY2025 quarterly average; consolidated operating margin hit 69.4% (versus 60.4% FY2025); the Options segment ran a 76.9% margin and +33% net revenue. A model anchored on the FY2025 Options operating income of $1,113M would understate the current run-rate by ~25–30%. But the lift leans partly on the March 2026 geopolitical-volatility spike and a Section-31-to-$0 denominator effect, both of which reverse — the durable part is the underlying index volume and rising index RPC.

  4. The stock declined 32% in the six weeks after the record Q1 2026 print. Consensus extrapolated the volatility-driven Q1 run-rate upward (CY2026E EPS revised +12% in three months and +34% over the year, the largest upward revision in the comp universe), then the VIX collapsed on a US–Iran peace deal in June and CFTC-approved prediction/perpetual contracts raised a structural-threat fear. The stock made an all-time high of $366.70 on 2026-05-13 and declined to $249.59 by late June. Forward EPS rose over the same period, so the entire fall was in the multiple (Section 15).

  5. The Options share number is misleading. Total options market share slipped to 29.1% (Q1 2026) and multi-listed share to 22.3%, which looks like competitive loss. The opposite is true economically: Cboe is ceding low-value multi-listed volume (net $0.066/contract) to MIAX and MEMX while gaining high-value index volume (net $0.924/contract) where it has ~100% share and no share to lose.▪ The segment is getting more index-weighted, which is margin-accretive even as headline share slips.

  6. Operating leverage runs both ways, and the bear case turns on a calm-VIX quarter never observed. The Options business converts ~85–88% of index-driven incremental net revenue to operating income, but a flat-volume year compresses margin because the opex base is fixed. The whole forward debate — base case ~9.5% net-revenue CAGR versus a bear of ~flat EPS for five years — reduces to one unanswered question: is the 2025/Q1-2026 index surge secular or a volatility peak? 0-DTE durability has never been tested through a low-volatility quarter; the quarterly record covers only the up-cycle.

  7. The model is a one-driver model. A ±4-percentage-point-per-year move in index-options ADV swings FY2030 operating income by $734M — about 2.5x the next-largest driver (index RPC, $299M) and about 7x the fifth (multi-listed RPC, $103M). The forward is a bet on the durability of 0-DTE/SPX volume, not on the four smaller segments. The model's risk is concentrated exactly where the moat is, which is also where the 2032 license expiry sits (Section 17).

  8. The 2020–2022 diversification wave was a value-destroyer, and the 2025–26 realignment is unwinding it. Cboe is a disciplined acquirer in its core (Bats, 2017, never impaired; the European clearing tuck-ins held) and a clear value-destroyer outside it: ErisX/Cboe Digital was ~$564M paid, ~96% written off within 2–3 years. The current management is winding down Japan and CEDX, selling Canada and Australia to TMX, and cutting ~20% of the workforce, framing the exits as accretive because the units were roughly breakeven (Section 11).

  9. The balance sheet is net cash and the capital structure is benign. Total debt is $1,443M of all-fixed-rate senior notes at a 2.82% blended coupon against $2,216M cash — net cash, 0.9x gross leverage, ~32x interest coverage. The only near-term item is the $650M 3.65% notes due January 2027, refinanceable from cash. Net dilution is negative (diluted shares 112.2M in 2018 to 105.1M in 2025). This is a high-return, asset-light, under-levered business (Sections 10, 12).

  10. A rising single-customer concentration sits unquantified. One customer was ~10% of total revenue ($480M, growing ~19%) with no contractual lock-in, and three clearing members clear ~71% of market-maker sides. Cboe does not name the counterparty or split the exposure by segment. It is flagged as a first-order risk but is not yet quantified (Section 18, Section 20).

8. Variant perceptions

Each point below is where the evidence cuts against the consensus or management framing.

  1. "CBOE is a high-multiple compounder." It de-rated to 18.5x CY2026E adjusted EPS, below its own 22.6x long-run forward-P/E median and below CME and Nasdaq, while posting the group's strongest estimate revisions (+34% over one year). The price, not the earnings, drove the compression — the multiple de-rated from ~24x at the May peak to 18.5x in six weeks on roughly flat-to-rising forward EPS. Quantification: $366.70 to $249.59, −32%, against CY2026E EPS revised +12% over the same three months. (market-pricing; descriptive only.)

  2. "The SPX-options franchise is unassailable, so volatility is the only swing factor." The June 2026 −32% drawdown was driven by a new structural vector — CFTC-approved prediction/perpetual contracts (Kalshi) and binary event contracts (Schwab) into retail index-directional flow — not by a volume or earnings miss. The market now prices a competitive-disruption discount onto the index profit pool, and Cboe itself launched Cboe Predicts to participate. The CNBC "revolution" quote is verbatim and the CFTC/Kalshi and VIX-collapse catalysts are corroborated across multiple sources, though the original CNBC URL could not be re-fetched.   3. "The license horizon is a distant, theoretical problem." It is an active, near-term competitive risk.   4. "CBOE has a strong guidance hit-rate." The truer statement is that Cboe sandbags the bands it controls and reliably beats them, while leaving the revenue line that drives the beat (transaction volume) unguided. The organic-growth band is a floor, not an expectation — FY2025 opened "mid-single-digit" and printed +17%; FY2026 opened "mid-single-digit" and was raised to "low-double-digit to mid-teens" after one quarter.   5. "Data Vantage is a ~mid-single-digit grower" (the guided framing). Realized growth has run high-single to low-double-digit for two-plus years, ~85–90% from new units rather than price, with a rising international mix (45% of new data sales overseas in Q4 2025 versus 35% a year prior). Management raised the FY2026 band to low-double-digit after pushing back on exactly this point one quarter earlier. Five key inflections:

  • Bats (2017): the company Cboe became. Net revenue went from $566M (2016) to $1,217M (2018) and the share count from ~81.4M to 112.2M on the stock consideration, so the early per-share story was thinner than the scale story — diluted EPS rose only $2.27 to $3.69 to $3.76, and 2017 net income was lifted by a one-time $66.2M tax benefit rather than operations. Diluted weighted-average shares were 107.5M (2017) and 112.2M (2018). The deal established Cboe's pattern: buy a platform, migrate it onto one stack, cut cost — the ~$65M synergy target was beaten (~$80M by 2019), and Bats goodwill has never been impaired.

  • The 0-DTE / SPX boom (2022–2025): organic growth that outran every acquisition. No acquisition; a product-design and index-license decision (daily SPX expirations). Net revenue went $1,742M to $2,429M and operating income $490M (impairment-depressed) to $1,467M; index ADV +21% in 2025, SPX 0-DTE ADV +66% in Q4 2025. It is the clearest evidence the real moat is the exclusive license, not the acquired venues.

  • The digital-asset bet and its impairment (2021 announce → 2022 write-off): the strategy walked back. Crypto was a 2021 Investor Day headline ("$75 trillion" addressable market); the $460.9M Cboe Digital impairment in 2022 was the proximate cause of the decline in FY2022 net income to $235M despite record revenue. The pattern: Cboe chases an adjacent "platform + clearing" TAM, then cuts losses after a real write-off.

  • The Tilly governance crisis and strategy reset (Sep 2023): forced exit, chosen narrowing. A conduct-driven CEO removal within weeks (the board ran an independent investigation and forced out a long-tenured, successful CEO), with the CFO already gone. No financial discontinuity, but it seeded the 2025–26 realignment — Tomczyk called the prior "any new asset class / geography" strategy too broad and pivoted to "redirect time, talent, capital to the core."

  • The 2025–26 realignment and current run-rate spike: shrinking the periphery while the core re-accelerates. Under Donohue/Johnston, Cboe is exiting Japan/CEDX/Canada/Australia/corporate listings (a ~3% net-revenue give-up) while the SPX/0-DTE/VIX core runs ~+30% YoY. The combination — periphery divestiture, core acceleration, opex discipline — produced the steepest margin/EPS inflection in the company's public history (Q4 2025 net revenue +28%, Q1 2026 +29%), with the guide raised and the opex range cut within one quarter. The headline growth is heavily volatility-levered.

Behavioral pattern: Cboe buys and integrates, then cuts cost; the core is organic and exclusive while the periphery is acquired and disposable (Digital, Japan, CEDX, Canada, Australia all impaired or sold); it is decisive on cost and conduct; it keeps a conservative balance sheet with dividend-led, not buyback-led, capital return; and demand rises with volatility, so a crisis raises revenue rather than stressing the business. (history-inflections.)

10. Management, incentives & capital allocation

Leadership. The CEO chair has turned over twice in three years, which is the single most important management fact about Cboe now. Ed Tilly was forced out in September 2023 after an investigation found he failed to disclose personal relationships with colleagues; board director Fred Tomczyk (ex-TD Ameritrade) stepped in, then handed off in mid-2025 to Craig Donohue, an external hire who ran CME (2004–2012) and then chaired the OCC (the clearinghouse directly upstream of Cboe's options franchise) through 2024. His background is directly in derivatives and clearing, but at 64 he is plausibly a fixed-horizon CEO. Beyond the CEO seat, 2025 saw the President and two division heads leave and a new bench installed (Scott Johnston COO; a Head of Retail Expansion for event contracts). The continuity anchors are CFO Jill Griebenow (promoted 2023) and COO Christopher Isaacson (a long-tenured Bats/Cboe operator, departing end-2026). After Tilly the board split the Chairman and CEO roles. (management-incentives §1.)

Compensation design. Pay is majority at-risk and majority equity. The annual bonus weights corporate-wide adjusted EBITDA at 40%, net revenue at 30%, and individual goals at 30%, with payouts capped at 200%. Long-term equity is half time-based RSUs and half PSUs, the PSUs split between three-year cumulative adjusted EPS and three-year relative total shareholder return versus the S&P 500. Both bonus metrics and both PSU metrics paid at the 200% maximum for FY2025 and the 2023–2025 cycle — earned on the numbers (Cboe's indexed TSR was 289 versus the peer index's 176 by end-2025), but the program is clearly at its ceiling with little room to reward more. Three 2025 design features measure per-share value rather than size: a per-share EPS leg, a relative-TSR benchmark to a broad index rather than a cherry-picked peer set, and a new net-revenue gate that bars earning above 100% on EBITDA unless revenue clears 97% of target. The metrics that bear watching are the "adjusted" non-GAAP definitions the committee controls, and the fact that the prior regime's size-led M&A expansion (2017–2022) was rewarded through pay before it required impairments — the comp design did not prevent it; the underlying SPX/VIX franchise did. Governance terms include aggressive clawbacks, no hedging or pledging, no change-in-control tax gross-ups, double-trigger CIC, and a 6x-salary CEO ownership guideline. (management-incentives §2.)

Insider ownership is low — this is a hired-management company, not an owner-operator. Total insider beneficial ownership is under 1% of the company; the CEO holds ~2,600 shares outright against ~$18M of pay. Alignment rests on plan design and holding guidelines (which the new CEO had not yet met at year-end), not on a founder-style stake. The largest holders are Vanguard (12.1%), BlackRock (9.0%), and AllianceBernstein (6.5%). Candor is reasonable but self-serving: each reversal of the 2021 expansion strategy (crypto, Japan, CEDX, Canada, Australia) was framed as "rigorous capital discipline" rather than an admission of overpayment, and the strategy language ("value ladder," "flywheel," "$75 trillion TAM") was quietly retired in favor of "optimize the core." The mitigant is that the current team is doing the cleanup and the relative-TSR record is strong. (management-incentives §4–§5.)

The 10-year uses-of-cash record. Cboe is a capital-light allocator: 10-year cumulative capex was only $488M against ~$8.4bn of reported operating cash flow (capex ~6% of OCF, ~3% of net revenue), so almost all cash is available for M&A, dividends, and buybacks.▪

11. M&A, divestitures & deal track record

Cboe is a disciplined acquirer in its core and a clear value-destroyer in the one big adjacency it chased. The record splits cleanly. The core deals worked: Bats (2017, $3,960M) built the modern company and beat its synergy target, and its goodwill has never been impaired; the European clearing tuck-ins (EuroCCP/Cboe Clear Europe, which even produced a $32.6M bargain-purchase gain) held their value. The digital-asset bet failed fast and completely: ErisX/Cboe Digital (~$564M, May 2022) had ~96% of its purchase price written off within 2–3 years — the entire $460.9M of goodwill impaired the same year it closed, plus an $81.0M intangible impairment in 2024.▪ The 2025–26 realignment is now reversing the 2020–2022 international tuck-ins (Japan wound down, Canada/Australia sold to TMX, CEDX wound down), which management quantifies as ~3% of net revenue and ~8–10% of opex — i.e. the units being exited were roughly breakeven-to-loss-making, a tacit admission several international deals did not earn their keep even where they produced no headline writedown.▪ The batting average is high inside the regulated U.S. derivatives/clearing/data core, where the moat is real, and poor every time Cboe stepped outside it; the capital at risk in the failures was modest relative to Bats, so the per-share damage is contained.

The buy-then-sell math on the periphery is not yet closeable: the TMX sale price for Canada and Australia is pending, so the cash-in/cash-out comparison against Chi-X Asia Pacific (~$289M) and NEO (~$222M) cannot be completed (flagged in Section 20).

12. Capitalization & balance sheet

Cboe runs a deliberately under-levered, all-fixed-rate balance sheet and is now net cash. Total debt is $1,442.9M (carrying; $1,450.0M principal) in three tranches of unsecured senior notes, against $2,216.5M cash and $36.1M financial investments — $774M net cash ($2,216.5M cash less $1,442.9M carrying debt). Interest coverage is ~32x against a 4.0x covenant floor.▪ The balance sheet does not break under ordinary stress; the structural risks are narrow and specific.

Weighted-average cost of debt. Principal-weighted coupon = (650×3.650% + 500×1.625% + 300×3.000%) / 1,450 = 2.82%. The all-in cash interest rate is ~3.46% ($49.9M FY2025 contractual interest ÷ $1,442.9M carrying) because the undrawn €1.2bn Cboe Clear Europe facility's commitment fee sits in interest expense. 100% of the stack is fixed-rate, so changes in market rates have no impact on interest paid; the only floating-rate exposure is undrawn.

Maturity wall and refinancing. The only near-term maturity is the $650M 3.65% notes due January 2027, refinanceable from cash on hand. Refinancing at current rates (or terming out a new ~5-year note) adds an estimated ~$10–13M of annual interest [estimate: a +1.5 to +2.0-point spread on the $650M tranche] — trivial against $1.59bn EBITDA, but it ends the era of sub-3% blended funding. Two of the three tranches were issued in the 2020–2022 low-rate window; the 1.625% notes are the standout cheap money, and that advantage runs off as tranches mature.

Ratings. The FY2025 10-K's debt risk factor states investment-grade ratings of S&P A− and Moody's A2 (FY2025 10-K Item 1A, line 4253). A downgrade below investment grade would raise the revolver's rating-based pricing, but with net cash and ~32x coverage the proximity risk is remote.

Leases, SBC, and dilution. Operating leases total $147.8M (no finance leases); there is no material defined-benefit pension. Net dilution is negative: RSU/PSU grants run ~370–380k shares/year (~0.35%), more than offset by buybacks and tax-withholding repurchases, so diluted weighted-average shares declined 112.2M (FY2018 peak) to 105.1M (FY2025). SBC expense is $50.4M (FY2025, ~2% of net revenue) and rising with the stock (PSU grant-date fair values rose with the share price); total unrecognized comp is $62.7M, about one year of expense.

Structural risks ranked. (1) The January 2027 $650M maturity and the end of sub-3% funding — low severity, near-certain, ~$10–13M of incremental interest if termed out. (2) The Cboe Clear Europe facility renewal (terminates June 26, 2026) — low-to-moderate, contingent; it is a clearinghouse liquidity backstop (Cboe guarantees only interest and fees), but failure to renew would impair settlement-liquidity capacity and feeds a cross-default chain. (3) Cross-default/cross-acceleration linkage from those facilities to the senior notes — low, structural. (4) Structural subordination of the holdco notes to ~$189.5M of subsidiary regulatory capital — low. (5) FX translation on ~$424M of offshore cash — low, non-leverage. No covenant-proximity risk (8x interest-coverage cushion), no floating-rate principal, no refi wall beyond the easily-funded 2027 tranche. (capitalization §2, §7.)

18. Risks

The dominant risk — the 2032–2033 SPX license, and the near-term prediction-market vector that could pull the cliff forward — is named throughout this dossier but sized nowhere. Its downside is order-of-magnitude (the ~$0.92 index RPC could compress toward the ~$0.066 multi-listed level) but unquantified; a bounded retention scenario is the single most valuable missing piece of analysis (Section 20). Risks are ranked by severity.

Tier 1 — existential / dominant-profit-pool

  • R1. Loss or non-renewal of the exclusive S&P 500 / SPX license (the binding constraint). SPX options can only be listed because of an exclusive S&P license, contractually exclusive only through ~2032–2033. Non-renewal on an exclusive basis makes SPX multi-listed (Cboe loses share and pricing power to fungible competition); non-renewal at all loses the product entirely. Exposure: ~68% of net revenue is the Options-plus-Futures segments, "the majority of which" is exclusively-licensed/proprietary index product; the 14x index-to-multi-listed RPC gap is the entire economic value of exclusivity.▪ Leading indicators: public renewal commentary; competitor filings to list S&P-500-economics products (a BofA analyst asked about this on the Q1 2026 call); any move toward MiFIR-style mandatory benchmark licensing. Mitigation: the SPX ecosystem (balanced flow, 0-DTE grid, floor depth, Section-1256 tax treatment) raises switching costs but does not change S&P's right to re-license.

  • R2. Erosion of the proprietary-product moat from event/prediction markets and new index-options entrants. New in the FY2025 risk factors. Two prongs: Kalshi-style platforms launching CFTC-overseen binary options on the S&P 500 that could replicate SPX economics outside Cboe's license (the near-term proxy for R1 — a competitor finding a way to offer S&P-500 economics before 2032), and competitors launching single-stock and index 0-DTE. The contest is partly jurisdictional (securities/SEC versus event-contract/CFTC). Exposure: the same ~68% pool. Leading indicators: regulatory rulings on whether broad-based-index binary contracts are securities or futures; ADV of competing S&P-500 event products; Cboe Predicts' own share. Mitigation: Cboe's own XSP binary options via OCC clearing and existing retail distribution — but the regulatory outcome is unsettled, and the June 2026 −32% drawdown shows the market is currently pricing this as a threat.

Tier 2 — major structural / cyclical

  • R3. Volume cyclicality and 0-DTE concentration. ~74% of net revenue is transaction-based and reprices with volume; growth has concentrated in SPX 0-DTE (~60–61% of SPX volume, up from low-20s% in early 2022). A volatility normalization or a 0-DTE-specific regulatory/risk event would hit the richest pool. The risk is not materializing now (Q1 2026 net revenue +29%), but 0-DTE durability has never been observed through a sustained low-VIX quarter. Leading indicators: 0-DTE % of SPX ADV; SPX ADV month-over-month; VIX regime; any FINRA/SEC 0-DTE retail-suitability action.

  • R4. Customer / clearing-member concentration. Top-10 customers ~57% of total revenue; one customer ~10% ($480.0M, rising ~19%, no contractual lock-in); three OCC clearing members clear ~71% of market-maker sides; ~69% of net transaction-and-clearing fees route through OCC. Exposure: a loss/routing shift by the ~10% customer or a clearing-member exit would hit volume disproportionately. The concentration is named but not pressure-tested — the customer is unidentified and the exposure is not split by segment (Section 20). Leading indicators: any disclosed reduction by the ~10% customer; a clearing-member exit.

  • R5. Multi-listed options + U.S. equities share erosion. Both products are fungible commodities; total options share slipped to 29.1% (Q1 2026) and multi-listed to 22.3%, U.S. equities to 9.8%, as MEMX/MIAX/off-exchange take fungible flow. Economically thin (multi-listed nets $0.066), so the share loss matters more for tape-data revenue and for keeping the SPX liquidity ecosystem intact than for profit. Leading indicators: monthly share prints; new-venue launches.

  • R6. Regulatory / market-structure rule changes. A thicket of pending rules: the SEC Tick Size / Access Fee Cap rule (effective Nov 2026, a headwind to thin equities transaction revenue), Rule 605/611 changes, a Volume-Based-Proposal that would impair equities pricing flexibility, the EU consolidated tape and PFOF ban (June 2026), and OCC UK third-country-CCP recognition (deadline Dec 2027, a tail risk to the options franchise). Not individually sized by management. Leading indicators: final-rule publication and implementation dates.

Tier 3 — operational, balance-sheet, capital-allocation

  • R7. Single-platform outage / third-party-infrastructure dependence. Cboe runs on one platform and depends on OCC (sole clearer), the SIPs, FINRA/CAT, and leased data centers. An SPX/VIX outage stops trading in Cboe's highest-revenue products, and no other venue lists them. The FY2025 10-K newly cites the Nov 2025 CME data-center halt as a live example. Mitigation: disaster recovery, but management concedes back-ups "may prove to be inadequate."

  • R8. Goodwill / intangible impairment from the acquisition-and-retreat cycle (realized, repeating). Cboe Digital $460.9M (2022) plus $81.0M (2024); Canada/CEDX/Japan impairments in 2025 — three impairment events in four years. The 2025 realignment removes the recurring source; FY2025 goodwill test showed no current impairment. Leading indicator: new "held for sale" classifications.

  • R9. Strategic-realignment execution risk. A ~12-month-tenure CEO plus new COO are simultaneously winding down Japan/CEDX, selling Canada/Australia, cutting ~20% of the workforce, and launching prediction markets/SFT clearing/tokenization. Risk of disruption, key-person loss, and mis-execution on the new bets. Leading indicators: segment net-revenue trajectory through the exits; executive turnover; milestone slippage.

  • R10. Clearinghouse default / liquidity risk (Cboe Clear Europe / U.S.). As CCP, Cboe is buyer-to-every-seller; a member default exceeding margin plus default fund hits Cboe. Cboe Clear Europe relies on a €1.2bn facility expiring June 26, 2026; failure to renew impairs settlement-liquidity capacity and feeds the cross-default chain. Leading indicator: the June 2026 facility renewal; member-default events.

  • R11. Balance-sheet / refinancing. Low severity: $650M 3.65% notes due 2027 (refinanceable from cash), S&P A−/Moody's A2 ratings, net cash, ~32x coverage. A downgrade below investment grade raises revolver pricing. Cyclicality and FX translation on ~$424M offshore cash are minor.

The bad-quarter scenario is a cluster, not one factor. A volatility-regime collapse (R3) simultaneously cuts the index pool, 0-DTE engagement, and market-data demand — and a low-vol stretch is exactly when multi-listed/equities share loss (R5) bites hardest because there is less total flow to optimize. A regulatory cluster (R6 + R2's jurisdictional ruling) would compress pricing flexibility across equities and attack the index pool at once. The 2032 cliff (R1) is the dominant single risk, but its near-term proxy is R2 — a competitor offering S&P-500 economics outside the license before 2032 pulls the cliff forward. (risks.)
