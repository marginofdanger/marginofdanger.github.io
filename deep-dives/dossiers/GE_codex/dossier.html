1. Business Description, Model And Unit Economics

What GE Aerospace sells

Commercial Engines & Services is the profit center: it was about 73% of FY2025 GE Aerospace revenue, and services were 75% of CES revenue. GE sells engines, parts, repairs, shop visits, technical support and long-term service coverage. The engine sale matters because it puts certified equipment under wing; the money over time is mostly earned after the engine starts flying.

Defense & Propulsion Technologies was about 23% of FY2025 revenue and still had a 51% services mix. It includes defense engines, marine and aeroderivative applications, Avio Aero, Unison, Dowty, additive manufacturing and other propulsion technologies. DPT is lower margin than CES, but it has sustainment tails and defense program exposure rather than a pure equipment profile.

How the money flows

GE’s commercial engine model turns a platform win into decades of parts, repairs, shop visits and service-contract economics. A new engine can be sold at weak early economics because it creates a certified installed base. Flight hours consume parts. Cycles create removals. Removals create shop visits. Shop visits create spare-part, repair and labor demand. The same field data then feeds durability fixes and the next program bid.

Long-term service agreements and RPO give GE visibility, but revenue and cash still depend on shop-visit execution. At Q1 2026, total RPO was $211.3 billion, of which $179.9 billion was services. That is real demand. It is not cash in the bank. The conversion requires parts, mechanics, repair approvals, engine inductions, workscopes and customer billings.

Unit economics and cost structure

GE does not disclose a complete per-engine lifetime profit bridge. Engine placements build the installed base; departures and cycles create wear; removals trigger workscopes; GE sells the certified material and the repair. The cost side is labor, supplier parts, warranty/durability reserves, R&D, certification, inventory and the cost of building capacity before demand is fully mature.

The 2026 segment change puts the full commercial engine lifecycle in CES and moves Aeroderivatives into DPT. This matters because the forward segment tables are closer to how management now runs the business than the old pre-2026 location of the aeroderivatives line.

Platform anatomy

GE Aerospace is easier to understand by engine family than by a single company average. CFM56 engines are late in life and still generating shop visits. LEAP is the fleet that must replace that profit: 1,802 delivered in 2025, service margins not yet mature. GE90 and GEnx carry widebody services today. GE9X is still loss-making ahead of 777X. DPT is a defense and propulsion portfolio with its own sustainment economics. The consolidated margin is the weighted average of these different curves, not one generic aerospace margin.

Long-term service agreements are economics and accounting

GE’s long-term service agreements create durable visibility, but the accounting depends on estimates of future billings, costs, workscopes and utilization. This is why a services-heavy engine company can look smoother than the underlying mechanics. A contract may run 10 to 25 years, but the profit recognized in any period depends on future shop visits, repair yield, inflation, warranty exposure and customer utilization. When GE revises the expected cost of completing those contracts, the income statement moves before a mechanic has touched the next engine.

A usage-based service agreement can collect cash as aircraft fly, recognize revenue as GE performs, and carry a liability or asset depending on where billings sit relative to expected costs. The contract improves visibility because it ties GE to the fleet. It also creates duration risk because a bad durability assumption or supplier cost problem can be embedded across many future periods.

What one engine placement buys

A commercial engine placement buys GE a long service opportunity rather than a one-time equipment sale. The first sale can have unattractive economics because GE is not trying to maximize the invoice on day one. It is buying access to the aircraft's operating life. Once the engine is certified on an airframe and delivered to a customer, GE can sell life-limited parts, repairs, shop labor, technical data, spare engines, service coverage and future upgrades. This is why launch economics and mature economics can point in opposite directions.

For the owner, the test is not whether engine deliveries rise in isolation. The test is whether each delivery becomes a profitable service annuity after warranty, durability work, partner economics and customer bargaining. LEAP has the scale to be the next large service engine; it still has to earn the CFM56 economics that the mature fleet already proved.

Working capital is part of the business model

The business consumes inventory before it releases revenue. A late supplier part can strand an almost-finished engine, delay a shop visit, increase work in process and push cash conversion into the next quarter. The same inventory can be valuable if it lets GE clear spare-parts backlog or complete inducted engines faster. That makes inventory quality more important than inventory size. The best signal is not one balance-sheet line; it is engine deliveries, spare-parts shipments, shop-visit completion and FCF conversion moving together.

2. Key Technologies And Technical Differentiators

Certified propulsion systems

GE or CFM engines power a very large share of commercial flight activity, and GE cites roughly 80,000 engines plus more than 2.3 billion flight hours as its field-data base. Certification, dispatch reliability, part traceability and airworthiness support make aircraft engines unlike ordinary industrial products. A new competitor cannot simply copy a blade or combustor and sell into a fleet; it must win airframer selection, certify the engine, support it for decades and persuade airlines that uptime will not suffer.

Durability, repair development and time on wing

Durability and time-on-wing are economic variables because they determine customer cost, shop-visit timing and warranty exposure. A longer on-wing interval lowers the customer’s cost of ownership and delays removals; a better repair catalog lets GE keep the engine flying with less new material. The same work can lower GE’s contract cost if the engine sits inside an LTSA.

RISE and the next aircraft generation

RISE is the main GE/CFM future-engine technology program, with open-fan, compact-core and hybrid-electric work aimed at a step-change in fuel burn. RISE is not yet a certified product. It protects technical relevance for the next cycle, but the hard tests are aircraft integration, noise, airport compatibility, certification, customer acceptance and whether the technology creates economics that survive the launch bargain.

Defense and advanced programs

Defense growth includes today’s engine platforms and advanced work in adaptive-cycle engines, hybrid-electric systems, hypersonics and unmanned applications. These programs carry long sustainment tails when GE wins, but they also live under government budgets, classified development cycles and procurement rules.

GE guides R&D to 6-8% of sales as a continuing level, not a one-off program cost. That level of spending is the cost of defending current fleets and staying relevant for the next propulsion cycle.

Engine technology pays only when it extends time on wing

Aircraft-engine technology matters when it lowers customer cost or protects dispatch reliability. A blade material is valuable if it extends time on wing. A repair process is valuable if it avoids a new part and returns the engine faster. A digital model is valuable if it improves removal forecasts, shop planning or durability fixes. The owner should read GE's technology claims through operating outputs: time on wing, shop-visit cost, spare-parts demand, warranty cost and customer acceptance.

Durability sets both customer cost and GE's service cost

Durability and time-on-wing are both customer metrics and margin metrics for GE. If a durability kit extends an engine's time on wing, the airline gets more utilization before removal and GE lowers the cost of meeting service obligations. If an engine comes off wing early, the customer's spare-engine burden rises and GE may absorb extra cost through warranty, concessions or LTSA revisions. This is why management talks about repair capability and time on wing in the same breath as margin.

RISE is a real option, not current earnings power

RISE is best read as a next-cycle technology option rather than a current commercial product. The program matters because future airframes will be chosen around fuel burn, emissions, noise, reliability and certification risk. It does not yet tell the owner what GE will earn. Open-fan architecture may offer a fuel-burn step change; it also has to fit an aircraft, pass noise and safety requirements, and persuade airlines that the operating economics beat a conventional design. The right question is not whether RISE is interesting. The question is whether it becomes a certified engine family with attractive service economics.

Defense technology has a different proof cycle

Defense propulsion technology is proven through program awards, funded development, qualification and sustainment rather than airline fleet economics. Adaptive-cycle engines, T901, F110, F404, F414, LM2500, hypersonic and unmanned applications all sit under government procurement. The technology can be durable once embedded in a fleet, but the route to revenue runs through budgets, classified requirements, testing, foreign military sales and contract modification rules.

Current fleets versus future fleets

The technology stack has to be read in two time horizons. The current earnings base comes from certified fleets already in service: CFM56, LEAP, GE90, GEnx, CF34 and defense engines. The future relevance comes from GE9X, RISE, advanced military propulsion, additive manufacturing and materials work. These are connected but not identical. A better future engine does not protect today's CFM56 cash tail; a profitable CFM56 cash tail does not by itself win the next aircraft generation.

Why repair technology can be more valuable than new-engine technology

Repair technology is less glamorous than a new engine, but it can be more directly economic. If GE can repair a part instead of replacing it, the customer gets a lower cost and GE can keep the engine inside the OEM technical system. If GE expands a repair catalog for LEAP, the same installed base can produce better service margins and better customer economics. This is why repair breadth, approved procedures and shop capability should be monitored beside engine deliveries.

FLIGHT DECK as an operating technology

FLIGHT DECK is GE’s operating system for improving material flow, output, delivery and shop performance. It is not a moat merely because the name is proprietary. It matters only if it changes factory and shop behavior: fewer shortages, faster turn times, better supplier input, less rework, more completed engines and more completed shop visits. The right evidence is throughput and cash conversion, not management vocabulary.

3. Competitive Advantages And Moat Assessment

The advantage is real, but it has named constraints

GE Aerospace has a durable position where it owns or co-owns certified engine families with long lives. The advantage is strongest in CFM narrowbody services, mature widebody services and selected defense sustainment. It is weaker where GE is still buying future economics through new-engine losses, where CFM economics are shared with Safran, and where suppliers can keep demand from turning into shipments.

Installed base and flight data

The installed base matters because it gives GE both demand and information. More cycles create more removals, more material demand and more field data. That data improves repair development, forecasting and future bids. The caveat is mix: a CFM56 shop visit, a LEAP durability kit and a GE9X launch loss are not economically equivalent.

Airframe lock-in

CFM is structurally important because it is the 50-50 GE/Safran vehicle behind CFM56, LEAP and RISE. LEAP is sole-source on the 737 MAX through LEAP-1B and competes on the A320neo through LEAP-1A. GE9X is sole-source on 777X. These positions narrow competition after certification, but the launch sale can still include discounts, guarantees and concessions.

Open MRO without abandoning the material economics

GE/CFM uses an open MRO network to add capacity and lower customer ownership cost while preserving OEM material and technical content. Third-party shops help customers and can increase fleet value. They also give large airlines more bargaining power. GE’s economic defense is that certified material, repairs, engineering approval and technical data remain central even when a third party turns the wrench.

GE is investing materially in MRO capacity, including dedicated LEAP capacity, so shop visits do not become the next bottleneck. The investment is a capacity decision and a customer-economics decision: GE needs enough shops, repairs and parts to turn installed-base growth into revenue.

Financial tests

The numbers improved sharply after GE became mostly aerospace and services-heavy. Adjusted revenue rose from $26.2 billion in 2022 to $42.3 billion in 2025; operating margin rose from 16.1% to 21.4%; FCF rose from $3.5 billion to $7.7 billion. The remaining test is whether GE can keep margins above 20% while LEAP and GE9X scale and the supply chain catches up.

Standalone financial tests

The standalone financial evidence supports a strong business, but it is a short pure-play record. From 2022 to 2025, adjusted revenue rose from $26.2 billion to $42.3 billion, operating profit rose from $4.2 billion to $9.1 billion, and FCF rose from $3.5 billion to $7.7 billion. The pattern is what an installed-base services business should show when utilization recovers and service mix rises. The limitation is that the period also includes post-COVID recovery, portfolio cleanup and constrained aircraft supply, so it cannot be treated as a full-cycle proof by itself.

Peer comparison

GE's peer set is awkward because no public company is a perfect copy. Safran shares CFM economics. RTX owns Pratt & Whitney plus Collins and Raytheon. Rolls is widebody-heavy. TransDigm and Howmet are component and proprietary-parts businesses. Their existence is still useful. They show that scarcity in aerospace can accrue at different layers: engine OEMs, JV partners, proprietary components, certified repairs and MRO. GE's advantage is broad and deep, but not exclusive across the chain.

Counter-evidence

The strongest counter-evidence is not that GE lacks technology. It is that other parties can claim value before the engine reaches GE's income statement. Safran shares CFM economics. Boeing and Airbus influence timing and launch economics. Airlines can bargain on service agreements and MRO access. Suppliers can constrain output. Pratt can recover in A320neo. Rolls controls the A350 engine pool. A durable GE advantage therefore has to be argued at the interface level, not simply asserted from fleet size.

What would prove the advantage is widening

The cleanest proof would be rising services revenue with stable or improving CES margin, declining spare-parts delinquency, LEAP repair capability expanding, LEAP service margins approaching mature CES services, and FCF conversion staying above net income while capex funds capacity. The opposite proof would be service growth with margin compression, repeated LTSA charges, rising supplier penalties, customer MRO capture without OEM material pull-through, or a faster CFM56 retirement curve before LEAP profit matures.

What GE controls and what it does not

GE controls engineering, certification support, many repairs, OEM material, internal shops, customer service and a large part of the engine knowledge base. GE does not control aircraft delivery schedules, airline traffic, the full CFM economics, all suppliers, defense budgets or every MRO decision. A strong moat does not mean control of every variable. It means that when the system operates normally, GE captures a large share of the value created by keeping engines flying.

The owner's conclusion

The advantage is durable but not effortless. GE owns or co-owns long-lived engine positions that customers cannot replace casually. The economics are best where the fleet is mature, flying, and dependent on OEM material and repairs. The economics are least proven where the engine is young, still absorbing durability work, or still waiting for the aircraft platform to ramp. That mix makes GE a strong business whose next decade of margin depends on LEAP service economics and supplier output, not on the installed base alone.

Narrowbody engines

The mature CFM56 fleet still has economic runway because retirements have been lower than expected and many engines still had shop visits ahead. The narrowbody market is concentrated around GE/Safran’s CFM, Pratt & Whitney and the airframers. GE’s narrowbody exposure is powerful, but not identical to full economics because CFM is shared with Safran and A320neo remains dual-source.

LEAP transition

LEAP is the central transition: installed base and shop visits rise, then service profitability must catch up with the mature fleet. The evidence to watch is not just deliveries. It is durability, time on wing, repair breadth, internal and external shop capacity, spare-parts availability and whether LEAP service margins approach the broader CES service pool around the 2028 period management has described.

Widebody and defense

Widebody engines provide a second service leg through GE90, GEnx and GE9X. Widebody fleets are smaller than narrowbody fleets but higher value. Defense is different again: it is governed by budgets, foreign military sales, sustainment awards and next-generation program choices.

Management says GE or CFM powers about 75% of narrowbody cycles and 55% of widebody cycles. That is utilization, not new-order share, revenue share or GE-only profit share. It is an economically useful metric because cycles produce wear, removals and parts demand. It is not a clean ownership metric because CFM is shared with Safran, old and new fleets carry different economics, and airline retirements change the service tail.

Pratt's GTF durability issues likely improved LEAP's relative standing with airlines and lessors. But a competitor's failure is not a permanent moat. Why Rolls matters outside narrowbody

Rolls is the widebody counterweight. GE has GE90, GEnx and GE9X positions, including the 777X sole-source engine. Rolls owns the A350 engine position and competes on 787. This matters because widebody shop visits are valuable and long-cycle, but the pools are platform-specific. GE's widebody case is not that it owns every route; it is that GE90 and GEnx remain valuable while GE9X builds the next service base.

5. Value Chain And Profit Pools

The loop

R&D and certification win an engine position. The position builds an installed base. Utilization creates removals. Removals create spare parts, repairs and shop visits. The service experience feeds the next durability fix and the next program bid.

Commercial engine OE deliveries can pressure near-term margin while creating the installed-base funnel that later drives high-value services. The owner should read OE growth and service growth together; one depresses near-term mix, the other is the reason to accept the mix pressure.

Where value can leak

Value can leak at launch through concessions, through Safran’s share of CFM economics, through sole-source suppliers, through customer-owned MRO capability, through durability misses, and through defense budget changes. These are not generic risks. They are the interfaces where a strong installed-base business can lose margin.

Customer-side economics

An airline buys reliability and aircraft availability. An engine with longer time on wing lowers spare-engine needs, disruption cost, maintenance planning risk and fuel burn. A service agreement can trade unpredictable repair events for planned cost. That customer-side logic is why GE can earn attractive service economics even when the customer has bargaining power. The customer is not buying a part in isolation; it is buying dispatch reliability and a lower probability that an aircraft sits idle.

Supplier bargaining and bottlenecks

Supplier power appears as missed revenue before it appears as lost share. A constrained casting, forging, control, accessory or repair input can hold up an engine or shop visit. GE can have demand, backlog and customer urgency and still miss a quarter because one qualified part is not available. In aerospace, qualification makes substitution slow. The same certification barrier that protects GE against new entrants can protect an upstream supplier against rapid replacement.

Open MRO as a bargain with customers

GE gives customers more repair options and capacity, and in exchange keeps the OEM material, technical data and approved-repair economics. The trade is rational. Airlines want choice, lower cost and faster turnaround. GE wants installed-base preference and material pull-through. The model works if third-party shops still depend on GE-approved repairs, OEM parts and technical support. It weakens if independent material, customer-owned shops or used-serviceable parts capture too much of the economics.

Defense chain economics

DPT has a different chain. The customer is often a government or defense prime. The revenue event can be development funding, engine delivery, sustainment, spares, overhaul or foreign military sales. The moat is less about airline fleet utilization and more about qualification, mission risk, program history and national-security procurement. The cash risk is also different: contract audits, cost growth, protests, program shifts and budget timing can matter more than passenger traffic.

Supply recovery is a growth driver, not just a cost item

FLIGHT DECK is economically relevant only if it raises output, lowers rework, improves supplier flow and shortens shop turnaround time. A backlog-heavy engine company can grow simply by turning constrained demand into shipped product and completed shop visits. That makes operational execution a growth lever. 7. Critical Points

  1. The business is now mostly the aerospace engine loop. GE still has legacy residue, but the operating center is CES services.
  2. Services RPO and LTSA billings provide long visibility, but execution still turns on parts, shop visits and contract assumptions.
  3. CFM56 cash tail and LEAP ramp must overlap cleanly. If CFM56 fades faster than LEAP services mature, margins compress.
  4. Supply chain is what currently limits revenue. Demand already exceeds parts availability in important pockets.
  5. GE9X is strategically valuable and near-term dilutive. The cash curve depends on Boeing 777X timing and GE9X maturation.
  6. CFM is both moat and sharing mechanism. GE has excellent narrowbody exposure, but Safran participates in the same profit pool.
  7. The standalone capital plan is meaningful because FCF is large and the old conglomerate balance-sheet repair is mostly complete.

1. The company is now simple enough to underwrite, but not simple to model

GE Aerospace is no longer a conglomerate puzzle. The operating company is mostly engines and services. The modeling problem remains hard because the economics sit across engine cohorts, service contracts, CFM partner economics, supplier capacity, defense programs and legacy insurance. A clean ticker does not mean clean unit economics. The business must be read as a set of installed-base curves.

2. Backlog is visibility, not conversion

The $211.3 billion Q1 2026 RPO number is important because most of it is services. It tells the reader that demand is already attached to engines and contracts. It does not tell the reader when parts will arrive, which workscopes will be performed, how contract cost estimates will move, or how much cash will be released in a given quarter. The company can be demand-rich and supply-constrained at the same time.

3. CFM56 is not finished; LEAP is not yet mature

This is the central bridge. CFM56 remains valuable because many engines have later shop visits ahead and aircraft retirements have been lower than expected. LEAP is valuable because it is building the next narrowbody service base. The risk is timing. If mature CFM56 profit declines before LEAP service margin and repair capability mature, GE can still grow revenue while the margin mix disappoints.

4. GE9X is a strategic asset with a near-term cost

The 777X position gives GE a future widebody platform. It also brings development, certification, production and early-loss burden before the service base exists. A new widebody engine is not bad because it loses money early; that is the normal curve. The risk is that Boeing timing, cost reduction or durability delays stretch the early-loss period.

5. CFM is both the moat and the sharing mechanism

CFM gives GE the narrowbody position that drives much of the long-term story. It also means GE shares economics and governance with Safran. A GE-only view that treats all LEAP and CFM56 economics as wholly owned is too strong. A bear view that ignores GE's CFM operating role and service participation is too weak. The right analysis keeps the JV boundary visible.

6. The supply chain decides the next few years

In a normal industrial business, supply constraints are often a transitory excuse. In aircraft engines, qualified suppliers, repair approvals, casting capacity, certification rules and customer safety requirements make the bottleneck more durable. That does not make it permanent. It makes the operating system, supplier development and inventory quality central to the case.

Backlog versus cash

Backlog carries no fixed margin. GE has strong evidence of demand, especially in commercial services. The open question is conversion. A shop visit waiting for parts is demand, not revenue; an LTSA with worse cost assumptions is visibility and exposure at once.

Services mix versus service quality

A high services mix normally signals quality. GE's services mix is real, and CES is structurally better than a pure equipment business. But service quality differs by platform. CFM56 mature spare parts, LEAP early shop visits, GE90 widebody workscopes and defense sustainment are not the same profit pool. The better question is whether the service mix is shifting toward or away from mature, high-return work.

Open MRO versus captive aftermarket

A captive aftermarket sounds attractive, but it can make customers resist the engine at selection. Open MRO lowers customer cost, protects aircraft values and adds capacity. The trade is capture. The model works if GE keeps the high-value material, repairs, engineering data and approvals. It weakens if independent shops and customer-owned maintenance capture a rising share of the economics.

Pure-play premium versus old-GE residue

GE Aerospace deserves to be analyzed as an aerospace company, not as the old conglomerate. But the residue still matters. Insurance, taxes, separation costs, transition services and pension or legacy obligations can distort GAAP results and cash in ways that do not belong to the operating engine business. The cleanest owner view bridges from reported GE to Aerospace operating profit and FCF, then separately tracks legacy items.

Cash, debt and interest

The balance sheet is no longer the central objection it was for old GE. GE Aerospace still has borrowings, cash, insurance liabilities and legacy obligations, but the operating company now produces enough FCF to make capital allocation an active choice rather than a repair program. Share count and buyback mechanics

Buybacks matter because GE's FCF base is large relative to its share count. But repurchases are only useful after R&D, supply-chain recovery, MRO capacity, durability and safety are funded. Legacy obligations

Legacy items should not be ignored just because the operating story is cleaner. Insurance liabilities, taxes, separation items and other old-GE obligations can affect reported cash and GAAP earnings. They are not the core aerospace engine economics, but they are claims on the same legal entity. The report therefore separates operating quality from balance-sheet cleanup rather than blending them into one margin number.

15. Forward Model Summary

Model construction

The base model starts with 2026 management guidance, then lets services grow faster than equipment while margins rise only gradually. That matters because the model does not assume a clean straight line from installed base to profit. It leaves room for LEAP maturity, GE9X losses, supplier delays, inflation, tariff mitigation and defense mix.

Recent margin expansion came from services volume, price, mix and lower equipment drag, not simply from unit growth. The medium-term margin bridge depends on LEAP services improving while GE9X losses peak around 2028 and become less dilutive later. The owner’s question is whether those two curves overlap well enough to keep operating margin above 20% while revenue grows.

Management’s cash-generation thesis is that higher earnings convert at or above net income despite inventory and supply-chain headwinds. The model keeps FCF above $8 billion in 2026 and rising toward $11.4 billion by 2030, with conversion above 100% but not treated as automatic.

Management bridge versus the model

The central departure from a stale target

The March 2024 Investor Day framework is no longer the right ceiling. GE later guided 2026 operating profit to roughly the same level as the old 2028 operating-profit target. The model therefore treats the old target as a historical bridge, not as a current cap. The key judgment is how much of the 2024-2026 acceleration was cyclical recovery and how much is durable installed-base earnings power.

Top sensitivities

The largest operating sensitivities are LEAP service margin, GE9X loss duration, CFM56 retirement pace, spare-parts availability, shop-visit workscopes, defense mix, tariff cost and working capital. A one-year delay in LEAP service maturity matters more than a small change in near-term OE deliveries because LEAP is the bridge from today's fleet to the next decade's service pool.

Management’s later-cycle margin inflection depends on LEAP services approaching broader CES service profitability while GE9X losses peak around 2028. That is the cleanest test of the medium-term case. If LEAP margins rise and GE9X losses peak, growth can compound with margin expansion. If both slip, GE can still grow revenue while operating leverage disappoints.

Scenario differences

Scenario driver Operating effect Evidence needed
Base case Services grow faster than equipment, margin expands gradually, FCF conversion remains above net income. Stable CES margin, improving LEAP service economics, controlled GE9X losses.
Upside Supply improves faster, CFM56 stays active longer, LEAP repairs scale, and GE9X cost reduction arrives on time. Lower spare-parts delinquency, faster shop turns, higher LEAP service profitability.
Downside Supply delays persist, LEAP service cost remains high, GE9X losses extend, and working capital absorbs cash. Inventory build without delivery growth, LTSA charges, delayed 777X cadence.

Consensus versus management versus internal model

Management guidance is most useful for 2026 because it is tied to current backlog, departures, tariffs and supply-chain assumptions. Bloomberg/BEst consensus is useful for market context because it shows where external expectations sit for EPS, EBIT and EBITDA. The internal model is useful because it forces the driver bridge to 2030: LEAP maturity, CFM56 fade, GE9X losses, DPT growth and FCF conversion. These three views should not be blended. They answer different questions.

What would make the model too high

The model would be too high if 2026 services growth proves front-loaded, if supply-chain recovery requires more inventory than expected, if LEAP service margin takes longer to approach CES service levels, if GE9X losses persist beyond the expected peak, or if tariff and inflation offsets require customer concessions. The first warning would probably appear in spare-parts backlog, shop-visit throughput, CES margin and working capital rather than in headline RPO.

What would make the model too low

The model would be too low if mature fleets stay in service longer, LEAP repair capability lowers service cost faster, GE9X production losses decline earlier, defense growth remains strong, and FLIGHT DECK improves material flow enough to release revenue from the backlog. In that case the same installed-base logic would produce both higher revenue and better cash conversion.

16. Risks

Risk Mechanism Exposure Leading indicator
Supply chain and parts availability Late or scarce parts delay engine deliveries, shop visits and spare shipments. Spare-parts delinquency, supplier bottlenecks, inflation and tariff cost. Material input, spare-parts backlog, shop-visit induction and engine-delivery cadence.
LEAP and GE9X margin curve New programs build future services but dilute current profit if durability, cost or delivery timing disappoint. LEAP repair catalog, GE9X losses, Boeing 777X timing. LEAP time on wing, service margins, GE9X certification/delivery updates.
CFM economics and governance GE shares CFM economics with Safran and must govern narrowbody technology through a JV. LEAP, CFM56 and RISE. Safran disclosures, CFM delivery/service trends, RISE program milestones.
Airframer and airline bargaining Airframers and airlines can demand concessions, guarantees and timing changes. OE margins, spare-engine ratios, delivery penalties, service contracts. Boeing/Airbus schedules, major order terms, customer MRO announcements.
Tariffs and inflation Costs rise before GE recovers them through price, productivity or customer action. GE estimated a net tariff impact of roughly $500 million in 2025, which it is offsetting through cost controls and pricing actions. Tariff updates, price/cost spread, supplier inflation.

Supply chain is the central execution bottleneck because management said more than 80% of 2024 delivery challenges were rooted somewhere in the supply chain. GE still expects supply-chain constraints and inflation to remain headwinds that require pricing, productivity and supplier engagement. Airframer delivery schedules and OEM production constraints can change GE equipment revenue even when aftermarket demand is strong. Tariffs became a material 2025 risk after GE estimated a net tariff impact of roughly $500 million in 2025, which it is offsetting through cost controls and pricing actions. A bad quarter can combine these risks: a supplier delay pushes spare parts and shop visits, LEAP/GE9X mix worsens, tariffs hit cost, and GE absorbs the cash effect in inventory and working capital before it can fully price the problem.

Correlated bad-quarter scenario

The most realistic bad quarter is not one isolated risk. Boeing or Airbus timing slips. A qualified supplier misses deliveries. Spare-parts demand remains high but delinquency rises. LEAP or GE9X mix worsens. Tariffs and inflation raise cost before offsets arrive. GE then reports healthy backlog, weaker conversion of demand into revenue, pressured segment margin and a working-capital use. That sequence would not mean the franchise is broken.

What evidence would reduce the risks

The best risk reducers are operating facts: lower spare-parts delinquency, faster shop turnaround, stable LEAP time on wing, expanding repair capability, no material LTSA charges, a clear GE9X delivery and cost path, and FCF conversion above net income while capex funds capacity. Management commentary is useful only when those operating facts move with it.

What would force a more cautious read

The caution signs are equally concrete: repeated supplier misses, rising inventories without delivery growth, customer MRO announcements that reduce OEM material content, Pratt share recovery without GE price response, Safran disclosures showing CFM economics diverging from GE expectations, GE9X losses extending beyond the expected peak, or defense wins that require heavy upfront investment without visible sustainment economics.

Risk severity by timing

The near-term risks are supply, tariff cost, GE9X losses and shop-visit throughput. The medium-term risks are LEAP service economics, CFM56 retirement pace and CFM/Safran sharing. The long-term risks are next-generation propulsion, defense program choices and whether airframers or customers shift more value away from the engine OEM. That timing matters because a one-quarter supplier miss and a failed next-generation engine program should not be weighted the same way.

LTSA estimate risk

Long-term service agreements create accounting sensitivity. A small change in expected contract profitability can move the LTSA balance materially because GE is estimating cost and billings over long lives. The risk is not that LTSAs are bad; they are central to the model. The risk is that inflation, workscopes, durability or utilization move against GE after revenue has already been recognized on an expected-cost basis.

Regulatory and safety risk

Safety is not a boilerplate risk for an engine company. A quality event can cause inspections, service bulletins, customer disruption, warranty cost, delivery delay, certification scrutiny and reputational damage with airframers. The same certification system that protects GE also raises the cost of errors. The monitoring items are airworthiness directives, service bulletins, shop-visit surprises and management language around durability.
