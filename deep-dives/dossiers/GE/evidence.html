Claims by section
business-model
  • The model is a razor and a blade. CES sells a commercial engine at or below cost (LEAP and GE9X both run at OE losses today), then earns its return over the next 20-to-30 years selling that engine its certified spare parts and shop visits. Management's own lifecycle arithmetic puts the original sale at only ~25% of an engine's lifecycle revenue and the three subsequent shop visits at ~75%, so each fielded engine throws off roughly 3.5x its new-engine revenue in services over its life [deck: 2024-03-07 GE Aerospace Investor Day, slide 28].
  • the *installed* engine, not the annual delivery, is the unit that compounds. GE's fielded base is ~50,000 commercial and ~30,000 military engines, more than 2.3 billion flight hours (10-K FY2025, Item 1).
  • Aftermarket splits roughly 60% internal shop visits and 40% spare parts (Q1 2026 call), so of FY2025 CES services revenue of $25,010M, approximately $15.0B is shop visits and $10.0B is spare parts `[estimate: 60/40 split applied to disclosed services revenue]`.
  • The annuity has barely begun to pay out on the newest fleet: roughly one-third of CFM56 engines have not had a first shop visit and two-thirds have not had a second (Q1 2026 call), and ~85% of long-term service-agreement lifetime billings are still ahead (2024 Investor Day).
  • a large slice of CES revenue passes straight through to risk-and-revenue-sharing partners (RRSPs) and the CFM JV structure, a feature most industrials lack. Net payments to collaboration participants were $5,887M in FY2025 (cost of services $4,941M + cost of equipment $946M), roughly 27% of CES cost of revenue and ~18% of CES revenue (10-K FY2025, Note on collaborative arrangements).
  • The aftermarket is margin-rich because a repaired part can cost ~50% less than a new part (Q1 2026 call) and the work runs on the customer's asset, not GE's plant.
  • DPT runs government and OEM contracting, roughly half services, at less than half CES's margin.
  • The single most common misframing is "cost-plus defense contractor": the disclosed defense mix is ~50% fixed-price, ~40% commercial, ~10% cost-plus (2024 Investor Day, slide 64). Fixed-price means GE bears cost-overrun risk on the largest slice and keeps its own productivity gains.
  • the government funds most of the development: customer-and-partner-funded R&D was ~$1.4B in FY2025, primarily from the U.S. Government, so GE's own DPT R&D outlay is only ~$308M (10-K FY2025).
  • ~16% of DPT revenue ($1,686M) is sold *into* other GE segments - overwhelmingly Avio/Unison/Dowty components going into CES engines and the CFM JV - so DPT is partly a leveraged play on GE's own commercial ramp.
  • P&AT compounded 15.8%/yr 2022-2025 (Avio-led) versus D&S at 6.6%/yr; P&AT is the growth engine inside the segment.
  • The "$1,987M revenue / 4.3% of total" headline is an accounting residual: $3,533M of run-off insurance investment income minus $1,546M of intercompany eliminations, neither of which is going-concern aerospace revenue.
  • The model is a CES aftermarket annuity, and it is ~73% of revenue at a 26.6% margin. Roughly half of total GE revenue is commercial aftermarket. Each fielded engine throws off ~3.5x its OE value in services over 20+ years, and ~85% of LSA lifetime billings are still ahead. This is the entire investment case and the source of the moat; everything else is second-order.
  • Reported CES margin is suppressed, not compounding, until ~2028 - and the recent quarter shows it. CES margin declined -230 bps to 26.4% in Q1 2026 because the LEAP/GE9X OE ramp has negative incremental margin, even though services margins rose year-on-year. Management guides FY2026 margin "flattish." The back-end-loaded expansion depends on LEAP aftermarket margins reaching CES-service level (~2028) and GE9X losses peaking (~2028), neither yet shipped.
  • **The growth rate is recovery-inflated and must fade.** GE's recast +16.3% three-year revenue CAGR sits *above* the entire 17-company reference class's 10-year maximum (+14.2%, HEICO); only HEICO and the payments duopoly sustained double-digit revenue for a decade. Roughly 3 of GE's ~14 points are durable air-travel volume; the rest is annuity timing, price, and a non-repeating recovery/supply-catch-up tailwind that exhausts ~2027-28.
  • **GE shares half the commercial business with Safran.** CFM is a non-consolidated 50/50 JV. Headline CES revenue ($33.3B) understates the commercial business, but GE's equity-method pickup was only $376M in FY2025 — there is no large hidden pool, and half the platform rent is Safran's.
competitive-advantage
  • CES holds a genuine, durable competitive advantage that sits close to the strongest end of the spectrum — a structural moat, not merely a contestable edge — but it is durable specifically over the *installed fleet*, and the one place it is contestable is the next-generation narrowbody platform that does not yet exist.
  • First, a sole-source aftermarket created at the point of OE sale: once an A320neo or 737 MAX flies a CFM engine, GE is the certified source for that engine's hot-section parts and overhaul for the asset's life, and the airline cannot re-tender it.
  • The margin gap is the single best moat evidence: the CES segment earned a 26.6% profit margin in FY2025 against 7.9% at RTX's Pratt & Whitney — the only true head-to-head engine segment in the comp set — an 18.7-point gap on a clean apples-to-apples basis.
  • The gap is not an accounting illusion: Safran, the independently-reporting other half of CFM, earned a 23.0% propulsion margin in FY2025 on the same platform, confirming the platform, not GE's ledger, generates the rent.
  • GE does *not* claim a patent moat.
  • a clean-sheet large commercial engine costs billions and a decade-plus to develop and certify, and is sold at or below cost; no new entrant has launched a Western large commercial engine in decades.
  • Switching costs are absolute at the engine level by physics and regulation — every spare part and overhaul must be certified for that engine type, so the customer cannot switch mid-life without re-engining (uneconomic) or qualifying a PMA alternative (slow, non-critical parts only). Buyer power is high at the OE-purchase moment and collapses afterward.
  • CES has earned a 22-to-27% segment margin every year on the recast basis, and the level has *risen* every year: 22.1% (FY2022) → 23.7% (FY2023) → 26.2% (FY2024) → 26.6% (FY2025).
  • CES standalone margin widened +450 bps over three years. The CES-versus-Pratt gap is structurally large and stable in the high-teens; it was flattered in 2023-24 by Pratt's crisis (Pratt was negative in FY2023) and is now settling at a still-vast ~19 points as Pratt heals — narrowing from a crisis-distorted peak, not being competed away.
  • The near-term caution: CES segment margin inflected *down* to 26.4% in Q1 2026, −230 bps year-on-year, on the recast basis. The driver is mechanical, not competitive — install-engine (LEAP + GE9X) OE growth has negative incremental margin and outpaced high-margin spare-engine and services growth, so the mix diluted the blend even though *services margins were up year-on-year in the quarter*.
competitive-landscape
  • Large commercial jet propulsion is a global oligopoly. Three Western OEMs — GE/CFM, Pratt & Whitney (RTX), and Rolls-Royce — plus engine alliances supply essentially the entire installed base, with no fourth entrant in 50+ years.
  • The decisive structural fact is single-source aftermarket capture: whoever wins the engine at OE selection owns the certified parts-and-overhaul stream for that engine for 20-30 years. The profit pool sits overwhelmingly in services (~75% of CES revenue, the high-margin part), not the OE sale.
  • The engine-MRO market is ~$47B in 2025, growing ~4.7%/yr to ~$71B by 2034 [Fortune Business Insights, accessed 2026-06-23] [web]; engine is the largest slice (~46%) of the ~$112B total commercial-aircraft MRO market [Mordor, accessed 2026-06-23] [web].
  • GE's CES services revenue alone ($25B) is roughly half the engine-MRO figure, though the two are not apples-to-apples (CES services includes parts sold to third-party MRO and licensing). The directional conclusion is robust: GE is the largest single participant in the engine aftermarket; a clean audited share is not derivable from disclosure.
  • On the 737 MAX, GE wins by sole-source structure (100%), not by beating a rival. On the A320neo, LEAP holds ~55-75% of selections — a wide band whose high end coincides with peak GTF groundings.
  • On the widebody, GE is the in-service share leader (~52%) and gaining on the contested 787, taking >3/4 of recent selections versus Rolls (Bernstein, 2026-05-27), and is sole-source on the new 777X with >1,000 GE9X on order.
  • The GTF crisis inflates GE's current narrowbody share — and is reversing. At end-October 2025, ~835 GTF aircraft were grounded; by early 2026 RTX reported groundings declining ~15% and MRO output +23% [FlightGlobal, accessed 2026-06-23] [web]. A meaningful slice of GE's A320neo selection lead is cyclical, not structural. This is the most important caveat on any "LEAP is winning narrowbody" framing.
  • **The A320neo is dual-source by Airbus design.** Airbus maintains two engine suppliers to preserve its own buyer power and has been public about pushing work toward whichever maker gives ground (Bernstein, 2026-05-27). This caps how high LEAP's A320neo share can structurally go for reasons unrelated to GE's competitiveness.
  • **Independent MRO captures roughly half the engine-services labor pool.** Independent third-party shops held ~48.8% of MRO revenue in 2025 versus ~43.2% OEM-captive [Mordor, accessed 2026-06-23](https://www.mordorintelligence.com) [web]. GE manages this with "open MRO" licensing (Delta TechOps, Iberia premier MROs), keeping the high-margin parts even when a third party does the labor — so GE does not capture 100% of the services dollar.
  • **PMA parts attack the highest-margin slice.** HEICO sells FAA-approved PMA parts at 15-40% below OEM list (the dossier's earlier "30-50%" figure is at the high end of what accessible sources support); HEICO's Flight Support Group posted FY2025 net sales of $3,117M, +18% [HEICO, accessed 2026-06-23](https://www.heico.com) [web], with organic growth nearer ~14% than the "+22% organic" some sources cite. PMA penetration of life-limited hot-section parts is still limited, so this is a margin nibble that scales with GE's own installed base, not a structural break.
  • Independent MRO holds ~49% of MRO revenue versus ~43% OEM-captive, and HEICO's PMA parts (15-40% cheaper, $3.1B FSG revenue, +18%) substitute for GE's highest-margin spares. GE keeps the certified parts even where third parties do the labor, but it does not capture 100% of the services dollar, and the PMA threat scales with GE's installed base.
expectations
  • Management's standalone-era guidance record is a serial, large-magnitude beat-and-raise: GE beat the high end of its own full-year guide on operating profit, adjusted EPS, and free cash flow in both completed standalone years, each time after multiple in-year raises (FY2025 was raised three times and still cleared the final high end on all three).
  • The spin-era "~$10B operating profit by ~2028" target is being hit in FY2026, two years early.
  • The street's burden has flipped. "Consensus is too low" was true in 2023-2024, when Aerospace-basis estimates tripled; by mid-2026 consensus FY2028 adjusted EPS ($9.86) runs ~17% above management's own framework ($8.40), so the beat is now in the number.
financial-history
  • Deliveries *declined* in 2024 (1,911 from 2,075; LEAP 1,407 from 1,570) "due to supply chain constraints" while the aftermarket kept growing, the clearest demonstration that the binding constraint is supply, not demand, then re-accelerated +25% in 2025 and +50% in Q1 2026 (LEAP +63%).
  • DPT services mix is *declining* (56% → 51%) as defense equipment grows faster than aftermarket, the opposite of the CES story, even as DPT orders inflect (book-to-bill >2.0 for two straight quarters).
forward-model
  • The model is overwhelmingly a CES-aftermarket story. The two CES aftermarket drivers — services growth and segment margin — together move terminal-year operating profit by ~±$2.7B for plausible moves, more than every other driver combined, because ~75%-services CES is ~73% of revenue at a ~27% margin.
history-inflections
  • The durable constant across every abandoned strategy (digital industrial, industrial-finance, the conglomerate itself) is the engine business, which held ~21% margins even while the rest of GE collapsed.
risks
  • **LEAP durability / warranty drag during the ramp (high).** New-engine programs lose money on OE and hold warranty and durability reserves until the fleet matures; LEAP aftermarket margin is not yet at CFM56-class levels. Q1 2026 disclosed a $1.0B increase in sales discounts and allowances, "primarily due to increases in allowances on new engine installs and spare parts, as well as product durability reserves at CES" (10-Q Note 14) — the first time durability reserves surface as a sized, named driver.
secular-trends
  • GE's growth is annuity-driven, not market-volume-driven, which makes it more durable in *mechanism* than a normal industrial cyclical but means the *rate* will fade from its recovery-inflated peak.
  • The demand stack has three layers with different durabilities: the CES aftermarket (~55% of total GE revenue), driven by flight hours on the installed base, lagged and durable; CES OE (~18%), driven by airframer build rates, supply-gated today; and DPT (~23%), driven by government defense budgets, counter-cyclical.
  • A ~14-19% company growth on a ~3% market means the growth is *not* market-volume share gain — GE's commercial volume share is already near structural maximum (sole-source 737 MAX, ~75% of narrowbody and ~55% of widebody cycles).
  • The ~11-16 points above market volume decompose as installed-base annuity maturation, price and workscope, a non-repeating COVID-recovery tailwind, and a supply-catch-up burn-down. Of GE's ~14% total-revenue CAGR, roughly 3 points is structural air-travel volume; the rest is annuity timing, price/workscope, and a fading recovery tailwind.
  • The dominant one is the mechanical maturation of a young, still-growing installed base into its shop-visit window: the LEAP fleet roughly triples 2024-2030 with shop visits at a ~25% CAGR, while CFM56 stays "stronger for longer" (shop visits stable at 2,300-2,400 through 2028).
  • A genuinely new, AI-linked optionality is emerging: data-center power demand is driving interest in repurposing CFM56 cores for power generation, which could lengthen the CFM56 aftermarket tail ("sold out through 2030" on aeroderivatives; Q1 2026 call) — small today, undimensioned, and moving CES->DPT in the 2026 recast.
  • The cleanest swing variable to watch is the CFM56 retirement rate, where management's plan bakes in 2% (2026) rising to 3-4% (2027) against sub-1% actual — a conservative gap that becomes a positive surprise if actuals keep undershooting and a shortened tail if 2027 suddenly hits the assumption.
technology
  • GE Aerospace's technical edge is not one breakthrough. It is a compounding loop of field data, durability engineering, and repair IP across the industry's largest engine fleet, which lets GE push time-on-wing and lower cost-of-ownership faster than rivals and convert that into a multi-decade aftermarket annuity. More engines in service means more field data on where parts wear, which means faster durability fixes and repair development, which means higher time-on-wing and stickier aftermarket, which means more OE wins. The largest fleet learns fastest.
  • It is the future installed base, expected to roughly triple between 2024 and 2030 and to overtake CFM56 as the industry's largest fleet (10-K FY2025). The technical heart is a sequence of hot-section durability fixes — improved coating (2018), bearing update (2019), and the FAR-33-certified improved-design HPT blade (Dec 2023) — that improve LEAP-1A time-on-wing more than 2x to CFM56-class levels, now in all new deliveries with ~1,500 kits shipped (Q4 2025 call).
  • The clearest external proof that this engineering is hard is negative: Pratt's competing GTF hit a powder-metal contamination defect that grounded roughly 30-38% of its A320neo fleet (about 720-835 aircraft) into 2027-2028, with overhauls stretching from 60-90 days to 250-300 days, while LEAP, which had its own early durability problems, engineered through without a fleet-grounding event despite comparable hot-section physics.
  • GEnx is the growth widebody: ~99% win rate of firm 787 orders in 2023, ~4x output ramp 2023-2026, with ~75% of the fleet yet to see a first performance-restoration shop visit [deck: 2024-03-07, slide 38].
  • GE9X is sole-source on the Boeing 777X — the strongest form of OE lock-in, because the airline cannot choose another engine — but it is the clearest near-term roadmap risk. GE9X is loss-making during ramp (~$200M in 2025, guided to roughly double in 2026, peak ~2028), the 777X has slipped from a 2020 entry-into-service toward ~2027, and GE flagged a mid-seal durability issue in January 2026 on an engine certified in September 2020.
  • RISE is CFM's next-generation program — open-fan architecture, compact core, hybrid-electric — targeting >20% better fuel burn than today's LEAP, with entry-into-service in the latter part of the next decade. The physics argument is that the next efficiency step comes from the fan rather than from running the core hotter, which lowers durability risk [deck: 2024-03-07, slide 51]. The counter-evidence is airframer hesitation: Airbus has publicly called for an open-rotor alternative and Boeing has voiced doubt that open-fan can hit the 20% target.
value-chain
  • The aviation propulsion dollar is captured overwhelmingly at the engine-OEM layer, and inside that layer overwhelmingly in the aftermarket. The highest unit margins in the whole chain sit at two ends that own scarce certified IP: the upstream forging layer and the OEM aftermarket. Value is thin in the middle (the OE sale) and at the most cyclical, capital-heavy end (the airline).
  • **The binding constraint sits two links upstream of GE, not at GE.** GE operates in a "supply-constrained environment" (10-K FY2025); demand is not the gate -- "Boeing will take every engine that we can possibly deliver" (Bernstein, 2026-05-27). The scarce inputs are forgings and skilled MRO labor at the sole/limited-source forging sub-tier. There the power is with the supplier: Howmet earns a 33.3% segment EBITDA margin (above GE's blended company margin) while GE embeds its own engineers in supplier plants and "writes checks for CapEx" to unlock flow rather than dictating price.
  • Spare-parts delinquency rose ~70% since end-2024 even as spare-parts revenue grew >25% -- the clearest single number that output, not demand, is the gate.
  • **The chain finances itself, and it is GE-favorable.** Under LSAs, airlines pay usage-based fees and progress collections ahead of overhauls; the LSA net liability is ~$7.2B and ~85% of lifetime billings are still ahead. RRSP partners share those customer progress payments, distributing the financing risk across the chain rather than warehousing it at GE.
  • CFM is a non-consolidated 50/50 JV; Safran independently earns a 23.0% propulsion margin on its half, and GE's CES equity-method income from CFM was only $376M in FY2025. Headline CES revenue understates the commercial business, but there is no hidden 100%-of-CFM pool -- half is Safran's.

There the power is with the supplier: Howmet earns a 33.3% segment EBITDA margin (above GE's blended company margin) while GE embeds its own engineers in supplier plants and "writes checks for CapEx" to unlock flow rather than dictating price.
