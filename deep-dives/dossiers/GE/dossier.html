1. Business description, model & unit economics

GE Aerospace designs, manufactures, and services jet engines. After shedding HealthCare and Vernova it is a pure-play in one of the most concentrated industrial markets that exists. It reports three segments, but the economics reduce to one: a commercial-engine installed base that throws off a multi-decade service annuity.

The model is a razor and a blade. CES sells a commercial engine at or below cost (LEAP and GE9X both run at OE losses today), then earns its return over the next 20-to-30 years selling that engine its certified spare parts and shop visits. Management's own lifecycle arithmetic puts the original sale at only ~25% of an engine's lifecycle revenue and the three subsequent shop visits at ~75%, so each fielded engine throws off roughly 3.5x its new-engine revenue in services over its life [deck: 2024-03-07 GE Aerospace Investor Day, slide 28].❝ At the segment level that shows up as services equal to 75% of CES revenue and a 26.6% FY2025 CES margin (10-K FY2025, MD&A Segment Operations), a margin no equipment manufacturer earns.

What drives volume: the installed engine, not the annual delivery, is the unit that compounds. GE's fielded base is ~50,000 commercial and ~30,000 military engines, more than 2.3 billion flight hours (10-K FY2025, Item 1).❝ Two stacked flows drive CES: OE deliveries seed the future annuity (2,386 commercial engines in FY2025, of which 1,802 LEAP; LEAP guided +15% in FY2026), and aftermarket flow runs off the existing fleet. Aftermarket splits roughly 60% internal shop visits and 40% spare parts (Q1 2026 call), so of FY2025 CES services revenue of $25,010M, approximately $15.0B is shop visits and $10.0B is spare parts [estimate: 60/40 split applied to disclosed services revenue].❝ The annuity has barely begun to pay out on the newest fleet: roughly one-third of CFM56 engines have not had a first shop visit and two-thirds have not had a second (Q1 2026 call), and ~85% of long-term service-agreement lifetime billings are still ahead (2024 Investor Day).❝

What drives price: the OE engine is sold competitively, frequently below cost on new platforms, and GE pays airframe allowances to airlines recorded as a reduction of revenue (10-K FY2025). The realized price lever sits in the aftermarket, where GE is the certified source. CES raised price in FY2025; "improved pricing" is a named profit driver (10-K FY2025, MD&A). Contracts run 10-to-25-year long-term service agreements (LSAs) recognized on percentage-of-completion, generally priced on per-hour usage, with total estimated life-of-contract LSA billings of ~$174B (10-K FY2025). The threat to aftermarket price is third-party PMA parts (HEICO) and independent MRO, which GE manages through an "open MRO" approach that licenses repair technology rather than fighting it.

What drives cost: a large slice of CES revenue passes straight through to risk-and-revenue-sharing partners (RRSPs) and the CFM JV structure, a feature most industrials lack. Net payments to collaboration participants were $5,887M in FY2025 (cost of services $4,941M + cost of equipment $946M), roughly 27% of CES cost of revenue and ~18% of CES revenue (10-K FY2025, Note on collaborative arrangements).❝ On top of that sit titanium and nickel-superalloy forgings and castings, CMC inputs, and labor. The aftermarket is margin-rich because a repaired part can cost ~50% less than a new part (Q1 2026 call) and the work runs on the customer's asset, not GE's plant.❝

The demand-driver tree — GE is not levered to "commercial-aerospace recovery" but to one ultimate exposure: the installed-base services annuity — flight departures × ~50,000 fielded commercial engines × shop-visit rate × spare-parts $ (§1, §6). The crux is that this annuity is ~55% of revenue and ~87% of segment profit, yet most of its structural driver, the LEAP shop-visit wave, is still ahead of the realized numbers — recent growth is the departures recovery and pricing on the mature CFM56/widebody fleet, not the young fleet maturing.

Unit economics — DPT (a different, lower-return model)

DPT runs government and OEM contracting, roughly half services, at less than half CES's margin.❝ The single most common misframing is "cost-plus defense contractor": the disclosed defense mix is ~50% fixed-price, ~40% commercial, ~10% cost-plus (2024 Investor Day, slide 64). Fixed-price means GE bears cost-overrun risk on the largest slice and keeps its own productivity gains.❝ That asymmetry is visible in the history: DPT margin declined from 12.2% (FY2022) to 10.1% (FY2023) as inflation hit fixed-price work, then recovered to 12.3% (FY2025) as contracts repriced and FLIGHT DECK productivity landed.

Corporate & Other is not a business

The "$1,987M revenue / 4.3% of total" headline is an accounting residual: $3,533M of run-off insurance investment income minus $1,546M of intercompany eliminations, neither of which is going-concern aerospace revenue.❝ The bucket also holds non-operating noise that swamps the underlying ~$1.1B of recurring corporate cost: GAAP Corporate & Other operating profit swung from −$1,876M (FY2022) to +$3,943M (FY2023) to −$96M (FY2025), a ~$5.8B range, almost entirely on the $5,776M FY2023 mark on retained spin stakes (10-K FY2025, MD&A Corporate & Other). No "unit economics" table can be built for this segment because it has no product, no unit, no price, and no cost of revenue; the substitute is the run-off insurance block's spread economics (Section 12).

2. Key technologies & technical differentiators

GE Aerospace's technical edge is not one breakthrough. It is a compounding loop of field data, durability engineering, and repair IP across the industry's largest engine fleet, which lets GE push time-on-wing and lower cost-of-ownership faster than rivals and convert that into a multi-decade aftermarket annuity. More engines in service means more field data on where parts wear, which means faster durability fixes and repair development, which means higher time-on-wing and stickier aftermarket, which means more OE wins. The largest fleet learns fastest.❝ GE states plainly that the business is "not materially dependent on the preservation of any singular intellectual property right or patent license" (10-K FY2025, Item 1): the protection is certification, scale, and know-how, not a patent wall.

LEAP and the durability roadmap (the technology the forward case most depends on). LEAP is the CFM narrowbody engine, in service since 2016, sole-source on the Boeing 737 MAX and sharing the A320neo with Pratt's GTF. It delivers ~15% better fuel burn than the CFM56 it replaces FlightGlobal, accessed 2026-06-23 [web]. It is the future installed base, expected to roughly triple between 2024 and 2030 and to overtake CFM56 as the industry's largest fleet (10-K FY2025). The technical heart is a sequence of hot-section durability fixes — improved coating (2018), bearing update (2019), and the FAR-33-certified improved-design HPT blade (Dec 2023) — that improve LEAP-1A time-on-wing more than 2x to CFM56-class levels, now in all new deliveries with ~1,500 kits shipped (Q4 2025 call).❝ The clearest external proof that this engineering is hard is negative: Pratt's competing GTF hit a powder-metal contamination defect that grounded roughly 30-38% of its A320neo fleet (about 720-835 aircraft) into 2027-2028, with overhauls stretching from 60-90 days to 250-300 days AviationWeek/FlightGlobal, accessed 2026-06-23 [web], while LEAP, which had its own early durability problems, engineered through without a fleet-grounding event despite comparable hot-section physics.❝

CFM56 and proprietary repairs (the cash annuity). CFM56 is the mature narrowbody engine that funds today's services profit while LEAP ramps. The commercial lever is GE's repair catalog: a repaired part can cost ~50% less than a new one, so developing more proprietary repairs lowers customer cost-of-ownership and captures aftermarket margin. GE expects to double the number of LEAP repairs it develops in 2026 (Q1 2026 call). The annuity is protected by a slow retirement rate — CFM56 retirements ran sub-1% in Q1 2026 versus the ~2% GE assumed for 2026 — so the cash cow runs longer than modeled.

Widebody (GEnx, GE9X, GE90, CF6). GEnx is the growth widebody: ~99% win rate of firm 787 orders in 2023, ~4x output ramp 2023-2026, with ~75% of the fleet yet to see a first performance-restoration shop visit [deck: 2024-03-07, slide 38].❝ GE9X is sole-source on the Boeing 777X — the strongest form of OE lock-in, because the airline cannot choose another engine — but it is the clearest near-term roadmap risk. GE9X is loss-making during ramp (~$200M in 2025, guided to roughly double in 2026, peak ~2028), the 777X has slipped from a 2020 entry-into-service toward ~2027, and GE flagged a mid-seal durability issue in January 2026 on an engine certified in September 2020.❝ So a meaningful slice of the forward CES margin story is gated on un-finished GE9X cost-down and Boeing certification, neither fully in GE's control.

RISE / Open Fan (the highest roadmap exposure). RISE is CFM's next-generation program — open-fan architecture, compact core, hybrid-electric — targeting >20% better fuel burn than today's LEAP, with entry-into-service in the latter part of the next decade. The physics argument is that the next efficiency step comes from the fan rather than from running the core hotter, which lowers durability risk [deck: 2024-03-07, slide 51]. The counter-evidence is airframer hesitation: Airbus has publicly called for an open-rotor alternative and Boeing has voiced doubt that open-fan can hit the 20% target FlightGlobal, accessed 2026-06-23 [web].❝ RISE underpins the post-2030 narrowbody business but contributes nothing to FY2026-FY2028 financials, and GE's record on clean-sheet next-gen programs is mixed: LEAP shipped on time, GE9X/777X slipped ~7 years.

FLIGHT DECK (operating model, not a technology). FLIGHT DECK is GE's proprietary lean operating model, the Danaher Business System lineage Culp imported. Management attributes real results to it — McAllen, Texas cut LEAP HPT repair time more than 50%; a Singapore nozzle line cut turnaround ~20%; an AI material assistant predicts LEAP shop-visit workscopes nine months ahead (Q1 2026 call). It is GE's genuine operational lever on the supply-constrained ramp, but it is a management system competitors can imitate, not a protected technology.

GE's durable advantage is a data-and-durability flywheel on the existing fleet plus an operating model, not proprietary breakthrough technology. The thing GE markets hardest as breakthrough, RISE/Open Fan, is the least proven, and the additive-manufacturing "technology" business was impaired $251M in FY2024 (10-K FY2024). The moat is real but it is scale, data, and certification — more durable for the existing fleet, less so at the next-generation frontier.

3. Competitive advantages & moat assessment

Top-of-section caveat. The standalone returns and margin trends below sit on a four-year Aerospace-only window (FY2022–FY2025), a post-COVID recovery period that cannot distinguish cyclical-peak margins from durable ones. A clean multi-cycle series on today's GE does not exist (the pre-2024 conglomerate is non-comparable). Treat the level as well-evidenced and the durability of the recovery-window expansion as the open question. The single undisclosed number that governs the post-2028 margin path is the LEAP aftermarket gross margin, which GE describes only qualitatively ("approaching CES service-margin levels by ~2028"); it is unverifiable from disclosure.

CES holds a genuine, durable competitive advantage that sits close to the strongest end of the spectrum — a structural moat, not merely a contestable edge — but it is durable specifically over the installed fleet, and the one place it is contestable is the next-generation narrowbody platform that does not yet exist.❝ The advantage rests on three reinforcing structural facts. First, a sole-source aftermarket created at the point of OE sale: once an A320neo or 737 MAX flies a CFM engine, GE is the certified source for that engine's hot-section parts and overhaul for the asset's life, and the airline cannot re-tender it.❝ Second, the largest installed base and field-data pool in the industry, feeding a durability-and-repair flywheel that lowers customer cost-of-ownership faster than any rival can match. Third, regulatory certification (FAA/EASA type certificates, repair-station approvals, per-repair PMA approvals) that converts the data lead into a legally protected service position.

The margin gap is the single best moat evidence: the CES segment earned a 26.6% profit margin in FY2025 against 7.9% at RTX's Pratt & Whitney — the only true head-to-head engine segment in the comp set — an 18.7-point gap on a clean apples-to-apples basis.❝ The gap is not an accounting illusion: Safran, the independently-reporting other half of CFM, earned a 23.0% propulsion margin in FY2025 on the same platform safran-group.com FY2025 results, accessed 2026-06-23 [web], confirming the platform, not GE's ledger, generates the rent.❝

Management claims: the installed base is the engine of the business (~80,000 engines, ~70% services revenue); the razor-and-blade lifecycle (~3.5x services per fielded engine); the data-and-durability flywheel; sole-source positions (LEAP-1B on 737 MAX, GE9X on 777X); FLIGHT DECK and repair IP. GE does not claim a patent moat.❝

External sources: the competitor's failure is the loudest signal — Pratt's GTF crisis grounded ~38% of the A320neo fleet at peak while LEAP kept flying. Safran's 23.0% propulsion margin corroborates the annuity independently. The one GE sell-side note in the local digest (Jefferies, 2026-04-26, Buy) is titled "In Any Hand That Is Dealt, GE Holds All the Cards." Where external sources cut against management's claims is the next generation: Airbus wants an open-rotor alternative to RISE and Boeing doubts the 20% target [web].

Ground-up view: a clean-sheet large commercial engine costs billions and a decade-plus to develop and certify, and is sold at or below cost; no new entrant has launched a Western large commercial engine in decades.❝ Switching costs are absolute at the engine level by physics and regulation — every spare part and overhaul must be certified for that engine type, so the customer cannot switch mid-life without re-engining (uneconomic) or qualifying a PMA alternative (slow, non-critical parts only). Buyer power is high at the OE-purchase moment and collapses afterward.❝ The economic rent therefore sits in the aftermarket, captured by whoever fielded the engine — and GE/CFM fielded the most engines with the best durability record. The structure predicts what the financials show: durable mid-20s margins, widening with the fleet, defended without proportionate reinvestment.

Moat tested three ways

Standalone. CES has earned a 22-to-27% segment margin every year on the recast basis, and the level has risen every year: 22.1% (FY2022) → 23.7% (FY2023) → 26.2% (FY2024) → 26.6% (FY2025).❝ GE does not allocate invested capital by segment, so a CES-only ROIC is not computable; the consolidated GE Aerospace ROIC was 17.1% (Bloomberg, FY2025) — and since CES is ~73% of revenue and ~87% of segment profit, the consolidated figure is overwhelmingly a CES number diluted by lower-return DPT and the insurance drag. Against any reasonable ~8-10% industrial cost of capital that is durable excess return. The model is structurally cash-generative because LSA progress collections fund working capital: FY2025 FCF was $7,694M on $8,601M continuing net income, with capex only ~2.8% of revenue. Three-year incremental margins (Δ segment profit / Δ revenue) ran 32.4%, above the segment-average margin — positive operating leverage even while the negative-margin LEAP/GE9X OE ramp suppresses the blended margin near-term.

ROIC definition. ROIC = NOPAT / (total debt + total equity − cash), NOPAT = operating profit × (1 − effective tax rate). On the operating-capital definition GE Aerospace earns ~29-30% (FY2025); Bloomberg's broader total-capital denominator gives 17.1%. The gap is the $39B run-off insurance investment portfolio matched against an equal liability; a total-assets denominator (~$130B) destroys the number to ~6% and is meaningless. The truth is between the operating-capital and total-capital figures and is comfortably above the cost of capital on any definition.

Against the three reference points, this is materially closer to a structural advantage that throws off excess returns without heavy reinvestment to defend the existing fleet (the Visa/Mastercard end) than to a real-but-contestable edge — with two qualifications that keep it from the very top. First, the moat is asset-specific: it protects the CFM56/LEAP/GEnx/GE9X fleets GE already fielded, and a future single-aisle platform must be won again on fuel burn. Second, GE shares half the CFM economics with Safran and does not control the next-gen RISE decision alone.

Large commercial jet propulsion is a global oligopoly. Three Western OEMs — GE/CFM, Pratt & Whitney (RTX), and Rolls-Royce — plus engine alliances supply essentially the entire installed base, with no fourth entrant in 50+ years.❝ The market splits into two structurally different sub-markets.

Four pieces of share counter-evidence matter, ranked by how much they reshape the structure.

  • The GTF crisis inflates GE's current narrowbody share — and is reversing. At end-October 2025, ~835 GTF aircraft were grounded; by early 2026 RTX reported groundings declining ~15% and MRO output +23% FlightGlobal, accessed 2026-06-23 [web]. A meaningful slice of GE's A320neo selection lead is cyclical, not structural. This is the most important caveat on any "LEAP is winning narrowbody" framing.❝
  • The A320neo is dual-source by Airbus design. Airbus maintains two engine suppliers to preserve its own buyer power and has been public about pushing work toward whichever maker gives ground (Bernstein, 2026-05-27). This caps how high LEAP's A320neo share can structurally go for reasons unrelated to GE's competitiveness.❝ The cited source supports an Airbus push for more from CFM and a shift from arm-wrestling to problem-solving; it does not establish a precise ~50-55% long-run equilibrium, which is an inference, not a documented number.
  • Independent MRO captures roughly half the engine-services labor pool. Independent third-party shops held ~48.8% of MRO revenue in 2025 versus ~43.2% OEM-captive Mordor, accessed 2026-06-23 [web]. GE manages this with "open MRO" licensing (Delta TechOps, Iberia premier MROs), keeping the high-margin parts even when a third party does the labor — so GE does not capture 100% of the services dollar.❝
  • PMA parts attack the highest-margin slice. HEICO sells FAA-approved PMA parts at 15-40% below OEM list (the dossier's earlier "30-50%" figure is at the high end of what accessible sources support); HEICO's Flight Support Group posted FY2025 net sales of $3,117M, +18% HEICO, accessed 2026-06-23 [web], with organic growth nearer ~14% than the "+22% organic" some sources cite. PMA penetration of life-limited hot-section parts is still limited, so this is a margin nibble that scales with GE's own installed base, not a structural break.❝

5. Value chain & profit pools

The aviation propulsion dollar is captured overwhelmingly at the engine-OEM layer, and inside that layer overwhelmingly in the aftermarket. The highest unit margins in the whole chain sit at two ends that own scarce certified IP: the upstream forging layer and the OEM aftermarket. Value is thin in the middle (the OE sale) and at the most cyclical, capital-heavy end (the airline).❝

GE sits across the highest-value node (certified-parts aftermarket) but does not capture the upstream forging margin — it pays it to Howmet, which is why GE invests its own engineers and capital upstream rather than buying purely on price. A precise cents-on-the-dollar split is not derivable because GE discloses no OE ASP, CFM is non-consolidated 50/50, and supplier revenues span all OEMs; the directional concentration at the two ends is robust.

The binding constraint sits two links upstream of GE, not at GE. GE operates in a "supply-constrained environment" (10-K FY2025); demand is not the gate — "Boeing will take every engine that we can possibly deliver" (Bernstein, 2026-05-27). The scarce inputs are forgings and skilled MRO labor at the sole/limited-source forging sub-tier. There the power is with the supplier: Howmet earns a 33.3% segment EBITDA margin (above GE's blended company margin) while GE embeds its own engineers in supplier plants and "writes checks for CapEx" to unlock flow rather than dictating price.❝ Spare-parts delinquency rose ~70% since end-2024 even as spare-parts revenue grew >25% — the clearest single number that output, not demand, is the gate.❝

Bargaining power is shifting in opposite directions at the two interfaces. Against its downstream airframer/airline customers GE's power is rising (supply scarcity; "Boeing will take every engine"). Against its upstream forging suppliers GE's power is falling (same scarcity, other side). The one structural erosion risk is PMA and independent MRO on the aftermarket, which scales with GE's own installed base.

The chain finances itself, and it is GE-favorable. Under LSAs, airlines pay usage-based fees and progress collections ahead of overhauls; the LSA net liability is ~$7.2B and ~85% of lifetime billings are still ahead. RRSP partners share those customer progress payments, distributing the financing risk across the chain rather than warehousing it at GE.❝ GE's only meaningful financing outflow is the deliberate, finite ~$1B MRO + ~$1B U.S. manufacturing spend to relieve the upstream constraint. There is no equity-stakes-in-customers loop; the legacy GE Capital aircraft-financing apparatus is gone. This is the inverse of a fragile vendor-financed demand loop.

The customer purchase makes sense under conservative assumptions. A LEAP-1A leases for ~$145-155k/month and a mature CFM56-7B for ~$80-90k/month, with CFM56-7B half-life market values of $5.7-6.4M IBA/Ishka, accessed 2026-06-23 [web]. CFM56 residuals are holding because retirements run sub-1% versus a 2% planning assumption, so the airline's depreciation assumption is conservative. LEAP burns ~15% less fuel than CFM56, and fuel is the largest airline cost, so the upgrade pays for itself before residual value — which is why customers re-up: in Q1 2026 alone, United committed 300+ GEnx, American kept LEAP-1A on its A321neos, Delta took GEnx on 787-10s, and Ryanair signed an agreement covering its entire ~2,000-engine fleet (10-Q Q1 2026). A demand shock pushes shop visits out a few quarters rather than destroying them.

6. Secular & industry trends

GE's growth is annuity-driven, not market-volume-driven, which makes it more durable in mechanism than a normal industrial cyclical but means the rate will fade from its recovery-inflated peak.❝ The demand stack has three layers with different durabilities: the CES aftermarket (~55% of total GE revenue), driven by flight hours on the installed base, lagged and durable; CES OE (~18%), driven by airframer build rates, supply-gated today; and DPT (~23%), driven by government defense budgets, counter-cyclical.❝

Share math: company revenue grew ~14%/yr (CES ~18%/yr, CES services ~19%/yr) FY2023–FY2025 against an underlying air-travel market growing ~3%/yr (revenue passenger kilometers, IATA/Oxford Economics, $1.8T→$2.1T 2023-2028F [deck: 2024-03-07, slide 11]). A ~14-19% company growth on a ~3% market means the growth is not market-volume share gain — GE's commercial volume share is already near structural maximum (sole-source 737 MAX, ~75% of narrowbody and ~55% of widebody cycles).❝ The ~11-16 points above market volume decompose as installed-base annuity maturation, price and workscope, a non-repeating COVID-recovery tailwind, and a supply-catch-up burn-down. Of GE's ~14% total-revenue CAGR, roughly 3 points is structural air-travel volume; the rest is annuity timing, price/workscope, and a fading recovery tailwind.❝ The durable share story is "keep the near-maximum share we already have," not "gain more."

The recovery overshoot is ending. Departures decelerated from high-teens (2023) to high-single-digit (2024) to +3% (2025) to flat-to-low-single-digit guided (2026), normalizing toward the ~3% structural trend. So the double-digit reported growth is the annuity plus price plus supply catch-up, not a double-digit air-travel market.

The current short-cycle shock is muted by the backlog buffer. As of Q1 2026 a Gulf conflict and jet-fuel spike led GE to cut FY2026 departures to flat-to-low-single-digit, with a low-double-digit Middle East decline (Middle East ~5% of departures; Q1 2026 call). The dollars are an installed-base service annuity, so a traffic dip pushes shop visits out rather than destroying them — services lag traffic by several quarters then over-recover. The leading indicators went the right way during the shock: parked aircraft declined, spare-parts orders accelerated from 30% to 40%, removals stayed strong, delinquency kept rising (demand still exceeds supply). The Q2-Q3 removal pipeline ran ~1/3 oversubscribed versus the shop-visit guide, and ~2/3 of all 2026 shop-visit engines were already off-wing.

The durable drivers, ranked. The dominant one is the mechanical maturation of a young, still-growing installed base into its shop-visit window: the LEAP fleet roughly triples 2024-2030 with shop visits at a ~25% CAGR, while CFM56 stays "stronger for longer" (shop visits stable at 2,300-2,400 through 2028).❝ A genuinely new, AI-linked optionality is emerging: data-center power demand is driving interest in repurposing CFM56 cores for power generation, which could lengthen the CFM56 aftermarket tail ("sold out through 2030" on aeroderivatives; Q1 2026 call) — small today, undimensioned, and moving CES→DPT in the 2026 recast.❝

What would flash first if the secular story were breaking, ranked by lead time: parked-aircraft count and retirement rate (currently declining — intact); spare-parts orders and delinquency (accelerating — demand>supply); engine removals/off-wing pipeline (oversubscribed — strong); departures by region (soft on a regional fuel shock, not a global collapse); workscope and price per shop visit (holding); CFM56 shop-visit trajectory (stable); RPO direction (rising, +11% to $190.6B). All currently point the secular story intact, with only the short-cycle fuel/Middle East overlay flashing yellow. The cleanest swing variable to watch is the CFM56 retirement rate, where management's plan bakes in 2% (2026) rising to 3-4% (2027) against sub-1% actual — a conservative gap that becomes a positive surprise if actuals keep undershooting and a shortened tail if 2027 suddenly hits the assumption.❝

7. Critical points

  1. The model is a CES aftermarket annuity, and it is ~73% of revenue at a 26.6% margin. Roughly half of total GE revenue is commercial aftermarket. Each fielded engine throws off ~3.5x its OE value in services over 20+ years, and ~85% of LSA lifetime billings are still ahead. This is the entire investment case and the source of the moat; everything else is second-order.❝

  2. The 18.7-point margin gap over the only true engine peer is the single best moat evidence — and it is real, not accounting. CES 26.6% versus Pratt & Whitney 7.9% (FY2025), corroborated by Safran's independently-reported 23.0% propulsion margin on the same CFM platform. The gap is widening on a standalone basis and stable at ~19 points versus Pratt, normalizing down only from a crisis-flattered 2023-24 peak.

  3. Reported CES margin is suppressed, not compounding, until ~2028 — and the recent quarter shows it. CES margin declined −230 bps to 26.4% in Q1 2026 because the LEAP/GE9X OE ramp has negative incremental margin, even though services margins rose year-on-year. Management guides FY2026 margin "flattish." The back-end-loaded expansion depends on LEAP aftermarket margins reaching CES-service level (~2028) and GE9X losses peaking (~2028), neither yet shipped.❝

  4. The growth rate is recovery-inflated and must fade. GE's recast +16.3% three-year revenue CAGR sits above the entire 17-company reference class's 10-year maximum (+14.2%, HEICO); only HEICO and the payments duopoly sustained double-digit revenue for a decade. Roughly 3 of GE's ~14 points are durable air-travel volume; the rest is annuity timing, price, and a non-repeating recovery/supply-catch-up tailwind that exhausts ~2027-28.❝

  5. The binding constraint is supply, two links upstream of GE. GE can sell every engine; output is gated by sole/limited-source forgings and MRO labor. Spare-parts delinquency rose ~70% since end-2024 even as spare-parts revenue grew >25%. Whether that delinquency is stored revenue that converts or demand that evaporates is the cyclical-versus-secular swing, and it is shown only as an indexed chart, never in dollars.

  6. GE shares half the commercial business with Safran. CFM is a non-consolidated 50/50 JV. Headline CES revenue ($33.3B) understates the commercial business, but GE's equity-method pickup was only $376M in FY2025 — there is no large hidden pool, and half the platform rent is Safran's.❝

  7. The street has underwritten GE beating its own raised 2028 framework by mid-teens. Consensus FY2028 adjusted EPS is $9.86 versus management's own ~$8.40 target (+17%). The beat is in the number; estimates have risen +16.5% over 12 months. See Section 8 for why this matters.

  8. Capital return is running hotter than the stated framework, and the buyback is momentum-following. GE returned ~114% of adjusted FCF in 2024-2025 versus a stated 70-75% framework, funded by cash drawdown and a 2025 debt issuance. The disclosed average repurchase price rose every window as the stock tripled (~$170 → ~$250 → ~$313/share), landing on the market average each time — a size-following program, not an opportunistic one. See Section 8.

  9. The legacy LTC run-off insurance block is the one real tail outside the operating model. $23.8B GAAP / $29.8B gross statutory long-term-care reserves on an aging block (average age 80); statutory testing can force capital contributions that bypass the operating company. The 2018 funding program is complete and zero was required in 2025, but the largest single disclosed adverse sensitivity is ~$1.2B present value. See Sections 12 and 18.

8. Variant perceptions

Each point cuts against a stated consensus framing, is quantified, and states whether the evidence supports it as written or qualifies it.

The moat is technology / RISE leadership. The durable rent is in the in-service aftermarket (75% of CES revenue, 26.6% margin), protected by scale and certification, not the next-gen technology consensus fixates on. GE's own 10-K says the business is not materially dependent on any single patent, and the most-marketed "breakthrough," RISE/Open Fan, is the least proven — Airbus wants an alternative, Boeing doubts the 20% target, no airframer has committed, and entry-into-service is ~2035. The moat is structural/industrial, not a technology bet. Supported.

CES is a smoothly compounding ~27% annuity. The most recent quarter shows CES margin down 230 bps to 26.4% because the OE ramp has negative incremental margin; services margins rose year-on-year, so the entire gap is OE mix, a deliberate installed-base-seeding investment, not a competitive squeeze. The annuity is intact; the reported margin is suppressed until ~2028. Supported.

GE keeps beating, so the stock works. Beats have stopped moving the stock up: GE beat adjusted EPS every quarter from Q3 2024 to Q1 2026, several times double-digit, yet the three sharpest single-session drops of the window all followed beats (Q3 2024 −9.1%, Q4 2025 −7.4%, Q1 2026 −5.6%). The marginal price-setter reacts to the guidance delta, not the print. These are not the three largest-surprise prints, though: the single biggest surprise (Q4 2024, +26.9%) produced a positive +6.6% move, and beats with positive reactions also occurred (Q1 2025 +6.1%, Q3 2025 +1.3%). What holds is the narrower claim that the three sharpest drops followed beats and were set by the guidance delta. Holds in the narrower form.

Consensus is conservative because GE keeps beating. That was true in 2023-2024 (Aerospace-basis estimates tripled); by mid-2026 the burden flipped. Consensus FY2028 adjusted EPS ($9.86) and operating profit ($13.2B) already run ~17% and ~14% above management's own raised 2028 framework ($8.40 / $11.5B). The street is not lagging a sandbagged guide; it has underwritten GE beating its own three-year targets by mid-teens. Supported.

A 47x multiple reflects the quality. The re-rating is now as much multiple as earnings: forward P/E went from ~10x (2022) to ~47x (FY2026E), roughly 2.3x the S&P 500's 20.2x. The market has priced multiple years of continued double-digit EPS growth, and the multiple has never been stress-tested by a demand shock in the pure-play form — the only aviation demand shock in the series (2020) occurred when GE traded at ~11-18x, not 47x. This is descriptive, not a judgment: the multiple, not just the earnings, has to keep being validated. Supported (descriptive).

GE captures the full CFM/LEAP economics. CFM is a non-consolidated 50/50 JV; Safran independently earns a 23.0% propulsion margin on its half, and GE's CES equity-method income from CFM was only $376M in FY2025. Headline CES revenue understates the commercial business, but there is no hidden 100%-of-CFM pool — half is Safran's.❝ The moat is shared, which is a quality and a constraint at once. Supported.

GE owns its engine aftermarket as a monopoly. Independent MRO holds ~49% of MRO revenue versus ~43% OEM-captive, and HEICO's PMA parts (15-40% cheaper, $3.1B FSG revenue, +18%) substitute for GE's highest-margin spares. GE keeps the certified parts even where third parties do the labor, but it does not capture 100% of the services dollar, and the PMA threat scales with GE's installed base.❝ Accessible sources support a 15-40% PMA discount and ~14% HEICO organic growth, below the 30-50% and +22% figures sometimes cited. Overstated on the specifics; the structural point holds.

Q4 2025 was a beat the stock declined ~7% on a thin guide. The beat and thin (~1.8%) guide raise are confirmed (revenue $12.7B vs $11.18B estimate, adjusted EPS $1.57 vs $1.43). The price move depends on the window: the day-of move was ~−1.8% (~−3.5% premarket), while the next-session move was −7.4%. The ~7% figure is the next-session reaction, not the day-of move. Magnitude depends on the window; the beat-with-thin-raise mechanism holds.

The LTC transfer market makes GE's block newly sellable. A transfer market for long-term-care tail risk did emerge in 2024-2025 (Unum→Fortitude Re $3.4B closed Jul-2025; Manulife→RGA $1.9B closed Jan-2025), and LTC is now a marketable asset where it was considered un-cedable for a decade. There were two distinct deals, not three: a third often-cited "RGA $2B" block is the same Manulife/RGA transaction (LTC plus structured settlements), not an additional one. Overstated on the deal count; the "a transfer market emerged" thesis holds.

Four key inflections:

  • The conglomerate breaks (2017-2018). Net income went from +$3,667M (2016) to a −$22,809M loss to common (2018); the dividend went $0.24→$0.01; CFOA collapsed from $11.0B (2017) to $2.3B (2018). The episode revealed that GE's reported 2010s earnings power was substantially an accounting and financial-engineering artifact — aggressive LTC reserves, Power revenue-recognition pull-forwards, and GE Capital dividends propping up the parent. The behavioral lesson, visible in every subsequent transcript, is an aversion to leverage and to managing-to-the-number.

  • Culp arrives; survival and deleveraging (2018-2021). The first outsider CEO converted GE into an asset-sale deleveraging machine (BioPharma, GECAS), closed the SEC investigation, and moved to one-column reporting. The pattern for "what GE does under stress": shrink the balance sheet, sell non-core, impose lean (FLIGHT DECK), defer growth.

  • The decision to break up (2021-11-09). Chosen, from a position of relative strength versus the 2018 nadir, but it was the terminal admission that the conglomerate model GE pioneered had failed. Management frames this as a confident value-unlock; the omitted context is that the run-off LTC insurance book and Bank BPH stayed with Aerospace, so the pure-play is not as clean as the framing implies.

  • GE Aerospace emerges (2023-2026). The COVID trough on the core was severe — Aviation segment profit declined −82% to $1,229M in 2020, margin 21.1%→5.6% — so the crown jewel was not immune. The recovery produced a backlog-rich, supply-constrained pure-play: revenue ~$31.9B (FY2022) → $45,855M (FY2025), CES margin 26.6%, RPO $190.6B rising to ~$211B by Q1 2026. The binding constraint flipped from demand (2020) to supply (2025-26). The 2020 episode is the cautionary footnote: this is still a cyclical commercial-aviation business, and a demand shock can compress the core's margin from 21% to 6% in one year.

The durable constant across every abandoned strategy (digital industrial, industrial-finance, the conglomerate itself) is the engine business, which held ~21% margins even while the rest of GE collapsed.❝ The current "pure-play aviation, FLIGHT DECK, installed-base annuity" story is consistent and well-evidenced — but GE's history is a series of confident strategies later abandoned, and the CES services line is still percentage-of-completion on 10-25-year LSAs with heavy management estimates, the same accounting mechanism class that burned GE before.

10. Management, incentives & capital allocation

Leadership: Larry Culp is Chairman (since April 2024) and CEO (since June 2022) of GE Aerospace, and was Chairman & CEO of GE from October 2018. He ran Danaher 2001-2014, where revenue roughly quintupled, and the Danaher Business System is the template for FLIGHT DECK. He was an external hire brought in during a crisis. Under his tenure $100 invested 12/31/2020 grew to $586 by 12/31/2025 versus $189 for the S&P 500 Industrials Index (2026 DEF 14A). CFO Rahul Ghai (since August 2022) came from Otis and Harris. The bench is mostly external hires layered over a thin core of long-tenure GE engineers (Russell Stokes, who runs CES through Feb 2026; Mohamed Ali, his internal successor). The largest governance overhang is CEO succession: Culp's agreement runs through Dec 31, 2027 (extendable to 2028), he is 62 and retirement-eligible, and no named heir is disclosed; shareholders pressed the Board on succession in 2025.

Compensation: CEO target pay is $21.25M, heavily at-risk (salary <10%, equity ~72%). The annual bonus weights operating profit 40%, free cash flow 40%, and adjusted revenue growth 20%, with a ±10-point safety modifier. Long-term equity is ~50% PSUs / ~30% options / ~20% RSUs; the PSUs measure cumulative three-year adjusted EPS (50%) and free cash flow (50%), modified ±20% by three-year relative TSR versus the S&P 500 Industrials Index, capped at 175%. The plan pays out both ways — in 2022, when GE TSR (98) trailed peers (114), Culp's compensation-actually-paid went negative (−$23.8M) as equity marked down; the 2019 PSU grants were cancelled with zero payout. Pay moved down with the stock, not only up.

Three features describe the design. First, the file is dominated by two enormous off-cycle megagrants (the restructured 2020 Leadership Performance Shares and the 2024 CEO Incentive Grant, 310,289 target PSUs on four-year cumulative adjusted-EPS CAGR), so CEO compensation-actually-paid of $182M/$285M/$213M in 2023/2024/2025 rests overwhelmingly on those grants marking up with the stock, not on the $21.25M annual program. Second, the 2024 grant drove say-on-pay support down to 71% in 2025 from 94% in 2023 and 2024; the dissent was specifically about off-cycle grants, not program design, and the Board states it does not anticipate further off-cycle grants through 2027. Third, the metric set is operating profit, FCF, revenue growth, and adjusted EPS — only EPS is per-share, and there is no ROIC, ROIIC, or capital-efficiency gate, so an executive could be rewarded for growth without a returns-on-capital check; the rTSR modifier and the 10x-salary ownership requirement are the main per-share guardrails. All NEOs are in compliance with the ownership guidelines; Culp's stake (~1.6M shares, ~$497M at year-end 2025) is far above the 10x requirement. No hedging or pledging; double-trigger CIC only.

Candor is high and the language plain across the multi-year transcript record. The strategy throughline (FLIGHT DECK, decentralization, balance-sheet repair, "win today/tomorrow/future") sharpened rather than mutated as the portfolio simplified. The crisis-era record (2020-2022) is the more informative sample — Culp owned the COVID downturn directly and folded restructuring into adjusted results to drive accountability, moving toward more conservative reporting — and it holds up.

Structural risks, ranked

  1. Run-off insurance reserve true-up — the largest tail. $36,894M of insurance liabilities, including $23,837M GAAP / $29,843M gross statutory long-term-care reserves on an aging closed block (ERAC + UFLIC; 201,700 policies, 253,000 covered lives, average attained age 80, 77% with inflation protection). Annual statutory testing can force capital contributions that bypass the operating company; the largest single disclosed adverse sensitivity is ~$1,200M present value (5% fewer disabled-life deaths, or +5% utilization, or no morbidity improvement). GE prefunded ~$15B into these subsidiaries from 2018, the final $1.8B in Q1 2024, and zero was required in 2025 — so the 2018-vintage funding plan is complete, but the residual is an uncapped tail. A counterparty wrinkle: UFLIC trust assets back Genworth subsidiaries, and Genworth has stated it will not bolster its insurance subs.
  2. Pension discount-rate / re-funding — $5.4B net deficit, 91% funded, no near-term call; a sustained rate decline lifts the obligation and hits AOCI.
  3. Refinancing cost creep — laddered maturities refinance at 4.3-4.9% versus ~4.0% embedded; a modest interest-expense headwind, not a wall.
  4. Bank BPH (Poland) FX-mortgage litigation — $2,334M reserved (down from $2,461M), portfolio at zero net carrying value; adverse Polish/ECJ rulings could raise the estimate. In discontinued operations but the obligation sits with GE Aerospace.
  5. Distribution above FCF — GE returned ~114% of FCF in 2024-25, funded by cash drawdown and the 2025 issuance; not a solvency risk given the cash buffer and IG ratings, but the reason borrowings ticked up in 2025 after years of paydown.

The legacy financial-services liabilities are positively correlated to each other and to a falling-rate macro (a rate decline lifts both the pension deficit and the LTC reserve discount sensitivity), even as the operating business is rate-insensitive.

17. Forward model summary

This is an operating model only — revenue through FCF, no valuation, no multiples, no discounting. It is built bottom-up by segment as units × price × mix, tied to FY2025 actuals and to the recast-invariant consolidated lines (op profit*, EPS, FCF), so the 2026 segment-recast does not affect the company-level forecast. The horizon is FY2026E-FY2030E.

The model is overwhelmingly a CES-aftermarket story. The two CES aftermarket drivers — services growth and segment margin — together move terminal-year operating profit by ~±$2.7B for plausible moves, more than every other driver combined, because ~75%-services CES is ~73% of revenue at a ~27% margin.❝

18. Risks

Severity-ranked over a 1-3 year horizon; each with mechanism, quantified exposure, and the leading indicator that flashes first.

  1. Commercial-aviation cycle turns / aftermarket proves cyclical (highest). Roughly half of total GE revenue is commercial aftermarket at the segment's high margin. The bull thesis treats it as a durable annuity; it is not weather-proof — shop visits and spare-parts volume fall when airlines fly less, defer overhauls, or retire aircraft. Management's own Q1 2026 guide already builds in a 2H deceleration in spare-parts growth, lighter workscopes, and reduced billings, and cut FY2026 departures to flat-to-low-single-digit on a ~$105/bbl Brent assumption. Watch: global departures growth (Q1 2026 +1.7%, Middle East declining), spare-parts orders, jet-fuel price, airline credit events. The bad-quarter scenario is a departures roll-over with a fuel spike.

  2. CFM56 retirement-rate / parked-aircraft swing (high). The cash-cow aftermarket depends on the CFM56 fleet staying in service. Management assumed 2% retirements for 2026 and 3-4% for 2027; Q1 2026 actual was sub-1%. Today that is a tailwind; the risk is the reverse — a downturn that pushes 2027 retirements above the 3-4% assumption would cut the aftermarket base years before LEAP can backfill. Watch: retirement rate versus the 2-4% assumption; parked-aircraft count.

  3. LEAP durability / warranty drag during the ramp (high). New-engine programs lose money on OE and hold warranty and durability reserves until the fleet matures; LEAP aftermarket margin is not yet at CFM56-class levels. Q1 2026 disclosed a $1.0B increase in sales discounts and allowances, "primarily due to increases in allowances on new engine installs and spare parts, as well as product durability reserves at CES" (10-Q Note 14) — the first time durability reserves surface as a sized, named driver.❝ Watch: LEAP aftermarket margin disclosure; CES segment margin versus the 26.6% level; warranty/durability reserve balances. The reserve balance (versus the Q1 quarterly increase) is undisclosed and is the leading indicator that the "annuity compounds smoothly" framing is mispriced.

  4. GE9X / 777X sole-source program losses (high). GE9X losses ~$200M in 2025, guided to roughly double to ~$400M in 2026, peaking ~2028; a mid-seal durability crack was found in January 2026 on a flight-test engine, and sole-source means GE cannot diversify away from 777X program risk. The base case models GE9X loss reversal as a ~2028 margin tailwind but isolates no further-777X-slip driver delta in the bear, so a named high risk is absent from the sensitivities. Watch: the GE9X annual loss versus the ~$400M 2026 guide; 777X certification milestones (FAA signals early-2027); any new airworthiness directive.

  5. Supply-chain / spare-parts delinquency — the binding constraint (high). Demand exceeds GE's ability to source materials and turn shop visits; output, not orders, is the gate, and rare-earth/critical-mineral exposure (newly added to the FY2025 risk factors) compounds it. Spare-parts delinquency rose ~70% versus end-2024; the CEO conceded "it is a number we are not proud of … we're failing to meet customer expectations." The constraint is currently capping recognized revenue. Watch: the spare-parts delinquency chart (shown only indexed, never in dollars); on-time-delivery; shop-visit turnaround. Partly an upside skew (delinquency is unmet demand), but a real revenue-timing and customer-satisfaction risk, and whether it converts or evaporates is undimensioned.

  6. Tariffs / rare-earth & critical-mineral input access (medium-high). Tariffs, export controls (newly including rare-earth restrictions, FY2025 10-K), and China decoupling raise input cost and can choke component flow. Not separately sized by GE; the detailed enumeration of mitigation (pricing surcharges, duty drawbacks, foreign-trade zones, rerouting) signals the exposure is non-trivial. The rare-earth/critical-mineral channel is the hardest to mitigate — "no surcharge fixes an unavailable input" — and GE's quantified dependence (volume, sole-source share) is named but never sized.

  7. Long-term-care run-off insurance reserve top-ups (medium, legacy). Annual statutory testing can force capital contributions if assumptions deteriorate; the LTC future-policy-benefit reserve is $25,792M (a designated Critical Audit Matter), with the largest single adverse sensitivity ~$1,200M present value. The 2018 funding program is complete and zero was required in 2025, but it is an uncapped tail not embedded in the operating model, and a Genworth solvency event would stress the UFLIC trusts. Watch: the Q3 annual statutory cash-flow test; the interest-rate path (lower rates raise reserve PV); Genworth solvency.

  8. Legacy legal / Bank BPH FX-mortgage / asbestos (medium-low). Bank BPH borrower-litigation loss estimate $2,334M (down from $2,461M), portfolio at zero net value, no contribution required in 2025; the major shareholder suit settled in 2024. Declining, ring-fenced.

  9. Credit-rating / refinancing / pension (low, well-managed). Net debt/EBITDA ~0.7-0.8x, interest coverage ~14x, single-A ratings — the strongest the balance sheet has been since pre-2018. Standard-issue risk.

The dominant correlated cluster is commercial-cycle stress: a fuel-price spike (#1) → airlines defer overhauls and park CFM56s (#2) → LSA billings and spare-parts volume drop, just as LEAP durability reserves (#3) and GE9X losses (#4) step up, while delinquency (#5) flips from "unmet demand we'll recover" to "demand that evaporated" and tariff cost (#6) is hardest to pass through when volume falls. The legacy buckets (#7-#8) are largely uncorrelated with the aviation cycle, but a recession that lowers rates would simultaneously worsen the LTC reserve test.
