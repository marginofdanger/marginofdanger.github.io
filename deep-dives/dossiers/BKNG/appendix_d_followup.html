D1 (2026-07-02): Regional revenue & market share of BKNG / EXPE / ABNB across Europe-EMEA, the US, and Asia-Pacific

Question. Can you estimate in more detail the revenue contribution and market shares of BKNG/EXPE/ABNB across the main markets of Europe / EMEA, the US, and Asia? Be extensive in your search, and try to triangulate from different angles (e.g. total hotel bookings in each market, online penetration, known revenue splits, any historical disclosures in transcripts, etc.).

A single clean region × company matrix is not honestly constructible, and the reason is itself the first finding. The three companies report geography on three incompatible bases. BKNG reports by legal entity, where the Netherlands-domiciled ~80.6% of revenue is a brand proxy for Booking.com, not a Europe-demand figure (BKNG FY2025 10-K, Note 17). EXPE reports by booking origin ("geographic location of our websites or points of sale," US vs all-other; EXPE FY2025 10-K). ABNB reports by destination ("location of the host's listing"; ABNB FY2025 10-K). A US resident booking a Paris apartment is US for EXPE and EMEA for ABNB, so one trip lands in different regions for different companies. On top of that, ABNB's $91,273M of gross booking value is alternative-accommodation only, with no hotels and no flights, while EXPE ($119,590M) and BKNG ($186,107M) are hotel-inclusive. The method below fixes one common denominator, the online-accommodation gross-bookings pool by region on a 2025 basis, divides each company's regional accommodation bookings into it, and states the basis mismatch as a range rather than papering over it.

The denominators, and the inversion that drives the table. For total travel, North America is the largest market at ~$539B of gross bookings in 2024 (Phocuswright, via industry-macro datasource [web]). For accommodation, the only pool these three contest at scale, the ranking inverts to Europe first (Table 1). The mechanism: North America's lead is high-cost US domestic air, which OTAs barely intermediate, whereas lodging dollars concentrate in Europe. Europe earns ~$663B of the world's ~$1.6T international-tourism receipts (~42%, UN Tourism 2023 [web]), and roughly 1 billion EU nights are booked through the four big platforms (Eurostat [web]). Booking.com's European concentration is therefore a scale position in the largest accommodation pool, not a regional limitation.

Booking Holdings. BKNG discloses its FY2025 booker-region room-night mix directly and consistently: Europe about half, Asia about a quarter, US a low-double-digit percent, Rest of World the residual (~13–14%), of 1,235M room nights (Q4-2025 earnings call, Feb 2026). Converting room nights to accommodation dollars requires regional ADRs. Asia is structurally lower-ADR and the US highest, and Asia also has the heaviest discounting recognized as contra-revenue (BKNG FY2025 10-K), so the dollar mix tilts further toward Europe and the US than the night mix does. Estimated FY2025 accommodation gross bookings are ~$85B Europe, ~$30B Asia, ~$26B US/North America, ~$17B Rest of World, totaling ~$158B ($155–165B) of the $186,107M total; the ~$18–22B balance is flights (growing +29%) and cars. Agoda, the APAC pure-play, is only ~$2.4B of revenue (~9% of group) and roughly two-thirds of the ~$30B Asia book, the rest being Booking.com's own fast-growing Asia business.

Expedia. EXPE is the most US-concentrated of the three. US revenue was $8,710M, 59.1% of $14,733M, with all other countries 40.9% (EXPE FY2025 10-K, Geographic Information). That US share has declined every year (63.5% FY2023, 61.1% FY2024, 59.1% FY2025) as internationally-weighted B2B bookings (+18–20%/yr) outgrow US-heavy B2C (~+2%/yr). EXPE does not split EMEA from APAC, so the only hard cut is US vs non-US; the EMEA (~26–32%) and APAC (~5–9%) figures below are triangulated from trivago's $417M all-Germany floor, Similarweb consumer-domain country mix, and a B2B global offset, at low-to-medium confidence. Lodging is 79.8% of EXPE revenue, so it is lodging-led like BKNG, but its gross bookings include more air and packages, so accommodation is ~$74B [estimate] of the $119,590M total.

Airbnb. ABNB is the one company that discloses the full region × metric table, by host-listing location (ABNB FY2025 10-K): North America $40,295M GBV (44%), EMEA $34,162M (37%), Latin America $8,542M (10%), Asia-Pacific $8,274M (9%); revenue splits 42/39/10/9. By nights the largest region is EMEA (215M, 40%) not North America (158M, 30%); North America leads only on dollars because of higher ADR. Two facts constrain any cross-company table. LatAm is ~10% of ABNB and sits outside the three-region OTA frame, so dropping it silently loses $8.5B. And the entire book is alternative accommodation, so ABNB competes only in the short-term-rental slice of each regional pool, never against hotel bookings. The Q1-2026 mix (EMEA 27.9%) is check-in seasonality, not a run-rate; FY2025 is the anchor (ABNB Q1-2026 10-Q).

D2 (2026-07-02): Relative scale of BKNG / EXPE / other OTAs — feet on the street vs marketing budget, by geography

Question. Can you try to characterize/quantify the relative presence/scale of BKNG/EXPE/other OTAs in terms of feet on the street and marketing budget, across geographies? I am interested to see if there are meaningful scale differences in supply acquisition/demand generation, etc.

The question splits into two scale axes, and the answer differs on each. On supply acquisition (the people who sign and service hotels and hosts, plus inventory breadth) Booking Holdings is the Western leader, but the edge is a regional coverage game rather than an exclusive asset. On demand generation (marketing dollars and the share of traffic that arrives without paying Google) is where the economics are actually decided, and leadership there is split by region.

Feet on the street. Booking Holdings employed ~24,300 people at Dec 31 2025, of which only ~2,900 were US-based and ~21,400 (88%) sat outside the US (BKNG FY2025 10-K, Item 1). That ex-US skew is the clearest supply-proximity signal in the group: Booking.com's market managers sit physically near fragmented independent-hotel supply across Europe and Asia, run from its Amsterdam base. Expedia employs ~16,000 across ~50 countries with about half in technology roles and a Seattle headquarters (EXPE FY2025 10-K). Airbnb runs the leanest model at ~8,200 employees plus ~13,000 outsourced community-support workers (ABNB FY2025 10-K); its supply is self-serve, so >9M active listings and >5M hosts are onboarded with almost no market-manager org (more than 1,000 listings per employee). Trip.com has the largest raw headcount at ~43,574 (stockanalysis.com / 20-F, accessed 2026-07-02 [web]), but it is Shanghai-centric and dominated by customer service (~38%) and product development (~45%); sales and marketing was only ~10% on the FY2023 function split. Applied pro-rata that implies ~4,300 market-facing staff [estimate: FY2023 20-F mix on the FY2025 total], the same order of magnitude as Booking.com's own ~4,000-4,300 sales-and-marketing-function headcount [estimate], despite a ~19,000-person total-headcount gap. No company discloses a clean supply-team count, so both figures are upper bounds that bundle demand marketing with partner services.

Inventory breadth follows the same shape. Booking.com listed ~4.4M properties at Dec 31 2025 (~500k hotels plus ~3.9M alternative-accommodation properties, ~8.6M AA listings on a listing basis), rising to ~4.5M by Mar 31 2026 (BKNG FY2025 10-K Item 2; Q1-2026 10-Q). That is the broadest combined hotel-plus-homes footprint of any single platform. Expedia lists ~3.6M lodging properties (~2.4M via Vrbo, US-skewed); Trip.com ~1.7M, concentrated in Greater China [web].

By region, Booking.com is the #1 hotel-distribution channel across the UK, Spain, Portugal, Ireland and Germany (SiteMinder 2025; the Germany rank rests on older data), with ~47% of group staff in Europe at end-2023; its channel rank in France and Italy is not disclosed. Expedia moved to #1 in North America in the 2025 SiteMinder edition (first time since ~2020/21), Booking.com #2, with Airbnb the US alt-accommodation leader. In Southeast and East Asia, Agoda leads the Western brands (Singapore HQ, Bangkok operations hub, offices across Tokyo, Seoul, Kuala Lumpur, Taipei, Manila, Jakarta and Gurgaon; SiteMinder #1 in the Philippines and Taiwan, #2 in Thailand) but contests the region with Trip.com. Greater China is walled off to foreign OTAs by local participation rules the BKNG 10-K describes as potentially "costly, difficult, or impossible," leaving Trip.com dominant there.

Marketing budget. The demand-generation spend, as filed for FY2025:

On the total selling-and-marketing line BKNG and Expedia are a dead heat at the top; on pure demand generation BKNG's ~$8,186M leads, then Expedia's direct ~$7,349M, then Trip.com ~$2,100M, then Airbnb's advertising ~$843M (BKNG financial_history.csv; EXPE / ABNB FY2025 10-Ks; Trip.com FY2025 results release [web]). Intensity ranks differently. Expedia is by far the most marketing-intensive at 55.6% of revenue and 6.8% of gross bookings, though its "direct" line includes B2B partner commissions that overstate the pure-Google gap; the gross-bookings comparison of 6.1% direct versus BKNG's 4.4% is the fairer one. BKNG sits in the middle at 30.4% of revenue and 4.4% of gross bookings, down from 35.1% and 5.5% in FY2016 as its direct/app channel mix climbed into the mid-50s% of room nights. Airbnb is the least intensive by a wide margin: advertising was ~$843M in FY2025, about 0.9% of GBV and ~6.9% of revenue, and it declined from $1.1B in FY2024 (ABNB FY2025 10-K; the $953M sometimes cited is the FY2023 figure). Trip.com's RMB14.9B (~$2,100M, +25% YoY, 24% of net revenue) has no gross-bookings denominator to normalize against.

The traffic split shows where those dollars escape or feed Google (Similarweb, May 2026, desktop-panel directional). Airbnb is the most brand/direct-led: Direct is its #1 channel at 45.65%, with only 41.4% of its search traffic paid. Booking.com's flagship is also direct-led at the channel level (~46-56% across panels) but its search traffic is majority paid at 53.7%, and its own sub-brands are outright paid-search-led: Agoda (paid search the #1 channel) and Priceline (75.1% of search paid, the most Google-dependent site in the set). Expedia is paid-search-led too (55.4% of search paid). Among the majors only Trip.com breaks the pattern on the demand side, direct/referral-led at 26.5% direct, so paid-search dependence is really a trait of the Western full-service brands (Agoda, Expedia, Priceline) rather than a universal one. Booking.com also owns the only globally diversified demand book: the US is just 11.3% of its visits behind a long European tail, whereas Expedia (91% US), Priceline (91% US) and Airbnb (77% US) are US-concentrated and Agoda is APAC-anchored.

Which axis leads where. Supply is multi-homed. SiteMinder's per-market top-12 lists Booking.com, Expedia, Agoda and Trip.com on the same hotels at once, so breadth is a requirement to compete, not an exclusive moat. The differentiated advantage is demand-side direct/brand traffic that avoids the Google toll now taxing ~30% of BKNG revenue. Europe is Booking's twin stronghold, where it leads both supply and demand. In the US its gap is specifically demand and brand, not supply: it holds world-class inventory but a sub-scale, most-Google-dependent US consumer brand in Priceline, so Expedia and Airbnb lead there. APAC ex-China is genuinely two-sided, with Agoda leading supply density and Trip.com leading demand (147.5M monthly visits versus Agoda's 104.8M). The most recent quarter is consistent with demand being the live battleground: Q1 2026 room-night growth was +6% at both BKNG and Expedia against +9% at brand-led Airbnb (competitive-landscape.md, from the Q1-2026 10-Qs). Booking Holdings therefore leads supply acquisition across the Western world outside North America, but the scale difference that decides economics sits on demand generation, where it dominates in Europe, trails in the US, and contests APAC.

D3 (2026-07-02): Take-rate differences across chain hotels, independents & alternative accommodation, geographic mix, and business-quality implications

Question. I am interested in differences in take rates between large chain hotels, independents, and alternative accommodations. How does the mix of these accommodation types differ by geography? What are the implications of this for business quality?

Booking Holdings discloses no take rate by accommodation type. It reports one segment and a single blended take rate — revenue over gross bookings — of 14.5% in FY2025, up from 14.3% in 2024 on higher payment revenues, with management stating the "underlying accommodation take rate" (the room commission alone, before payments and merchant mix) held stable in line with 2019 levels (BKNG Q4-2025 call; Q1-2022 call). The type-level rates below are triangulated estimates with wide bounds, because the standard commission itself spans 10-25% and no per-type economics are filed. The right way to state it is that the take rate is a stack, not one number: a ~15-15.5% underlying room commission that varies with supplier bargaining power, pulled down by flight mix (~4-5% take) and pushed up by payments (+1.1-3.1% on the 70% merchant book), netting to 14.5% (Goldman Communacopia, Sep-2025; business-model workpaper).

Accommodation type Take rate [estimate] Share of Booking.com room nights What sets the level
Large chain hotels ~10-13% (bounds 10-14%) top-10 chains = "low double-digit %" Negotiate below the ~15% standard; hold brand.com/CRS/GDS plus Bonvoy/Honors loyalty, so they push volume direct and bargain the OTA down
Independent hotels ~15-18%+ (bounds 14-20%) bulk of the ~90% independent/AA/small-chain block Pay the ~15% standard, have no global-marketing alternative, and most often opt into Preferred (+3-5%) and fund Genius discounts for visibility
Alternative accommodations ~15% host commission (bounds 14-16%) ~36% of room nights Hotel-style host commission with no separate guest fee, so the take rate approximates the hotel rate: neutral, not dilutive

The mechanisms behind the spread are bargaining power and marketing overlays. Kalibri Labs benchmarks a hotelier's all-in OTA cost of acquisition at 13-17% of room rate versus 3-8% for direct, up to 6.5x the direct investment [web]. A fragmented independent cannot run its own global performance marketing, so that ~15% is accretive on incremental cross-border demand and is BKNG's stickiest, highest-quality take. A chain can, and holds a loyalty-direct alternative, so it negotiates the rate down. On top of the base, Preferred Partner adds +3-5% for placement, and Genius (partners fund 10-20% discounts for roughly 70% more views) lifts effective take on the properties that opt in, mostly independents and increasingly alt-accom; Genius L2/L3 travelers are now in the high-50s percent of Booking.com room nights (Q4-2025 deck). The structural point on alt-accom is the fee model: Booking.com applies its hotel-style ~15% host commission with no separate guest service fee, so alt-accom take approximates hotel take. Airbnb historically did the opposite, a split fee (host ~3% plus guest ~14-16.5%, blended ~13% of GBV), and only began moving to a single host-only ~15.5% fee in October 2025 (ABNB 10-K FY2025). So alt-accom growth is take-rate-neutral for BKNG. Its genuine drags are lower ADR and higher cost-to-serve (partner liability insurance up to $1.0M/occurrence), not the commission. Flights, at ~4-5% take, are the real diluter of the blend.

Accommodation-type mix is a regional variable, and BKNG's book sits on the favorable side of it. The US is chain-heavy, roughly 70% of rooms branded, with Marriott and Hilton running powerful loyalty-direct channels: Marriott booked ~75% of US and ~68% of global room nights through loyalty members in 2025, and Hilton Honors has 243 million members (MAR, HLT 10-Ks FY2025). Europe is the opposite, majority-independent, with integrated-chain penetration only ~20-25% of rooms [web]; fragmented independent supply is where a demand aggregator is most valuable and hardest to disintermediate, and it is Booking.com's home market. Asia is mixed, independent-tilted at the property level with strong local OTA competition from Trip.com and Agoda, won by localization rather than chain deals. BKNG's own book mirrors the European weighting: ~80.6% of FY2025 revenue is attributed to a Netherlands entity (a tax-domicile proxy that overstates European end-demand but confirms a non-US-centric base), Booking.com draws only ~11% of its traffic from the US [web], and ~90% of room nights come from independents, alt-accom, and small brands (Q4-2025 call). BKNG under-indexes where chains are strongest: SiteMinder's 2025 channel data show Expedia Group leading the US/Canada/Mexico aggregate for the first time since 2020, though in the US alone Booking.com remained #1 with Expedia #2 [web]. Alt-accom demand is itself skewed, EMEA-heaviest (Airbnb ~40% of nights) and APAC under-penetrated (~13%), so the same European fragmentation that favors Booking.com's hotels favors its homes, while APAC is greenfield (ABNB 10-K FY2025).

For business quality, this mix is both the source of BKNG's superior economics and the reason they are stable rather than widening. Over-indexing to fragmented European independents earns the ~15.5% accommodation take and a 32.8% operating margin, roughly 2.6x Expedia's. The advantage is durable: European chain penetration is deep-set and slow-moving, and ~77% of European independent-hotel bookings still route through OTAs [web]. But it is bounded. Chain consolidation is a slow supply-side erosion vector. Marriott (+4.3%) and Hilton (+6.7%) each added ~100k net rooms in 2025, a third to two-fifths from conversions of independents, and Hilton launched Outset Collection, a conversion brand aimed at exactly BKNG's core inventory [web]. Every convert gains a loyalty-direct channel that moves volume off the OTA meter. Europe at ~20-25% branded is being penetrated at low-single-digit points a year, not collapsing. Post-DMA parity removal (EEA, mid-2024) has not yet compressed realized commissions: the blended take rose through the transition, the hotels that undercut were chains rather than independents, and algorithmic ranking now enforces what the contract used to [web]. The exposure is a backward-looking damages tail (Spain CNMC ~$476M recorded and suspended on appeal, a 10,000-hotel HOTREC claim), not a forward repricing. Alt-accom is a necessary defensive growth vector that lowers the quality of incremental economics: dilutive on ADR and margin, and BKNG is the #2 brand growing slower than Airbnb (+5.5% versus +9% nights, Q1-2026). Operating margin already compressed about 7 points from ~40% (2014) to 32.8% (2025) on the agency-to-merchant shift and flight/alt-accom dilution, partly offset by improving marketing efficiency. The binding vulnerability, though, sits on the demand side and is independent of accommodation mix: about 30% of revenue is a marketing tax paid largely to Google, and SEO is declining. Europe's independent-heavy mix is a durable floor under take rate and margin, and the directly chain-exposed slice of the book is small. It is a floor, not a growth engine, and it does not protect the Google/AI distribution interface where the real pressure is building.
