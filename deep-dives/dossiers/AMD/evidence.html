Evidence

Claims by section
business-model
  • AMD designs high-performance processors and sells them through three reportable segments: **Data Center** (EPYC server CPUs, Instinct AI GPUs, Pensando networking, data-center FPGAs — 48% of FY2025 revenue), **Client and Gaming** (Ryzen PC processors, Radeon graphics, and sole-source semi-custom console chips for Sony, Microsoft and Valve — 42%), and **Embedded** (the former Xilinx FPGA/adaptive-SoC franchise plus embedded x86 — 10%).
  • Sales run on purchase orders that "may be revised or canceled without penalty"; remaining performance obligations on contracts longer than a year total just $315M, about three days of revenue (10-K FY2025, Backlog and revenue notes).
  • Triangulated for FY2025: server CPU ≈ $9,000–9,700M, Data Center AI GPU (Instinct) ≈ $6,600–7,100M, other (DC FPGA, Pensando, ZT Design) ≈ $300–600M [estimate: two agreeing angles — the CFO endorsed a ~$6.5B Instinct consensus figure at the November 2025 analyst day, and the quarterly path sums to $6.3–7.1B].
  • A marginal EPYC dollar comes in at ~62–68% gross margin against a largely sunk platform R&D base; a marginal Instinct dollar comes in at ~48–52% with memory the largest and currently inflating input, priced deliberately for share ("maximizing gross margin dollars is our #1 priority" — CFO, FAD).
  • Over FY2022→FY2025 the segment's entire net growth reduces to that one variable: units +55% cumulative against a TAM that declined ~3%, with ASP contributing ~16% of the client log-growth and the "AI PC premium" not yet separable from ordinary mix.
  • Semi-custom is a sole-source annuity: co-development NRE plus multi-year production, revenue recognized over time as units are built, no return rights. It tracks console-maker build schedules, not consumer sell-through — the FY2024 collapse (Gaming −58%) happened while consoles still sold ~23M units.
  • AMD guided FY2026 semi-custom down a "significant double-digit percentage" and H2 FY2026 gaming down more than 20% versus H1 (Q4 2025 and Q1 2026 calls).
  • The economic unit is the design win, not the chip: a socket designed into a radar, basestation or test instrument is sole-source for a 10–20-year product life, with the Vivado/Vitis toolchain as the switching-cost anchor.
  • Segment gross margin triangulates to 65–70% from four agreeing angles: Xilinx standalone (68.4% FY2021), Lattice as pure-play comparator (68.2% CY2025), the segment back-out (36.0% operating margin plus a 29–35% allocated-opex ratio), and the CFO ("Embedded actually is very accretive to our gross margin" in a 55% corporate-GM quarter — Q1 2026 call).
  • Design wins are management's stated KPI — $9B (2022) → $12B → $14B → $17B (2025), more than $50B cumulative — but the metric has no disclosed definition, duration or conversion rate, and cumulative segment revenue FY2022–FY2025 was $16,884M, one-third of the claimed win value.
capitalization
  • In five quarters, unconditional purchase commitments went $4,968M → $25,662M (5.2x; $18.3B due in the remainder of 2026), not-yet-commenced leases went to $4.4B, AMD guaranteed $4.1B of a commercial partner's data-center lease obligations for up to 15 years (partner unnamed; guarantees "may be issued in exchange for warrants"; recorded as a credit derivative at immaterial fair value), and AMD issued two customer warrants covering 320M shares at $0.01 — 19.6% of the basic count.
  • The live overhang is the warrants: at the 2026-07-17 price, full vesting of 320M shares transfers ~$158.6B of equity value to the two customers — versus $8.8B of cumulative buybacks in company history.
competitive-advantage
  • AMD holds three different competitive positions on one P&L. The strongest is the least discussed (the FPGA business), the most discussed is the weakest (AI GPUs), and the business in between (x86 CPUs) is real but must be re-earned every product generation.
  • Mercury puts AMD at a record 46.2% of x86 server-CPU revenue on 33.2% of units in Q1 2026; holding 13 points more revenue share than unit share implies a blended ASP roughly 1.7x Intel's [estimate: share arithmetic, bounds 1.5–1.9x]. That premium, sustained across five generations while share still rises, is the cleanest pricing-power evidence in the company — and Intel's own filing concedes the mechanism from the other side: server ASPs down 4% in 2025 "driven by a competitive environment," then repriced +27% in Q1 2026 when its own supply ran short (INTC 10-K FY2025; 10-Q Q1 2026).
  • AMD's ~$6.6–7.1B of 2025 data-center AI revenue [estimate] sits against Nvidia's $193,737M of Data Center revenue (NVDA 10-K FY2026), a merchant share of roughly 3–4%; gross margin is below corporate average by management's own statement; and the anchor relationships were partly paid for — the OpenAI warrant grants up to 160M shares at $0.01, about 9.8% of the share count, vesting on GPU purchase milestones, with grant-date fair value to be recognized as a reduction of revenue (10-K FY2025 Note 14; 10-Q Q1 FY2026). A company with a secure moat does not pay its anchor customer in equity to adopt the product.
  • The client CPU business is a thinner version of the server story: same duopoly membership, same design-execution record, but a blended ASP only ~1.05–1.1x Intel's [estimate: Mercury share arithmetic] because Intel still holds 71.7% of mobile units, near-zero consumer switching costs, and an entrant wave (Qualcomm above 10% of US >$800 Windows retail per Circana; Nvidia's N1X Arm platforms from June 2026; the Nvidia–Intel x86+RTX products beyond that) aimed at the premium tier where AMD's mix gains are concentrated [web].
  • The console business is a real lock without excess economics: switching within a generation is absolute (x86/RDNA backward compatibility held Sony and Microsoft across two-plus generations), but two-to-three monopsony buyers extract the value — Gaming segment operating margin ran 11.3–16.7% across FY2021–FY2024, always below corporate — and the highest-volume console of 2025–26 (Switch 2, 19.9M units) runs Nvidia silicon [web].
  • Three tests most claimed moats fail: (a) pricing integrity through a severe cycle — revenue declined 35.1% peak-to-trough with no evidence of price concessions, while cohort gross margins held (Lattice 68–70% through its own 31% drawdown; Xilinx 66.9–71.8% across FY2019–FY2022); (b) an intra-duopoly gap measured in the same trough year — FY2024 AMD Embedded earned a 40.0% segment margin on $3,557M while Altera, the only other high-end supplier, earned 2.3% non-GAAP on $1,536M ([intel newsroom, accessed 2026-07-18](https://newsroom.intel.com/corporate/intel-partner-deal-news-april2025) [web]); (c) standalone returns when last visible — Xilinx earned 51–114% ROIC in FY2019–FY2021 on ~25% R&D intensity and under 4% capex.
  • Gross margin 23.2% → 49.5% over FY2016–FY2025 is the clearest footprint of an improving position. FY2025 ROIC was 6.7% on the acquisition-inclusive base — below the cost of capital — and ~30.5% excluding goodwill and intangibles.
  • The gap versus Intel widened in AMD's favor on every returns metric; the gap versus Nvidia widened against AMD on the same metrics (GM gap −17.8pts → −21.6pts; ROIC from rough parity in FY2020 to 6.7% vs 110.3%). The moat evidence is strong against the x86 incumbent and absent against the AI incumbent.
competitive-landscape
  • The market is currently a sellers' market in both directions: Intel raised server ASPs 27% YoY in Q1 2026 while shipping 5% fewer units because demand exceeded its supply, and AMD's growth was "much more unit driven" at a ~1.7x ASP premium (INTC 10-Q; Q1 2026 call).
  • Management's 2030 TAM claim doubled from ~$60B (FAD, Nov 2025, "AMD Internal Projection") to >$120B (Q1 2026 call) in six months; the base is verifiable against vendor revenue, the >35%/yr slope is not — observed pool growth is ~28–33% in the best year the server market has ever had.
  • These figures rank AMD third, not second, among merchant AI-silicon suppliers: on AI silicon revenue Broadcom has been larger than AMD's data-center AI line since at least FY2023, and during FY2025 its AI revenue also passed AMD's entire Data Center segment ($20.0B vs $16.6B).
  • Console SoCs are not a merchant market: AMD holds the Sony and Microsoft sockets (PS5 93.7M cumulative; Xbox ~35M [estimate: S&P Kagan tracker]) plus Valve, Nvidia holds Nintendo (Switch 2: 19.9M in ten months, out-selling PS5 over the trailing year [web]); on generation installed base AMD leads ~6.5:1, on trailing-year socket units Nvidia's Nintendo volume was larger.
  • Bottom-up vendor revenue puts the merchant FPGA market at ~$5.7B in CY2025 versus ~$6.0B in CY2019 — zero net growth across six years, with a +54% shortage boom to 2023 and a −38% digestion bust [estimate: sum of vendor disclosures].
  • The profit pool is almost entirely AMD's: Embedded operating income of $1,243M versus Altera's ~$190M annualized and Lattice's $11M GAAP — roughly 85% of the disclosed merchant-FPGA operating profit [estimate].
expectations
  • The headline disagreement: management's 2027 Data Center AI language ("tens of billions") is satisfiable at ~$25–30B, while consensus has written ~$37–41B into 2027 [estimate: residual arithmetic] — the street's central case sits above the floor of management's open-ended target, and the base rates support neither.
  • The guidance record is the internal anchor: 21 of 22 quarters at or above the midpoint, gross margin accurate to ±50bps outside two shock quarters, and one product-line dollar record — the 2024 Data Center GPU ladder — of systematic under-forecasting ($2B initial → >$5B delivered, raised every quarter).
  • models set at troughs (2015, 2017, 2020) were beaten roughly 2x; the one model set during a boom (FAD 2022) missed every margin line at the three-year mark — gross margin by ~3pts, operating margin by ~10pts, FCF margin every year — and was replaced, not reconciled, at FAD 2025, with the gross-margin target itself rebased from >57% to 55–58% without comment [deck: FAD 2022-06-09, slide 203 vs FAD 2025-11-11, slide 186].
financial-history
  • Three margin regimes: Zen converting share into gross margin (23.2% → 48.2%, FY2016–FY2021, ROIC peaking at 80.8%); the Xilinx purchase accounting and PC crash compressing GAAP results (FY2022–FY2023, ROIC 0.7%); and the AI-cycle recovery (FY2024–) in which profit growth runs on very little incremental invested capital — ROIIC on the FY2022→FY2025 window was ~48% because AMD's growth capital sits in R&D, purchase commitments and inventory rather than PP&E.
  • inventory days roughly doubled from the FY2021 trough (84) to FY2025 (165) as the ramp was pre-bought, and FY2025's headline FCF included roughly $2.0B of DSO-timing benefit (87.7 → 66.5 days) [estimate: DSO-held-constant arithmetic]; the underlying continuing FCF margin ex-timing is ~10–12%, versus the 19.4% headline.
forward-model
  • Base incremental operating margins of ~38% in FY2027–28 exceed AMD's best-ever company-level realized incremental (34.2% in FY2021) for consecutive years, so the base asserts operating leverage AMD has never demonstrated even though it sits below consensus.
  • The contra-revenue assumption matters most: the street models it at zero, its magnitude is undisclosed until the first tranche vests, and at the evidence-supported rate it outranks Instinct gross margin as a swing factor.
m-and-a
  • AMD is a disciplined acquirer on the measure that most reliably exposes bad acquirers: zero goodwill impairments across eleven consecutive fiscal years (FY2015–FY2025) while goodwill grew from $289M to $25,126M; the last impairment was $233M in 2014 against ATI-era goodwill.
management-claims
  • The base-rate evidence: in a 15-name reference class over 2004–2025, one company-observation in 37 sustained a ≥35% three-year CAGR from a ≥$25B base (Nvidia 2022→2025), and zero of 26 sustained it for five years — the best five-year outcome ever recorded is TSMC's +21.9%/yr.
  • The claim is open-ended (satisfiable at ~$25–30B) while consensus embeds ~$37–41B [estimate]; a low-end "tens of billions" outcome would meet guidance and miss consensus revenue by $8–12B.
  • The pattern in the disclosure behavior is itself informative: dollar targets appear when the trend runs ahead of them and are replaced by qualitative language when visibility deteriorates.
  • The deals are real demand with priced customer acquisition attached: 160M-share warrants to each (strike $0.01), the OpenAI final tranche vesting at a $600 stock price, expected contra-revenue treatment, and a customer whose ~$1,150B of aggregate vendor commitments run ~55x its revenue [web]. OpenAI concurrently committed ≥10GW to Nvidia and 10GW to Broadcom custom silicon — AMD's 6GW is roughly a quarter of OpenAI's first ~26GW of announced commitments [web].
  • Realized: like-for-like franchise growth ~+0.4%/yr over four years [estimate]; the current recovery (+3%, +6%) is the slowest in the cohort (Lattice +42%, Microchip +35% in their nearest quarters); the guided FY2026 trajectory (+12–14%) undershoots the claim's 20–30% implication; and the headline design-win metric ($17B in 2025, >$50B cumulative) has no disclosed definition while cumulative segment revenue since the acquisition is one-third of it.
  • Integration execution survives every test — zero goodwill impairment, the Altera control case halved in value over the same decade. The economics do not yet: segment operating income of $1,243M is a 2.5% pretax return on the $48.8B consideration (5.4% at the FY2023 peak), the only market transaction since priced the #2 franchise at $8.75B, and the raised >$400M cost-synergy target plus the >$10B revenue-synergy figure were never publicly reported against after June 2022.
  • Program repurchases (~81M shares) approximately equal employee-plan issuance (~80M); the program has functioned as an SBC offset, not a net capital return, and the FAD 2022 commitment to return >40% of FCF was replaced at FAD 2025 by "offset the employee stock dilution first" without reconciliation.
risks
  • The 2027 target is anchored on OpenAI (6GW) and Meta (up to 6GW); AMD granted each a 160M-share penny warrant, guarantees $4.1B of a commercial partner's data-center leases for up to 15 years, and its 10-K concedes customers "may request alternative financing or deferred-payment arrangements."
secular-trends
  • AMD's FY2021→FY2025 revenue CAGR was 20.5% reported; excluding the acquired Embedded segment from both endpoints, organic growth ran ~17.8%/yr [estimate: segment-exclusion method] against world semiconductor dollars compounding 8.6% (WSTS: $556B 2021 → $772.2B 2025 [web]).
  • of the +$8,854M revenue increase, Client contributed 40%, server CPU ~27%, Gaming 15%, and the AI GPU roughly 17% — approximately 83% of the record year came from x86 share gains plus the gaming restock [estimate].
  • in the year the market re-rated AMD as an AI accelerator company, AMD's share of AI accelerator dollars declined — from ~4.2% to ~3.4% of the disclosed AI-silicon pool (Nvidia DC + Broadcom AI + AMD + Intel) [estimate], because Nvidia grew 68% and Broadcom 65% while AMD's Instinct line grew ~30–40% (about ~60% without the ~$1.5B China/MI308 removal).
  • server revenue share 46.2% against a >50% target and a 60–70% plausible ceiling leaves ~1.2–1.5x of share runway; client 27.8% against >40% leaves ~1.4–1.8x. The two franchises that produced ~83% of FY2025 growth have consumed most of their share runway; at management's own targets their growth converges to market growth (~0–4% PC units, ~10–14% server dollars).
technology
  • The forward Data Center AI case rests almost entirely on unshipped technology — MI450/Helios, Venice CPUs, HBM4 supply, rack-scale ROCm — against a strong silicon shipping record since 2017 and no company precedent in rack-scale systems.
  • The evidence of differentiation is the competitor's record: Intel went from ~100% server share in 2017 to 53.8% of x86 server-CPU revenue in Q1 2026 across five failed roadmap responses, while AMD reached 46.2% of revenue on 33.2% of units (Mercury via [Tom's Hardware, accessed 2026-07-18](https://www.tomshardware.com/pc-components/cpus/amd-reaches-46-percent-of-server-x86-cpu-revenue-intel-still-controls-70-percent-of-the-consumer-pc-market-share) [web]).
  • Instead of one monolithic die, AMD partitions products into compute chiplets on the newest node and I/O dies on cheaper nodes, stitched by the proprietary Infinity Fabric and 2.5D/3D packaging. The economics: higher yield on leading nodes, IP reuse across all four product lines, and silicon scaling past reticle limits — a 30x increase in silicon per package from first-gen EPYC (2017) to the Helios-generation MI400, per the CTO (FAD 2025), with 3D V-Cache in its fourth generation and, per management, still without a shipped competitor.
  • Independent tests place MI355X at near-parity, not leadership: MLPerf Training v5.1 (Nov 2025) shows MI355X 3–6% behind averaged B200/B300 systems on the two comparable workloads ([mlcommons.org, accessed 2026-07-18](https://mlcommons.org/2025/11/training-v5-1-results/) [web]), and SemiAnalysis's continuous inference benchmark shows a real but workload-dependent cost advantage — $0.22 vs $0.30 per million tokens against B200 on one FP8 workload at low-to-mid interactivity, with B200 ahead at high interactivity and roughly 20% better throughput per watt ([inferencex.semianalysis.com, accessed 2026-07-18](https://inferencex.semianalysis.com/) [web]).
  • Progress metrics are volume proxies — 2M Hugging Face models runnable, downloads up 10x YoY, day-0 model support (FAD 2025) — while CUDA holds roughly 4M developers and 20-plus years of libraries; SemiAnalysis judges ROCm "genuinely good" and still behind on communication libraries and developer infrastructure ([semianalysis.com "AMD 2.0", accessed 2026-07-18](https://newsletter.semianalysis.com/p/amd-2-0-new-sense-of-urgency-mi450x) [web]).
value-chain
  • The anchor arithmetic: management stated "for each gigawatt of compute, significant double-digit billions of revenue for us" (OpenAI call, 2025-10-06); press aggregation of the 6GW agreement implies ~$15B per GW, or ~$27–33k of AMD content per deployed GPU ([tomtunguz.com, accessed 2026-07-18](https://tomtunguz.com/openai-hardware-spending-2025-2035/) [web]) [estimate].
  • For Client and Gaming the constraint is memory price, not availability: the same shortage AMD navigates upstream is guided to suppress its own H2 FY2026 PC and gaming demand.
  • From FY2023 to FY2025 AMD's GAAP gross margin rose 3.4 points (46.1% → 49.5%) while TSMC's rose 5.5 points (54.4% → 59.9%) and Micron's quarterly margin rose from 38% to 85% in a single year; Nvidia's declined ~4 points as it moved into systems, and the integration layer (Dell ISG, Sanmina) stayed thin.
  • OpenAI's aggregate vendor commitments (~$1,150B across seven vendors, 2025–2035, per press aggregation [web]) run ~55x its ~$20B annualized revenue, so AMD's largest announced ramp rests on its customer's continued access to external capital.
