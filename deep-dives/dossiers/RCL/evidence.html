Evidence

Claims by section
business-model
  • Reported revenue of $17,935M splits 69.8% passenger ticket ($12,515M) and 30.2% onboard and other ($5,419M), a mix stable across the decade.
  • occupancy, which routinely exceeds 100% — 109.7% in FY2025 — because the third and fourth passengers in a cabin are not in the double-occupancy denominator, so every point above 100% is near-pure incremental revenue on a ship that is already sailing
  • The realized incremental operating margin (change in operating income over change in revenue) was 47.5% from 2023 to 2024 and 55.4% from 2024 to 2025, and 40.5% through the cycle from 2019 to 2025 — all far above the ~27% average margin, because most of the cost base is already committed.
  • There is no customer concentration to speak of: revenue comes from roughly 9.4 million discrete passengers a year, no single country exceeds 10% of ticket revenue, and guest sourcing is about 74% United States (FY2025 10-K, Note 3).
  • Partner Brands (50% TUI Cruises, which itself owns Hapag-Lloyd) contributed $414M of equity income in FY2025, up 59% from $260M, with no consolidated revenue or APCD attached — high-return earnings the headline KPI framing omits.
  • Working capital is structurally negative: customer deposits were $5,739M at year-end 2025, about 32% of revenue, an interest-free liability that funds operations and partly funds newbuild deposits.
  • The binding constraint is shipyard slots — only a handful of yards can build large cruise ships — which caps industry supply growth to mid-single-digits and is the structural reason capacity cannot flood the market (Section 5).
  • Q1-2026 confirms the run-rate: revenue $4,452M (up 11.3%) on capacity up 8.3% and Net Yield up 3.6% ($268.23 vs $258.83), with Net Cruise Cost excluding fuel per APCD up only 0.6%.
  • Company-defined ROIC (adjusted operating income over five-quarter-average invested capital) was 18.0% in FY2025, above any plausible cost of capital.
  • The 2024-to-2025 pretax improvement of $1,432M (a 48.7% jump) was only about 56% operating: operating income rose $804M, but net interest expense dropped $598M — about 42% of the total gain
capital-allocation
  • Cumulative free cash flow (operating cash less capex) was -$5.4B; even excluding the three COVID years the model is capex-hungry, capex ($29.0B) exceeding operating cash ($23.7B).
  • Through the 2020 crash (low ~$19) and the 2021-2023 recovery ($31-131) RCL bought back nothing, while it was a forced equity issuer at the bottom (~$9.3B raised in 2020, including converts at $72.11 and $82.50 conversion prices).
  • Measured from the 2019 peak, revenue is up 64% but revenue per share only 25% - dilution consumed roughly 60% of the per-share top-line recovery; margin expansion (operating margin ~19% to ~27%) drove EPS to +74%.
capitalization
  • Net debt to Adjusted EBITDA declined from above 8x (2020–2022) to 3.0x (FY2025) and ~2.9x (TTM through Q1-2026), within sight of the ~2.8x pre-COVID level.
  • RCL is refinancing down, not up: it called 11.625%, 9.25%, 8.25%, and 7.25% COVID-era notes in 2024 and reissued at 5.375%–6.25%, saving roughly 3–5 percentage points on ~$3.7B refinanced, which is why the weighted-average rate keeps falling even as base rates stayed high.
competitive-advantage
  • RCL's advantage is real and, on the financial evidence, widening - but it sits inside a cyclical, capital-intensive, capacity-contestable industry, so it is a strong but contestable advantage, not a structural one of the Visa or Google type.
  • FY2025 operating margin 27.4% versus Carnival 16.8% and NCLH 15.9% - a premium that grew from about 3 points over Carnival and 1 point over NCLH in 2019 to about 11 points over each in 2025.
  • FY2025 ROIC about 16.1% (computed here), up every post-COVID year (9.7% -> 11.7% -> 14.2% -> 16.1% across 2019/2023/2024/2025), versus Carnival 12.1% and NCLH 9.2%.
  • Revenue compounded 8.6% a year since 2019, the fastest of the three (NCLH 7.2%, Carnival 4.2%), while also being the most profitable - growth and returns moving together.
  • The advantage also requires continuous heavy reinvestment to defend - capex ran 20-30% of revenue even at peak profitability - the opposite of a capital-light compounder.
  • The live test is the 2026 Caribbean capacity surge, into which RCL guided constant-currency Net Yield growth of only +1.5% to +2.5%, decelerating from +3.7% in 2025 - pricing power that is real but visibly moderating at the margin even as the absolute margin premium widens.
  • What is RCL-specific and survives the test is owned destinations (near-irreplaceable, high-margin), scale and unit cost (the lowest Net Cruise Cost per APCD, visible in the margin premium), and a deeper loyalty and digital ecosystem (modest switching costs).
  • NCLH posted negative free-cash-flow margin in FY2023 and FY2025 — it cannot self-fund its growth capex, the clearest sign its position is the weakest.
competitive-landscape
  • RCL is the global number two cruise operator by capacity and revenue (behind Carnival) and the clear profit leader of the mass-ocean group: its FY2025 Adjusted EBITDA of $7,025M slightly exceeds Carnival's (~$6.97B) on roughly 55-57% of Carnival's berths.
  • It is an oligopoly: the four largest operators - Carnival, Royal Caribbean Group, NCLH, and MSC - represented approximately 80% of cruise-industry capacity at end-2025 (Carnival FY2025 10-K, citing Cruise Industry News).
  • Ocean cruise demand was about 37M global guests in 2025 (35M in 2024, 32M in 2023), a roughly 7.5% two-year CAGR, against a weighted-average ~725,000 berths marketed across ~430 ships (FY2025 10-K, citing CLIA and G.P. Wild).
  • North American cruise penetration reached 5.96% of population in 2025 (up from 3.36% in 2015), Europe 1.73%, and Asia/Pacific only 0.09%.
  • The "$2 trillion+ vacation market" management cites is the entire leisure-travel wallet - hotels, resorts, theme parks - not an addressable cruise market; the decision-relevant market is the ~37M-guest, roughly $60-70B-revenue ocean cruise market in which the big four already hold ~80% of capacity.
  • RCL generates Adjusted EBITDA of $131.75 per APCD versus Carnival's roughly $72 per ALBD
  • MSC is the structural share-gainer: private, ~10% of global passengers, going from 23 to 27 ships by 2030 with 10 ships on order through 2033
  • Carnival leads on raw scale (94 ships, 272,380 lower berths, 13.6M guests, $26.6B revenue — about 1.8x RCL's capacity) but is deliberately not growing it (capacity-days up 2.8% a year, only 7 ships on order through 2033).
expectations
  • management says it is tracking ahead of Perfecta's 20% adjusted-EPS CAGR, while Bloomberg consensus CY2027 ($19.95) sits about 2% below the Perfecta-implied ~$20.4.
  • the live forward requirement (~14.2%/yr 2025->2027, because 2025 already delivered +32.5%) is only about 1 point above consensus's ~13.1%, so the gap is about $0.45 of EPS, not a chasm.
  • The most consequential disagreement is therefore not the EPS endpoint but the durability of margin and ROIC at all-time-high, top-of-class levels -- the part of the package with the weakest cross-sectional base-rate support
financial-history
  • By itinerary, North America was about $11,542M (64% of revenue), Europe $2,951M (16%), Asia/Pacific $1,716M (10%), and other regions the balance — a US-centric footprint (~74% US guest sourcing).
history-inflections
  • RCL emerged more profitable than pre-COVID — Adjusted EBITDA margin 30% (2019) → 39.2% (2025); net income per APCD $36 → $80; 2025 net income ~2.3x the 2019 record.
m-and-a
  • Royal Caribbean is an organic grower; its engine is newbuild ships and owned destinations, not acquisitions.
  • There is one substantial acquisition (Silversea), strategically coherent — it filled the only gap in the brand ladder (ultra-luxury and expedition, the highest-yield end) — but bought at a full peak-cycle multiple with no margin of safety: within ~18 months RCL wrote off $576.2M of the $1,090.0M recorded goodwill (53%) plus a $30.8M trade-name charge in Q1-2020.
secular-trends
  • Asia/Pacific penetration more than halved from its 2019 level on "lack of cruise supply and itineraries in China," so the emerging-market leg the pre-COVID thesis leaned on has gone backwards.
  • The 2022–2024 yield surge was a one-time level reset, not a permanently steeper slope. Net Yield growth ran +13.5% (2023 vs 2019), +11.5% (2024), then decelerated to +3.8% (2025) and a +1.5% to +2.5% guide (2026).
  • On guests, RCL grew 44.1% (6.55M → 9.45M) against the industry's 23.3% (30M → 37M), lifting guest share from 21.8% to 25.5%.
  • Management's own "durable" range is +2% to +4% a year, barely above the pre-COVID pace (Net Revenues per APCD grew ~5.9%/yr 2016–2019).
technology
  • RCL markets itself as an innovation leader, but on inspection it is a brand, scale, and owned-content business that uses technology competently rather than a technology company.
  • more than half of Caribbean sailings visit a private destination, over 70% of Royal Caribbean brand Caribbean guests do so today rising to 90% by 2028, and CocoCay is the brand's highest-rated destination on Net Promoter Score (Q4 2024 / Q4 2025 calls)
  • Chantiers de l'Atlantique builds MSC's LNG World-class mega-ships at the same Saint-Nazaire yard where it builds RCL's Oasis and new Discovery-class ships, and Fincantieri states one in every three cruise passengers sails on a ship its yards built
  • Carnival ran the world's first LNG cruise ship, AIDAnova, in December 2018
  • digital booking penetration more than doubled versus 2019, app monthly active users are about 5x 2019 with over 90% adoption, pre-cruise booking-engine penetration is over 70% with more than 5 items per booking, more than half of onboard revenue is now booked before boarding, and pre-cruise purchasers spend about 2.5x more (Q1 2026 / Q2 2025 calls)
  • The defensible part is the first-party data at scale and the captive multi-day surface, not a unique algorithm; Carnival and NCLH run their own apps and loyalty programs, so the gap is the scale of data, contestable in principle.
value-chain
  • RCL is unusually vertically integrated for a service company: it owns the ships (the largest cost in the chain, amortized over 25–35 years), increasingly owns the itinerary content (private destinations), co-owns the scarce drydock-repair capacity (33% of Grand Bahama Shipyard), and is pushing distribution in-house.
  • RCL is capturing more of the dollar at every interface except the shipyards. Its own take rose from 19.0% of revenue (2019) to 27.4% (2025).
  • Customer deposits ($5,739M at end-2025, $6,548M by March 2026) are interest-free customer financing that produces the industry's structural negative working capital — the same model Carnival ($8.9B working-capital deficit) and NCLH ($3.2B advance ticket sales) run.
  • sovereign export-credit financing funds ~80% of every newbuild: each loan is 95–100% guaranteed by the export credit agency of the build country (Finnvera, Bpifrance, Euler Hermes) at coupons of 3.0–4.3%, so France, Germany, and Finland subsidize the financing to keep their yards full.
variant-perceptions
  • On the industry's own pricing metric, RCL's Net Yield ($273.51) trails NCLH ($301.10, +10%) and Viking ($583, +113%). RCL's edge is occupancy (109.7%, the highest), scale, owned-destination content, and cost, not per-day price.
  • The current orderbook is 47 ships and ~113,000 berths through 2029, versus 78 ships and ~183,000 berths through 2027 in the FY2021 10-K - roughly 38% fewer berths on order.

The forward-delivery window differs across the two anchor disclosures: the FY2021 10-K counts ships 'through 2027' (a six-year window from the December 31, 2021 'as of' date) while the FY2025 10-K counts 'through 2029' (a four-year window). A shorter forward window mechanically captures fewer ships, so part - but not all - of the headline 38% decline reflects the narrower 2025 horizon. The FY2022, FY2023 and FY2024 10-Ks all use a constant 'through 2028' window and still fall from 143,000 to 110,000 to 116,500 berths, confirming a genuine multi-year contraction in the industry-wide orderbook rather than a pure window effect.
— Cross-year reconciliation of RCL 10-K 'increase in capacity worldwide' risk factor (FY2021-FY2025), 2025-12-31
