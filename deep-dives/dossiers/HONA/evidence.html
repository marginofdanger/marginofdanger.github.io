Evidence

Claims by section
business-model
  • The single largest exposure is the recurring installed-base annuity, not new-build.
  • A narrowbody auxiliary power unit generates more than 20x its initial original-equipment sale value in lifetime maintenance revenue, and the commercial book runs roughly 3:1 aftermarket to original equipment today
  • The reason the aftermarket dollar is defensible is certification: replacing a certified engine-start, environmental-control, or avionics box mid-program requires re-qualification the airframer will not fund, so the operator buys mandated parts from the design-authority incumbent at a price the incumbent sets.
  • the commercial APU base grows roughly 5% a year and the HTF7000 business-jet fleet roughly 9% a year through 2030
  • RMU was $1.6B in FY2025, about 9% of aerospace revenue, growing roughly 18% a year since FY2021
  • FY2025 product gross margin was 24.4% and service gross margin was 48.9%
  • Consolidated gross margin declined 3.5 points over three years, 38.3% (FY2023) to 34.8% (FY2025)
  • the highest-margin segment (Electronic Solutions) is the least aftermarket-weighted, with services only 38.6% of revenue and declining from 45.3% in FY2023, while the most aftermarket-weighted segment (Engines & Power Systems, services 49.3%) earns the lowest margin.
  • The $373M Flexjet charge cuts the reported FY2025 E&PS margin to 12.8% from an underlying 19.7%, confirmed at 19.8% in Q1 2026.
  • top-5 ES customers are 37% of ES revenue, top-10 E&PS customers 43%.
  • FY2025 net sales were $17,404M across three reported product segments: Electronic Solutions ($6,816M, 39.2%), Engines & Power Systems ($5,411M, 31.1%), and Control Systems ($5,177M, 29.7%)
  • The company also discloses an end-market cut that is more revealing of the economics: Commercial Aftermarket $7,661M (44%), Defense & Space $7,149M (41%), and Commercial Original Equipment $2,594M (15%)
capitalization
  • Opening leverage is roughly 3.3–3.5x adjusted (4.1x net on GAAP EBITDA), above the 2.5x target, and the gap closes only through the full 2030 growth plan (needing roughly $6.4B EBITDA), not scheduled paydown
competitive-advantage
  • The advantage is real and durable but sits one notch below a structural monopoly: real excess returns protected by certification barriers and a 30-year installed-base annuity, but earned in a business that must continuously re-win each platform, defend a contestable aftermarket at the edges, and reinvest in R&D and design-win capture to hold its position.
  • It is the second tier: a durable-annuity advantage with real excess returns today, defensible for now, but liable to compress as original-equipment mix normalizes, large operators bargain harder on aftermarket terms, and next-generation platform competitions reopen the content fights.
  • Electronic Solutions' 29.2% segment margin sits about 13 points above RTX's Collins Aerospace (16.3%) and well above L3Harris's Space & Airborne Systems (12.3%)
  • Only TransDigm (about 54% EBITDA margin) earns materially more per dollar, and it does so with a proprietary-parts pricing model HONA does not replicate.
  • the moat is strongest in commercial avionics/navigation and APUs, and thinner in defense electronics, connectivity, and flight-control actuation.
  • the adjusted segment margin moved only from 27.4% to 25.6% and troughed in FY2024, not FY2025
  • HONA holds roughly 70% of the commercial APU market with a 47,000-unit installed base compounding at 5% a year, sole-sourced onto all in-production narrowbodies and all but one widebody.
  • It is instead HONA's lowest-margin segment — FY2025 adjusted EBIT margin 19% versus 29% for both ES and CS — earning only about 24% of adjusted group segment profit on 31% of revenue
  • segment margin contracted 320 basis points over 2021–2025 while every peer expanded in the same upcycle
competitive-landscape
  • Management estimates a company-wide addressable market of roughly $167B — Commercial Air Transport $85B+, Business Aviation $25B, Defense & Space $57B
  • HONA's 6–8% organic target embeds roughly 1 point a year of durable share gain over the market, sourced from RMU and content-per-shipset wins rather than winning share of aircraft
  • is the clear scale leader in commercial APUs (roughly 70% share, ~47,000 units in service) and the leader in midsize/super-midsize business-jet propulsion, but it is structurally absent from the large-cabin business-jet engine pool that Rolls-Royce dominates
  • is the number-two broad-line thermal/motion-control integrator behind Collins (roughly 2x CS on the ~$10–12B of overlapping content [estimate]), comparable to or larger than Safran's landing/actuation franchise, Parker Aerospace, Liebherr, and Woodward on the overlap.
  • it is a sub-scale challenger in flight-control actuation — a sub-market that just consolidated around Safran, which bought Collins' roughly $1.55B flight-control/actuation business (closed July 2025).
  • Starlink's LEO broadband is displacing legacy GEO SATCOM inside avionics fast (roughly 100 to 2,000+ aircraft in about a year, including Lufthansa 850 and NetJets 600)
expectations
  • Management guides adjusted EBIT to grow faster than 6–8% sales, implying roughly +250–300 basis points of margin expansion to 2030
  • The FCF and margin-level targets are ordinary for the model and likely delivered; the margin-expansion target is a plausible-but-not-easy reversal; and the growth target's high end is a top-quartile outcome.
secular-trends
  • The durable floor under HONA's demand is mid-single-digit, not zero, and rests on seven drivers, five durable and two cyclical.
  • HON Aerospace revenue declined 18% in 2020 to $11,544M, then reached a $11,026M trough in 2021, a peak-to-trough decline of roughly 22% — yet segment margin held 25.2% in 2020 and expanded to a 27.7% peak in 2021 on the shrunken base
  • Peak-to-peak growth from the 2019 pre-COVID peak to FY2025 is only about 3.7% a year
  • content-per-shipset expansion from electrification, connectivity, and autonomy, with more than $90B of life-of-program awards won 2022–2025
technology
  • Company-funded R&D is only 3.9% of sales ($677M in FY2025); total R&D reaches roughly 10% of sales only because customers fund the other ~$1,074M
  • HONA's large APUs are on all in-production narrowbody platforms and all but one in-production widebody, with more than 95,000 delivered and 47,000 in service across a 50-year franchise
  • Honeywell and Pratt & Whitney together hold effectively the entire commercial APU market as a duopoly, with Honeywell around 70% and Pratt & Whitney around 30%
  • The HTF7000 has roughly 13 million hours of service life and 99.99% dispatch reliability
  • Anthem is a cloud-native, modular flight deck with five platform wins and more than $10B of claimed lifetime value, but it completed first flight only in May 2023 and is not yet in commercial service, and management's own slide places broad adoption in the 2030s
  • the demonstrable leads are APUs, business-jet engines, precision inertial navigation, rad-hard space, and select post-CAES EW franchises, while avionics, thermal, actuation, and connectivity are contested fields with several strong players.
value-chain
  • The tier-1 systems layer and its aftermarket, not the airframe assembler, is where the aerospace dollar concentrates.
  • Boeing — HONA's single largest downstream customer — earns 4.8% at the company level and loses money assembling the airplane, while HONA earns 23.5% segment profit and TransDigm 53.9% EBITDA on the content bolted into it.
  • HONA sells the 49%-gross-margin service stream directly to the airline over the 30-year life, bypassing the airframe OEM, which never touches the richest part of HONA's dollar.
  • customers provide net ~$1,314M of working-capital financing through contract-liability advances, and a majority of R&D is customer-funded

The single largest exposure is the recurring installed-base annuity, not new-build.
