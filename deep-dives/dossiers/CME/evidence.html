All harvested proof points · company first, then peers

Evidence

Claims by section
business-model
  • CME Group runs the largest futures and options exchange and clearing house in the world. It earns a small fee each time a futures or options contract trades and clears, plus recurring subscription revenue for the market data those markets generate.
  • Revenue is volume times rate per contract. In FY2025, clearing and transaction fees were $5,281M of $6,521M total revenue, or 81%, and within that line the futures-and-options fee equals average daily volume times trading days times the average rate per contract.
  • Volume is overwhelmingly the driver of growth, not price: management decomposed the FY2025 fee increase of $290M into $261M from higher contract volume and $28.5M from a higher average rate per contract, so roughly 90% of fee growth was volume
  • The rate per contract drifts down over time, from $0.700 in FY2023 to $0.696 in FY2025 and $0.652 in Q1 2026, because volume-tiered pricing lowers the per-contract rate as a customer trades more, and because lower-priced micro-sized contracts keep growing as a share of the mix
  • The rate per contract spans a 3x range across lines, from $0.48 in interest rates (the cheapest unit but the highest volume, so the largest revenue line) to about $1.43 in metals and agriculture. The only variable cost is the index-licensing revenue share, roughly $0.05 per contract blended, so the next contract on an existing line earns about $0.65 of its $0.70 rate as incremental operating income, a 92% contribution margin.
  • Three lines are economically distinct from the per-contract derivatives model. Cash markets (BrokerTec for cash U.S. Treasuries, European and U.K. government bonds and repo; EBS for spot foreign exchange and spot precious metals) are priced on notional value, not per contract, and face direct price competition; they came in with the 2018 NEX acquisition and have been roughly flat at $284M for three years.
  • Market data is recurring subscription revenue, billed per device or subscriber, with 32 consecutive quarters of year-over-year growth, about 53% earned outside the United States and roughly 30% via the two largest resellers
  • The defining feature is a nearly fixed cost base. Total expenses were $2,280M in FY2019 and $2,291M in FY2025, essentially flat in dollars, while revenue grew about $1.65 billion over the same six years
  • That fixed base produces incremental operating margins of 75% to 90% in normal years (FY2023 75%, FY2024 90%, FY2025 76%).
  • Capital intensity is minimal: capital expenditure averaged 2.0% of revenue over the decade and is now about 1.3%, below depreciation in most years, so CME grows with almost no incremental tangible capital
  • One clearing firm accounted for 12% of clearing and transaction-fee revenue in 2025, up from 10% in 2024 with none above 10% in 2023, and the two largest resellers for about 30% of market-data revenue
capitalization
  • Total assets of $198.4 billion at year-end 2025 are dominated by $159.7 billion of clearing-firm performance bonds and guaranty-fund contributions — restricted cash CME holds as central counterparty, matched by an identical $159.7 billion liability. The operating balance sheet excluding collateral is roughly $38.8 billion of assets.
  • This is an under-levered, AA-/Aa3-rated balance sheet with no leverage maintenance covenant; the material counterparty risk sits off the operating balance sheet, in the clearing house's $159.7 billion collateral pool and default waterfall, not in this debt.
competitive-advantage
  • CME's advantage is real and sits close to the structural end of the scale, closer to Visa and Mastercard network economics than to an advantage that is eroding, but it is not uniform across the business and one public peer earns comparable economics in its own protected niche.
  • The margin evidence is unambiguous and points the opposite way from a moat being competed away: CME's net-revenue operating margin (64.9% FY2025, a record 69.7% in Q1 2026) is the highest of the four listed exchange peers, and its premium over ICE widened from 3.8 points in 2020 to 15.2 points in 2025, while over Nasdaq it widened from 16.3 to 20.5 points.
  • Cboe earns a 60.4% FY2025 operating margin on its own exclusive SPX and VIX index-options franchise, within 4.5 points of CME at the full-year level and roughly level in the most recent quarter, and it actually out-margined CME in 2020, so "highest margin in the industry by a wide margin" is true versus ICE and Nasdaq but only narrowly true versus Cboe.
  • Operating margin rose from a 53.2% trough in FY2019 (the NEX integration year) to 64.9% in FY2025, and to a record 69.7% in Q1 2026
  • Free-cash-flow margin was 64.3% in FY2025.
  • On a tangible-capital basis, stripping the acquired intangibles and goodwill, the business earns several-hundred-percent returns on the cash actually tied up, because it grows on essentially no incremental tangible capital — capital expenditure is about 1.3% of revenue and below depreciation
competitive-landscape
  • CME is the scale leader in every listed-derivatives line it competes in, and the gap is structurally wide in its two most valuable lines: it runs essentially the entire U.S.-dollar listed interest-rate complex (rates ADV 14.2 million contracts/day FY2025) and owns the deepest U.S. equity-index futures pool (E-mini complex ADV 7.4 million/day).
  • The only direct-attack challenger, the bank-backed FMX Futures Exchange, reached roughly 39,000 SOFR-futures contracts a day in Q1 2026 against CME's roughly 3.9 million SOFR-futures ADV — on the order of 1% after 18 months — so the incumbency gap in the flagship line is not closing.
  • Cboe's SPX index options (ADV 3.9 million) sit beside CME's E-mini S&P futures (ADV 4.1 million), but these are different instruments — options on the index versus futures on the index — so apparent parity overstates head-to-head competition; CME owns the futures pool, Cboe owns the listed index-options pool.
  • CME (NYMEX) owns the U.S. inland crude benchmark (WTI, share back above 79-80% in Q1 2026) and U.S. natural gas (Henry Hub); ICE owns the global seaborne crude benchmark (Brent) and European gas (TTF). Each is dominant in its own benchmark, and neither is dislodging the other's home benchmark.
  • BrokerTec's U.S. Treasury cash average daily notional ($96.6 billion) is well below Tradeweb's government-bond ADV (roughly $230 to 250 billion) and shrank 5% in FY2025, while Tradeweb grew double digits.
critical-points
  • **The business is a volume story on a fixed cost base.** Total expenses were flat in dollars from FY2019 to FY2025 (roughly $2.28 billion to $2.29 billion) while revenue grew about $1.65 billion, producing incremental operating margins of 75% to 90% and lifting operating margin from a 53.2% trough to 64.9%, then to a record 69.7% in Q1 2026.
  • **The rate per contract falls as volume rises, so record revenue comes with a declining blended rate.** The blended futures-and-options rate went from $0.700 in FY2023 to $0.696 in FY2025 to $0.652 in Q1 2026, down 6.3%, as volume-tiering and micro-contract mix grew with the volume itself.
  • **Interest rates are the largest line and the most secularly supported, but their durability is the central question.** Interest rates are 26.4% of revenue with Treasury open interest at an all-time high, yet rates revenue decelerated to +3.6% in FY2025 from +6.9% in FY2024, and the secular driver is futures penetration of the cash market — already over 100% — which has a ceiling.
  • **The moat is real, durable, and its premium over scale peers is widening.** Operating margins near 65% and tangible-capital returns in the hundreds of percent are consistent with a structural liquidity-network advantage, and CME's margin premium over ICE widened from 3.8 to 15.2 points over five years while ICE's margin was flat. The one qualifier is Cboe, which earns a comparable 60.4% margin on its own SPX/VIX niche.
  • Consensus EPS growth of about 7% a year through 2028 is the lowest of a 15-name exchange-and-data reference class whose median is roughly 12%, because CME returns essentially 100% of free cash as dividends and only began buying back stock in FY2025, forgoing the 2 to 4 points of EPS growth peers manufacture from buybacks.
  • **CME is a disciplined, low-velocity acquirer with zero goodwill impairment in its history.** Goodwill of $10,515M has only ever moved by foreign-currency translation and the OSTTRA divestiture, never a write-down. The one large deal in the window (NEX, 2018) hit its $200M cost-synergy target on schedule; the OSTTRA JV was monetized twice for about $707M of gains; and CME walked from a contested 2014 auction (GFI) and collected a break fee rather than overpay.
expectations
  • A 7% forward EPS expectation is what the outside view predicts for a 100%-dividend, low-buyback franchise; lifting CME from no net buyback to a peer-typical 2% annual share shrink would add roughly 2 points to EPS growth, moving it toward the cohort's lower-middle. The entire EPS-growth gap to peers is a capital-allocation choice, not a business-quality deficit.
financial-history
  • The operating margin moved from 61.2% in FY2016 to a 53.2% trough in FY2019 — the NEX integration year, when a full year of NEX operating costs and an intangible-amortization jump from $130M to $315M hit before revenue synergies arrived — then rebuilt to 64.9% by FY2025 on flat expenses, a +11.7-point recovery driven almost entirely by operating leverage rather than any price action.
  • Within FY2025, the growth leaders were metals (+24.8% on the safe-haven and retail surge), agricultural commodities (+12.5%), and market data (+13.1%); the laggards were energy (+1.6% after a +13.9% FY2024), FX (−0.8%, the only line down), and IRS clearing (−4.3%).
  • Free-cash-flow conversion exceeds net income (FCF/NI about 1.05 over the decade, 1.03 in FY2025) because non-cash intangible amortization and depreciation add back and capital expenditure is trivial.
forward-model
  • The base case extends the last six years: revenue compounds 5.6% on volume (aggregate ADV from 28.1 to 42.5 million a day) with the rate per contract drifting down about 0.5% a year, and operating margin rises to 67.3% on the fixed-cost leverage.
  • EPS compounds slower than operating income (5.0% versus 6.1%) because the net collateral-reinvestment income line fades as the Fed cuts, only partly offset by a modest buyback.
  • The bear case is the calm-markets, cost-creep world: the 2026 volatility steps are small, steady CAGRs sit near GDP, the rate per contract declines 1.5% a year, and core operating expense grows 7% a year, so margin compresses to 57.3% and EPS goes nowhere, protected only by the recurring market-data floor and the Treasury and SOFR secular base.
  • The bull case is a continuation of the elevated-volatility, retail-adoption regime, with rates and energy compounding 7%, metals 10%, and core opex held to 4%; the weakest link is that it sustains roughly 85% incremental margins for seven straight years on a near-doubling of revenue, at the top of CME's historical band.
  • The rate per contract is the largest and most fragile assumption because it is coupled to the volume steps — the volume-tiering that drives volume is the same mechanism that lowers the rate — so the model's high-volume scenarios are internally optimistic if they keep the rate decline shallow.
history-inflections
  • Across these episodes CME hardened the cost structure in crises rather than retreating (the 2020 floor closure; expenses up 0.5% in dollars from 2019 to 2025 across a pandemic and a rate cycle); forced changes are converted into franchise (LIBOR retirement became the SOFR and Term SOFR franchise; negative oil became a benchmark-resilience proof point); M&A is rare, deliberate and cleanly absorbed; windfalls go back to shareholders, predominantly as dividends; and competitive threats are met combatively in the narrative and, so far, defeated in the data.
m-and-a
  • CME is a disciplined, deliberate, low-velocity acquirer, closer to "rarely does big deals, and the ones it does are absorbed cleanly" than to a serial roll-up.
  • The cleanest deal-quality signal is zero goodwill impairment across the entire history. Goodwill of $10,515M at year-end 2025 has only ever moved by foreign-currency translation and the OSTTRA deconsolidation, never a write-down, and the three blocks (CBOT $5,066M, NYMEX $2,462M, NEX ~$2,914M) have been held at acquisition value through the 2020 volatility shock and the post-LIBOR rates regime.
  • The one large deal in the window, NEX (2018), hit its $200M cost-synergy target on the originally stated timeline.
  • The divestiture record is value-accretive: the OSTTRA business was acquired bundled inside NEX, contributed to a JV for a $400.7M formation gain, then sold to KKR for about $1.55 billion and a further $306.1M gain.
  • in 2014 CME agreed to acquire GFI Group at $4.55 a share for its Trayport energy-software assets, matched a hostile BGC tender at $5.25 in a contested auction, then walked when the deal was terminated in January 2015, collecting a $22.5M break fee; BGC won and sold Trayport to ICE for $650 million.
  • The one real criticism is that NEX's headline rationale under-delivered. The cash-markets line it was sold on generated $283.7M in FY2025, roughly flat for three years and price-competitive, so on the cash-markets pitch a roughly $5.6 billion deal bought a $284M flat revenue line; on what it actually delivered (Treasury and repo scale, market data, the Term SOFR benchmark business) it was sound.
  • The NEX cost program is the one hard scorecard: a $50M target for end-2019 was beaten ($64M), $110M for end-2020 achieved, and the full $200M cumulative run-rate hit in Q3 2021 as guided; management never quietly retired the target.

management-incentives
  • CEO total compensation was $23.4M in FY2025, about 88% variable, roughly 5x the next executive. The annual bonus runs on a single metric, Cash Earnings — a near-EBITDA distributable-cash measure that is also the number setting the dividend — so management is paid on the same cash pool it distributes. Long-term equity is 50% time-vested and 50% performance shares, measured against relative total shareholder return versus the S&P 500.
  • Over FY2016 to FY2025 CME generated $28.2 billion of operating cash flow, spent just $1.2 billion (4.3%) on capex, paid out $25.3 billion in dividends (89.8% of operating cash flow), and bought back nothing until FY2025. CME pays out nearly all of its cash flow as dividends; per-share value has compounded through operating growth, not capital deployment.
market-pricing
  • Every one of the last 12 next-session reactions sat inside ±4%, and 9 of 12 inside ±2%, so there are no ±5% moves to explain. CME prints almost never move the stock, because the variable that drives the quarter (average daily volume by product line) is published monthly by CME in advance, so by report day the revenue line is nearly fully known.
  • In every up year the total return runs 2 to 6 points above the price return, and that wedge is CME's large regular-plus-special dividend. From the FY2016 close to the run date the price rose about 102% ($115.35 to $232.91) while the gross-dividend total-return index rose about 250%, so reinvested dividends more than doubled the price-only return.
risks
  • The whole revenue base is volume × rate, and Q1 2026's record volume was driven by Fed-policy, geopolitical, and safe-haven volatility that CME's own risk language says is transient. A 10% volume decline across the derivatives complex is roughly $480-520M of revenue, nearly all of it operating income.
  • **A credible new rates-futures entrant.** The bank-backed FMX Futures Exchange targets CME's most valuable line (interest rates, 26.4% of revenue) directly. The network-effect moat has repelled it so far — FMX's SOFR-futures ADV is about 1% of CME's after 18 months — but it is the one to watch.
  • **Erosion of the cross-margining capital-efficiency advantage.** The roughly $72-85 billion a day of margin savings depends partly on CME cross-margining with FICC, which CME's own CEO calls "critically important" and locates the value "with DTCC and the FICC clearing organization." As the SEC's expanded Treasury-clearing mandate brings FICC, DTCC, ICE and LCH alternatives online, the offset exclusivity could narrow.
  • The November 27, 2025 CyrusOne cooling failure (human error) forced CME to halt its markets and reopen the next day delayed — the first disclosed market halt of this kind in the 10-K window. The Google Cloud migration adds a new third-party dependency the 10-K says "may increase our risk of a cyber attack."
  • **The cash-markets and FX lines are price-competitive and not protected by the liquidity moat.** Cash markets (4.4% of revenue) have been flat for three years against Tradeweb and MarketAxess; FX futures (3.0%) declined in FY2025.
  • The single most important correlated pair is rates volume and collateral income: both are driven by Fed policy and rates volatility, so a benign-rate, low-volatility regime simultaneously pulls volume down, lets the rate per contract keep falling, and fades the collateral line. The bear case models that combination.
secular-trends
  • Revenue grew at a 6.8% nine-year compound rate (FY2016 $3,595M to FY2025 $6,521M) and aggregate ADV grew at the same ~6.8%, so long-run growth comes from contract volume on a fixed cost base, with the blended rate roughly flat.
  • Penetration has a ceiling: once futures turnover exceeds cash turnover, the tailwind that drove the 2012-2020 outperformance exhausts and the rates line reverts toward issuance growth plus volatility, a materially lower slope than the historical penetration gain implies.
  • Q1 2026 is a cyclical peak on a secular trend. Aggregate ADV reached 36.2 million contracts a day, up 22% year over year, with the surge concentrated in the most volatility-sensitive lines: metals revenue up 67% on a silver and gold safe-haven surge (silver moving from roughly $50 to $118 to $86), energy revenue up 22% on geopolitical disruption, while the calmer lines (equity index +4%, agriculture +2%) sat near the secular base.
technology
  • CME's own 10-K lists the competitive factors in order — brand and reputation, clearing and settlement, depth and liquidity, capital and margin efficiencies, product breadth — with "technological capability and innovation" near the bottom; the durable edge is the concentrated open interest, not the matching engine.
  • Because CME both lists and clears, it can offer netting and portfolio offsets a stand-alone exchange or stand-alone clearer cannot, which is the source of the capital-efficiency advantage.
  • SPAN is the official performance-bond mechanism of more than 50 registered exchanges, clearing organizations and regulators worldwide — a de-facto standard a rival must also use, which CME licenses to its own competitors
  • in April 2026 CME received approval to extend the FICC cross-margin to end-user clients, where the house-side program already delivers average daily savings just over $1 billion and a maximum near $1.5 billion, with client-book savings management estimates at "upward of 80%"
  • Running a sub-150-microsecond, zero-downtime matching and clearing system in a public-cloud region is unproven at this scale — no peer derivatives exchange runs its live core markets in a hyperscaler cloud — which makes this the single biggest technology execution risk in the forward case.
  • Perpetual futures on crypto-native venues never expire and run at high leverage with auto-liquidation; in June 2026 the CFTC approved a Bitcoin perpetual for Kalshi, and the S&P Dow Jones Indices JV that CME partly owns licensed an S&P 500 perpetual to a venue on the Hyperliquid blockchain.
value-chain
  • The only layer that takes a cut of CME's revenue is the index licensor, and CME owns 27% of the biggest one. The $371.0M licensing expense is the single variable cost, paid mostly to S&P Dow Jones Indices, but because CME owns 27% of that JV, a portion of the royalty returns below the line as equity-method earnings ($371.7M of all-JV equity earnings in FY2025, of which S&P/DJI is the largest piece), so the net economic leakage on equity-index products is smaller than the gross royalty.
  • The collateral layer is a $5.3 billion gross flow that nets to about $411M and is a pass-through, not a profit pool CME owns: reading the $5.3 billion as CME income overstates the rate-sensitive earnings by roughly 13x.
  • is the liquidity and open-interest network effect, not any physical capacity or input supply; regulatory status reinforces it but is secondary.
  • The one new upstream threat is that CME's own 27%-owned S&P Dow Jones Indices JV licensed S&P 500 perpetual futures to a Hyperliquid-blockchain venue, a competing rail, without notifying CME until hours before listing.
variant-perceptions
  • The 0.5% base is the model's single largest sensitivity, applying to the entire roughly $5 billion futures-and-options fee base: a 1-point change swings FY2032 operating income by about $0.5 billion and EPS by about $1.00. The bear rate of −1.5% a year is the more internally consistent center when paired with the high-volume scenarios.
  • **The NEX deal's headline rationale — transform the cash and FX business — under-delivered, even though the deal overall was sound.** The cash-markets business (BrokerTec plus EBS) that was NEX's pitch generated $283.7M in FY2025, about 4.4% of revenue, and has been roughly flat for three years against direct price competition from Tradeweb and MarketAxess; listed FX was the only product line down year over year in FY2025.
  • The record is the opposite: CME bought hardest as the price rose, with the pace doubling from $266M in FY2025 to $536M in Q1 2026 and the heaviest month, March 2026, coinciding with the all-time-high stock price of $326, after which the stock declined about 29% to $233.
  • Consensus framing treats CME as having the highest margins in the industry by a wide margin. Cboe earns a 60.4% FY2025 operating margin on its own SPX/VIX network and was roughly level with CME in the most recent quarter, so the wide premium is true versus ICE (+15.2 points) and Nasdaq (+20.5 points) but only +4.5 points versus Cboe.
