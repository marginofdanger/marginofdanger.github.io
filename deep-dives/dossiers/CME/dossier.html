1. Business description, model & unit economics

CME Group runs the largest futures and options exchange and clearing house in the world. It earns a small fee each time a futures or options contract trades and clears, plus recurring subscription revenue for the market data those markets generate. The whole business is one regulated network: traders bring orders to the CME Globex electronic order book, CME Clearing stands as central counterparty to both sides of every trade, and the depth of open interest concentrated in CME's flagship contracts is what keeps the next trader coming. Management reports a single operating and single reportable segment, so there is no segment P&L; all unit economics come from how revenue disaggregates (10-K FY2025, Note 3).

What drives revenue. Revenue is volume times rate per contract. In FY2025, clearing and transaction fees were $5,281M of $6,521M total revenue, or 81%, and within that line the futures-and-options fee equals average daily volume times trading days times the average rate per contract. FY2025 ran 7,060M total contracts over 251 trading days at a blended rate of $0.696 per contract (10-K FY2025, MD&A volume tables). Volume is overwhelmingly the driver of growth, not price: management decomposed the FY2025 fee increase of $290M into $261M from higher contract volume and $28.5M from a higher average rate per contract, so roughly 90% of fee growth was volume (10-K FY2025, MD&A). The rate per contract drifts down over time, from $0.700 in FY2023 to $0.696 in FY2025 and $0.652 in Q1 2026, because volume-tiered pricing lowers the per-contract rate as a customer trades more, and because lower-priced micro-sized contracts keep growing as a share of the mix (10-K FY2025; 10-Q Q1 2026 MD&A). A record-revenue quarter therefore coincides with a declining blended rate, which is what Q1 2026 showed: clearing and transaction fees rose 15.3% while the blended rate declined 6.3%.

The demand-driver tree — strip the "secular demand for risk management" framing and the exchange reduces to one measurable exposure: aggregate contract volume (ADV) set by the volatility regime and the rate cycle, priced at a per-contract rate that falls as volume rises. About 81% of revenue is that cyclical transaction-volume leg and only the ~12% market-data annuity is recurring. The last two years' growth over-indexed to the highest-amplitude, most volatility-driven lines: physical commodities plus FX drove ~42% of it on 31% of revenue.

The per-contract economics drive the rest. The rate per contract spans a 3x range across lines, from $0.48 in interest rates (the cheapest unit but the highest volume, so the largest revenue line) to about $1.43 in metals and agriculture. The only variable cost is the index-licensing revenue share, roughly $0.05 per contract blended, so the next contract on an existing line earns about $0.65 of its $0.70 rate as incremental operating income, a 92% contribution margin. That is the arithmetic source of the 75% to 90% incremental operating margins the income statement shows. The dispersion in rate per contract also explains why physical commodities, 20% of futures volume, generate 35% of clearing and transaction-fee revenue: their per-contract rate is several times the interest-rate and equity-index rate [deck: 2026-02-12 investor presentation, slide 16].

Three lines are economically distinct from the per-contract derivatives model. Cash markets (BrokerTec for cash U.S. Treasuries, European and U.K. government bonds and repo; EBS for spot foreign exchange and spot precious metals) are priced on notional value, not per contract, and face direct price competition; they came in with the 2018 NEX acquisition and have been roughly flat at $284M for three years. Interest rate swap clearing is a cleared-only OTC fee. Market data is recurring subscription revenue, billed per device or subscriber, with 32 consecutive quarters of year-over-year growth, about 53% earned outside the United States and roughly 30% via the two largest resellers (10-K FY2025, MD&A).

Cost curve and operating leverage. CME has no cost of goods, so there is no gross-margin line; the profitability ladder runs operating margin to net margin to free-cash-flow margin. The defining feature is a nearly fixed cost base. Total expenses were $2,280M in FY2019 and $2,291M in FY2025, essentially flat in dollars, while revenue grew about $1.65 billion over the same six years (financial-historian workpaper). That fixed base produces incremental operating margins of 75% to 90% in normal years (FY2023 75%, FY2024 90%, FY2025 76%). The one material variable cost is "licensing and other fee agreements" of $371M in FY2025, the revenue share paid mainly to S&P Dow Jones Indices for the equity-index franchises, which scales with the licensed product lines. The other large expense lines — compensation of $907M, technology of $283M, and purchased-intangible amortization of $223M — are largely fixed. The cost base is not literally frozen: FY2025 expenses rose about 4% on headcount and Google Cloud spend, and Q1 2026 core operating expense ran up about 7% year over year on variable compensation. The point is that it grows far slower than revenue, which is what converts volume into operating income. Capital intensity is minimal: capital expenditure averaged 2.0% of revenue over the decade and is now about 1.3%, below depreciation in most years, so CME grows with almost no incremental tangible capital (financial-historian workpaper).

Concentration. One clearing firm accounted for 12% of clearing and transaction-fee revenue in 2025, up from 10% in 2024 with none above 10% in 2023, and the two largest resellers for about 30% of market-data revenue (10-K FY2025, Note 2). The concentration at the clearing-firm layer stepped up over two years, which bears watching; management's stated mitigation is that if a clearing firm withdrew, the underlying customer flow would route to another firm because the end user wants CME's liquidity regardless of which intermediary clears the trade, so the economic exposure is smaller than the 12% headline.

Why a formal input-cost unit-economics table cannot be built. A table with input cost per unit and gross profit per unit reconciled to a reported gross margin cannot be built, because CME has no per-unit input cost — its "cost of production" is a shared fixed platform, not a bill of materials, and it reports no per-line cost. The economically meaningful version is the rate-per-contract column in the product-line table above, which decomposes the per-trade price the business actually realizes, paired with the 92% incremental-contribution arithmetic on the cost side. A per-contract cost allocation across lines would be arbitrary, so the operating-leverage figures are the honest substitute.

2. Key technologies & technical differentiators

CME is not primarily a technology company. Its edge is a self-reinforcing liquidity and open-interest network concentrated in a handful of flagship contracts, monetized through the capital and margin efficiency that network makes possible. Technology is the rails that network runs on, plus a small number of hard-to-replicate assets. CME's own 10-K lists the competitive factors in order — brand and reputation, clearing and settlement, depth and liquidity, capital and margin efficiencies, product breadth — with "technological capability and innovation" near the bottom; the durable edge is the concentrated open interest, not the matching engine.

CME Globex is the central-limit-order-book matching engine, the venue for 93% of CME's contract volume in 2025. It accepts orders through an open API and a binary protocol (iLink 3), matches them in roughly 150 microseconds, and disseminates market data through MDP 3.0 [web: Databento/Network World, accessed 2026-06-24]. The mechanism is fast but not uniquely so. FMX launched in 2024 with what trade press called a "state-of-the-art trading system," and ICE, Cboe, Deutsche Börse and Nasdaq all run low-latency engines. What is hard is making a new engine matter: liquidity providers co-locate next to the engine that already has the order flow, and order flow concentrates where the liquidity already is. The engine is a commodity; the open interest sitting on it is not.

CME Clearing is the integrated central counterparty that novates every trade, becoming buyer to every seller and seller to every buyer, and moving roughly $6.7 billion a day in variation margin. Because CME both lists and clears, it can offer netting and portfolio offsets a stand-alone exchange or stand-alone clearer cannot, which is the source of the capital-efficiency advantage. A new clearer must amass enough open interest for its netting to beat CME's — the same chicken-and-egg problem as the engine, but harder, because clearing also requires a default waterfall, a guaranty fund, a systemically-important-utility designation, and regulatory trust built over decades. CME is a designated systemically important financial market utility and systemically important derivatives clearing organization, under additional CFTC and Federal Reserve oversight.

SPAN and SPAN 2 are CME's portfolio value-at-risk margin methodology, and SPAN is not only CME's internal tool. SPAN is the official performance-bond mechanism of more than 50 registered exchanges, clearing organizations and regulators worldwide — a de-facto standard a rival must also use, which CME licenses to its own competitors [web: CME/CFTC, accessed 2026-06-24]. Replicating SPAN's adoption would require not a better model (several exist) but unwinding 35-plus years of embedded back-office and regulator-accepted plumbing. SPAN 2, a full value-at-risk replacement, is rolled out to two of six asset classes so far, which is the main execution risk attached to it.

The cross-margining web lets a clearing firm offset positions held at two different clearing houses, reducing the margin it posts. CME has live arrangements with FICC (the Government Securities Division of DTCC) and OCC; in April 2026 CME received approval to extend the FICC cross-margin to end-user clients, where the house-side program already delivers average daily savings just over $1 billion and a maximum near $1.5 billion, with client-book savings management estimates at "upward of 80%" (Q1 2026 transcript). Each link is a bilateral, regulator-approved integration between two systemically-important clearers, years of work apiece, and the web is cumulative: every added offset deepens the capital-efficiency advantage and raises the bar for a single-clearer challenger.

The Google Cloud migration is the largest stated technology shift: a ten-year partnership signed in 2021 to move clearing and market-data applications to the cloud first, then the live markets to a new Google Cloud private-cloud region and co-location facility in Aurora, Illinois. The clearing and data phase completed only by the end of Q1 2026, roughly five years, so the markets-in-the-cloud phase should be assumed to take years more; a Dallas testing environment opens in summer 2026 and the first two agricultural products migrate by the end of 2026 (Q1 2026 transcript). Running a sub-150-microsecond, zero-downtime matching and clearing system in a public-cloud region is unproven at this scale — no peer derivatives exchange runs its live core markets in a hyperscaler cloud — which makes this the single biggest technology execution risk in the forward case. The deck dates the cost J-curve: 2025 was the final year of four years of incremental net-cash migration cost, and 2026 is expected to turn net-positive [deck: 2026-02-12 investor presentation, slide 19].

CME Term SOFR is the benchmark-administration franchise, the only SOFR term rate endorsed by the Alternative Reference Rates Committee and the forward-looking term rate endorsed by the Federal Reserve Board. That official-sector endorsement is the moat — a competitor cannot simply publish a rival term rate and have it accepted — and it feeds licensing revenue inside the market-data line while reinforcing the SOFR-futures franchise. Two further technologies are early-stage and unproven, so any forward narrative resting on them is speculative: a tokenized-cash "Universal Ledger" with Google and a planned CME stablecoin (early-stage, regulatory-gated), and CMESC, a new SEC-approved securities clearing house for cash Treasuries and repo launching later in 2026 into a market FICC dominates, explicitly flagged in the 10-K as "new to our business."

A real instrument-level substitution threat sits at the retail edge. Perpetual futures on crypto-native venues never expire and run at high leverage with auto-liquidation; in June 2026 the CFTC approved a Bitcoin perpetual for Kalshi, and the S&P Dow Jones Indices JV that CME partly owns licensed an S&P 500 perpetual to a venue on the Hyperliquid blockchain. Management's rebuttal is that perpetuals do not serve hedgers (no cash-futures convergence, funding-rate erosion) and that CME's business is 85% to 90% institutional, so the threat is to a few percent of retail-adjacent volume, not the core. The threat is real, legally contested, and the one thing management spent the most energy on in mid-2026; it is sized in Section 18.

3. Competitive advantages & moat assessment

Assessment. CME's advantage is real and sits close to the structural end of the scale, closer to Visa and Mastercard network economics than to an advantage that is eroding, but it is not uniform across the business and one public peer earns comparable economics in its own protected niche. The advantage is a self-reinforcing liquidity and open-interest network in a handful of flagship contracts — principally U.S. Treasury and SOFR interest-rate futures and the E-mini equity-index complex — amplified by a cross-product margin offset that a single-product entrant structurally cannot replicate on day one. The margin evidence is unambiguous and points the opposite way from a moat being competed away: CME's net-revenue operating margin (64.9% FY2025, a record 69.7% in Q1 2026) is the highest of the four listed exchange peers, and its premium over ICE widened from 3.8 points in 2020 to 15.2 points in 2025, while over Nasdaq it widened from 16.3 to 20.5 points. CME's own operating margin rose 10.9 points over 2020 to 2025; ICE's was essentially flat. The business grows on almost no incremental tangible capital (capex 1.3% of revenue, the lowest of the group), so the cash returns on capital actually employed are extraordinary; the conventional ROIC of about 12% understates this because invested capital is dominated by $28 billion of CBOT, NYMEX and NEX acquisition goodwill and intangibles, not by reinvestment the business needs to defend itself.

Three things keep this from being a pure Visa or Mastercard analogue. Cboe earns a 60.4% FY2025 operating margin on its own exclusive SPX and VIX index-options franchise, within 4.5 points of CME at the full-year level and roughly level in the most recent quarter, and it actually out-margined CME in 2020, so "highest margin in the industry by a wide margin" is true versus ICE and Nasdaq but only narrowly true versus Cboe. Second, the cash-markets line (BrokerTec and EBS, about 4.4% of revenue) has no such moat: it competes on price against Tradeweb and MarketAxess, has been flat for three years, and is the one place CME is a price-taker. Third, the flagship network rests on a real dependency: the liquidity self-reinforces because hedgers need cash-futures convergence, and a venue that captured the hedger base could in principle fracture it. The clearest live test of that — FMX, the best-funded attack on record — has so far failed.

Standalone financial evidence. The returns are the strongest support. Operating margin rose from a 53.2% trough in FY2019 (the NEX integration year) to 64.9% in FY2025, and to a record 69.7% in Q1 2026 (financial-historian workpaper). Free-cash-flow margin was 64.3% in FY2025. Reported ROIC rose from roughly 7% to 8% in 2016 to 2020 to 11.7% in FY2025, but that figure is depressed by acquisition accounting: invested capital includes a $17.2 billion indefinite-lived "trading products" intangible and $10.5 billion of goodwill. On a tangible-capital basis, stripping the acquired intangibles and goodwill, the business earns several-hundred-percent returns on the cash actually tied up, because it grows on essentially no incremental tangible capital — capital expenditure is about 1.3% of revenue and below depreciation (financial-historian workpaper, ROIC footnote). The pricing-power test is partly passed and partly ambiguous: the rate per contract drifts down over time, which on its face is not pricing power, but that decline is a deliberate consequence of volume-tiering that buys volume, and CME implemented a fee increase effective February 2025, so realized economics improve even as the headline per-contract rate falls.

ROIC definition. NOPAT = operating income × (1 − 24% normalized tax rate). Invested capital = total debt + shareholders' equity − cash, excluding the $159.7 billion pass-through clearing collateral on both sides. Cost-of-capital reference: an investment-grade exchange with ~$3.4B of fixed-rate debt at a ~4.0% weighted coupon and low-beta equity earns a WACC in the high-single digits (~8–9%), used as a business-quality hurdle, not a valuation.

Management claims: the breadth of the product base, the capital efficiencies of clearing across asset classes, and the network of liquidity (10-K FY2025 business section). Management quantifies the moat's cash value as average daily margin savings of about $72 billion in FY2025, rising above $85 billion a day in Q1 2026, and describes the offsets as "a huge part of our defensive moat" with switching costs that are "very, very high." These claims are consistent with the financial evidence.

External sources: trade press places CME near 90% of U.S. Treasury futures and roughly 35% to 40% of global exchange-traded derivatives volume, and characterizes FMX as failing to gain meaningful share. The one place external data cuts against what management says is cash Treasuries, where Tradeweb's government-bond volume grew while BrokerTec's shrank — but that is the price-competitive cash line, not the cleared-derivatives business. No local sell-side coverage exists for CME, so the external view rests on trade press and peer filings rather than a bank-research consensus.

Ground-up view: a futures exchange plus clearing house is close to a natural monopoly per contract because of a liquidity externality — the value of trading a contract rises with the number of others trading the same contract — and open interest cannot be moved without a coordinated migration of the whole pool. That produces winner-take-most outcomes per product. The barriers, ranked by how much each does the work: the open-interest network effect (binding); the cross-product margin efficiency, which is a switching cost because leaving CME for one product forfeits the offset against the others; regulatory status, which raises the entry cost but does not by itself produce the pricing power; and scale, a consequence of the near-fixed cost base rather than a separate moat. The exposed lines are the ones where liquidity is not concentrated at CME: cash markets and listed FX, the one product line that declined year over year in FY2025.

4. Competitive landscape & market sizing

Listed derivatives are not a single global market; they are a set of currency- and asset-specific liquidity pools, each tending toward a single dominant venue because of the open-interest network effect. The right unit of analysis is the pool, not "global exchange-traded derivatives." CME is the scale leader in every listed-derivatives line it competes in, and the gap is structurally wide in its two most valuable lines: it runs essentially the entire U.S.-dollar listed interest-rate complex (rates ADV 14.2 million contracts/day FY2025) and owns the deepest U.S. equity-index futures pool (E-mini complex ADV 7.4 million/day). The named exchange peers compete mostly in adjacent currency pools or different instruments, which is why a "five big exchange competitors" framing overstates contestability.

Interest-rate derivatives ($1,719.6M, 26.4% of revenue): effectively a CME monopoly in U.S.-dollar listed rates. The only direct-attack challenger, the bank-backed FMX Futures Exchange, reached roughly 39,000 SOFR-futures contracts a day in Q1 2026 against CME's roughly 3.9 million SOFR-futures ADV — on the order of 1% after 18 months — so the incumbency gap in the flagship line is not closing. ICE and Eurex (Deutsche Börse) lead GBP and EUR listed rates (Euribor, SONIA, Gilt, Bund/Bobl/Schatz), a different currency complex CME does not contest at scale.

Equity-index derivatives ($1,170.4M, 17.9%): CME owns the benchmark E-mini S&P 500, Nasdaq-100 and Russell 2000 futures. Cboe's SPX index options (ADV 3.9 million) sit beside CME's E-mini S&P futures (ADV 4.1 million), but these are different instruments — options on the index versus futures on the index — so apparent parity overstates head-to-head competition; CME owns the futures pool, Cboe owns the listed index-options pool. Eurex and HKEX dominate European and Hong Kong/China index futures, different underlyings.

Energy ($813.2M, 12.5%): the one line where CME shares the field, a two-pole structure. CME (NYMEX) owns the U.S. inland crude benchmark (WTI, share back above 79-80% in Q1 2026) and U.S. natural gas (Henry Hub); ICE owns the global seaborne crude benchmark (Brent) and European gas (TTF). Each is dominant in its own benchmark, and neither is dislodging the other's home benchmark. ICE's energy book grew modestly faster in 2025 (+15% versus +8% ADV), led by natural gas and environmentals rather than WTI-share capture.

Smaller lines: CME is the dominant global grain, oilseed and livestock benchmark in agriculture ($658.1M, the second-fastest two-year revenue CAGR at +13.8%); dominant in U.S. gold, silver and copper in metals ($354.7M, +25.6% two-year revenue CAGR, the fastest-growing line off a small base, with LME leading industrial base metals); and the largest listed FX-futures venue, though listed FX is a small slice of a vast bank-dominated OTC market and was the only line with declining ADV in FY2025.

Cash markets (BrokerTec and EBS, $283.7M, 4.4%): the one line where CME is not the scale leader. BrokerTec's U.S. Treasury cash average daily notional ($96.6 billion) is well below Tradeweb's government-bond ADV (roughly $230 to 250 billion) and shrank 5% in FY2025, while Tradeweb grew double digits. CME's cash strength is repo (an interdealer order book, $365.7 billion daily notional, up 21%) and the cash-futures basis link, not outright cash-Treasury share. FMX's separate cash UST platform reached about 35% of its interdealer niche — another cash-side gain that pressures BrokerTec's small line, not the futures pool.

Share counter-evidence, ranked by structural impact: FMX in USD rates futures is the only direct attack on the most valuable franchise and still sub-scale (the one to watch); Tradeweb and FMX are gaining in low-margin cash Treasuries; ICE energy is growing slightly faster but in a stable two-pole structure; prediction-market and crypto-native venues are a structurally new venue type that could fragment retail flow over time (low near-term impact, watch the slope); and customer concentration is a tail risk: one clearing firm is 12% of clearing and transaction-fee revenue. The durable-advantage interpretation of this structure is in Section 3.

5. Value chain & profit pools

CME is not in a normal supplier-to-customer chain for its core product. A derivatives exchange and clearing house is a two-sided marketplace plus a utility: the input is liquidity supplied by the same population that consumes it, and the scarce resource CME monetizes is its own open-interest pool, which it owns rather than buys. The only upstream suppliers with bargaining power are the index licensors and, newly, Google Cloud; the downstream chokepoints are the clearing firms and the two market-data resellers.

What the decomposition shows. CME captures the fattest margin in the chain and sets its own price on the largest layer, because the per-contract fee is small relative to the capital efficiency the customer gets and the customer cannot take the open interest elsewhere. The only layer that takes a cut of CME's revenue is the index licensor, and CME owns 27% of the biggest one. The $371.0M licensing expense is the single variable cost, paid mostly to S&P Dow Jones Indices, but because CME owns 27% of that JV, a portion of the royalty returns below the line as equity-method earnings ($371.7M of all-JV equity earnings in FY2025, of which S&P/DJI is the largest piece), so the net economic leakage on equity-index products is smaller than the gross royalty. The collateral layer is a $5.3 billion gross flow that nets to about $411M and is a pass-through, not a profit pool CME owns: reading the $5.3 billion as CME income overstates the rate-sensitive earnings by roughly 13x.

The binding constraint is the liquidity and open-interest network effect, not any physical capacity or input supply; regulatory status reinforces it but is secondary. There is no upstream capacity bottleneck — capex is 1.3% of revenue and below depreciation. The scarce resource is open interest, and CME holds it: U.S. Treasury open interest hit an all-time high of 36.3 million contracts in Q1 2026. A competitor cannot replicate the capital-efficiency offset (about $85 billion a day in margin savings) unless it also holds the offsetting open interest, which the network effect prevents.

Customer-side economics. The customer's return on buying CME's product is the capital efficiency from clearing and cross-margining, not a payback on capital equipment. On the new FICC end-client cross-margin program, client-book savings can run "upward of 80%" of the margin that would otherwise be posted separately. The fee is a rounding error relative to that saving, which is why fee competition does not threaten the core franchise. The customer's saving is realized and recurring, so it does not depend on future utilization the way a capital-equipment purchase does. The revenue-durability risk is not that the customer math breaks; it is that volume is cyclical.

Bargaining-power trajectory and circular flows. The margin migration over six years runs toward CME (operating margin 53.2% in FY2019 to 64.9% in FY2025), and the index licensor has not extracted a rising share, because the licensing expense tracked equity-index volume, not supplier price hikes. The one new upstream threat is that CME's own 27%-owned S&P Dow Jones Indices JV licensed S&P 500 perpetual futures to a Hyperliquid-blockchain venue, a competing rail, without notifying CME until hours before listing. Downstream, the clearing-firm concentration stepped up (one firm to 12% of fee revenue), and some of the largest clearing firms have lobbied to make clearing a not-for-profit utility separable from the exchange — the one structural attack that would break the open-interest lock if it succeeded, and it has not. The FanDuel prediction-markets JV (December 2025) is CME paying for retail distribution by sharing economics with a partner who could, absent the contract, become a competitor. The revenue share is roughly $0.01 per contract, too small to model. CME's chain is not propped up by financing its own demand: the $1 billion Google equity investment ran the other way, a supplier investing in CME.

6. Secular & industry trends

CME's decade growth has been a mid-single-digit volume compounder on a near-fixed cost base. Revenue grew at a 6.8% nine-year compound rate (FY2016 $3,595M to FY2025 $6,521M) and aggregate ADV grew at the same ~6.8%, so long-run growth comes from contract volume on a fixed cost base, with the blended rate roughly flat. On cyclical versus secular, the base rate of growth is durable but the level of any given quarter is heavily cyclical, and Q1 2026 is a record quarter management itself questioned ("Now the question will be, is that sustainable?" — Duffy, Q1 2026 call).

The durable secular drivers, each with its mechanism:

  • Rising U.S. debt and SOFR build-out drive the interest-rate complex (26% of revenue). The mechanism is not debt issuance alone — an analyst correctly noted that Treasury debt tripled from 2007 to 2014 without growing CME volume — but rising futures penetration of the cash Treasury market, which went from about 55% in 2012 to over 121% by 2020 via Ultra futures, tighter ticks and portfolio margining. Penetration has a ceiling: once futures turnover exceeds cash turnover, the tailwind that drove the 2012-2020 outperformance exhausts and the rates line reverts toward issuance growth plus volatility, a materially lower slope than the historical penetration gain implies. Treasury open interest at an all-time high of 36.3 million contracts is the live evidence the rates business is still deepening.
  • Global benchmark adoption of WTI and Henry Hub drives energy (12.5%). Each supply disruption converts more foreign commercial users into permanent hedgers at the deepest pool; energy open interest was up 14% since year-end even as the price spike faded, a stickiness marker, and LNG exports rising from ~15% to 20-25% of U.S. production by 2030 is a structural tailwind for Henry Hub [deck: 2026-02-12 investor presentation, slide 23].
  • Micro-sized contracts broaden the customer base across equity index, metals and energy. Micro-product ADV grew 32% in 2025. This is structurally double-edged: it broadens the customer base but micros clear at a much lower rate (about $0.52 per micro energy contract versus the blended $0.65), so the secular broadening dilutes the blended rate even as it lifts contract counts.
  • Recurring market-data subscription and benchmark licensing drive the most defensible line (12.3%): 32 consecutive quarters of year-over-year growth, recurring, sticky, IP-protected, about 53% earned outside the U.S., not volume-cyclical.
  • Retail acquisition is early-stage option value. The retail base roughly tripled in a decade (+12% CAGR, +23% in 2025), and the FanDuel JV brought more than 150,000 new accounts since December 2025, but rate capture is tiny, so it adds almost no revenue today [deck: 2026-02-12 investor presentation, slide 11].

Share math. CME's aggregate ADV grew 6.0% in FY2025 against global futures volume up 8.1% [web: fia.org, accessed 2026-06-24], implying a small raw-contract share give-back. Contract counts are a poor unit for that comparison: total exchange-traded-derivatives volume fell about 45% in 2025 on a collapse in equity options, largely NSE India micro contracts, which CME does not serve, and an NSE micro option and a CME 10-year Treasury future are orders of magnitude apart in notional. On the revenue-relevant basis, CME's clearing-and-transaction-fee revenue grew 6% in FY2025 and 15% in Q1 2026, and WTI share rose back toward 80% in the Q1 stress, so CME is roughly holding share in its core franchises.

Cyclical versus secular. Q1 2026 is a cyclical peak on a secular trend. Aggregate ADV reached 36.2 million contracts a day, up 22% year over year, with the surge concentrated in the most volatility-sensitive lines: metals revenue up 67% on a silver and gold safe-haven surge (silver moving from roughly $50 to $118 to $86), energy revenue up 22% on geopolitical disruption, while the calmer lines (equity index +4%, agriculture +2%) sat near the secular base. Management's own flags are explicit: Duffy questioned the durability of the metals and energy surge, and Fitzpatrick noted "softer activity so far here in April." Strip the cycle and the durable run-rate is roughly the decade base — mid-single-digit volume-driven revenue growth plus the ~8% market-data annuity — so a model should anchor on the secular base and treat the volatility lift as cycle that reverts. The KPIs that would show first if the secular story were breaking, in order: open interest by complex (positions held, not churn); the rate-per-contract mix versus contract count; the 32-quarter market-data streak; and WTI and U.S.-rates share.

7. Critical points

  1. The business is a volume story on a fixed cost base. Total expenses were flat in dollars from FY2019 to FY2025 (roughly $2.28 billion to $2.29 billion) while revenue grew about $1.65 billion, producing incremental operating margins of 75% to 90% and lifting operating margin from a 53.2% trough to 64.9%, then to a record 69.7% in Q1 2026. The next contract earns about 92% of its rate as incremental operating income, which is the arithmetic source of everything that follows: returns, free cash flow, and the model's sensitivity to cost growth.

  2. Q1 2026 is a sharp positive inflection that FY2025 figures understate, and it is partly cyclical. Revenue rose 14.5% year over year, aggregate ADV reached a record 36.2 million contracts a day (+22%, not the +37% that is the energy line alone), and metals revenue rose 67% on a safe-haven surge. Management flagged softer April activity. The model treats the surge as a cyclical high, not a baseline.

  3. The rate per contract falls as volume rises, so record revenue comes with a declining blended rate. The blended futures-and-options rate went from $0.700 in FY2023 to $0.696 in FY2025 to $0.652 in Q1 2026, down 6.3%, as volume-tiering and micro-contract mix grew with the volume itself. The two are coupled, which is the model's largest sensitivity and the reason high-volume scenarios cannot assume a stable rate (Section 8 and Section 17).

  4. Interest rates are the largest line and the most secularly supported, but their durability is the central question. Interest rates are 26.4% of revenue with Treasury open interest at an all-time high, yet rates revenue decelerated to +3.6% in FY2025 from +6.9% in FY2024, and the secular driver is futures penetration of the cash market — already over 100% — which has a ceiling. The base case's growth rests on this line.

  5. The moat is real, durable, and its premium over scale peers is widening. Operating margins near 65% and tangible-capital returns in the hundreds of percent are consistent with a structural liquidity-network advantage, and CME's margin premium over ICE widened from 3.8 to 15.2 points over five years while ICE's margin was flat. The one qualifier is Cboe, which earns a comparable 60.4% margin on its own SPX/VIX niche.

  6. CME out-margins its peers but is the slowest forward EPS compounder in its quality cohort, by choice. Consensus EPS growth of about 7% a year through 2028 is the lowest of a 15-name exchange-and-data reference class whose median is roughly 12%, because CME returns essentially 100% of free cash as dividends and only began buying back stock in FY2025, forgoing the 2 to 4 points of EPS growth peers manufacture from buybacks. The quality is real and is being paid out, not compounded.

  7. CME is a disciplined, low-velocity acquirer with zero goodwill impairment in its history. Goodwill of $10,515M has only ever moved by foreign-currency translation and the OSTTRA divestiture, never a write-down. The one large deal in the window (NEX, 2018) hit its $200M cost-synergy target on schedule; the OSTTRA JV was monetized twice for about $707M of gains; and CME walked from a contested 2014 auction (GFI) and collected a break fee rather than overpay. (See Sections 9 and 11.)

8. Variant perceptions

Each item below is a point where the evidence cuts against a clean view of the business, with the quantification that sizes it.

The 2026 volume steps fade a volatility peak rather than extrapolate it. The model steps several product lines up in FY2026 to capture the booked Q1 run-rate, then fades the most cyclical lines hardest: metals from a Q1 of +130% to a +28% full-year base, energy from +37% to +13%, rates from +24% to +13%. Each of these 2026 steps holds up, because the base is expressed against the FY2025 average and so implies a give-back of roughly two-thirds to four-fifths of the spike through the back half of 2026. The history is that energy and metals volumes spike then return to trend after sentiment events, and the base case respects that.

Falling rate per contract is coupled to the volume it buys, so pairing high volume with a shallow rate decline is internally optimistic. The base case assumes the rate per contract drifts down only 0.5% a year while volume steps up. But Q1 2026 already showed the rate down 6.3% as micro mix surged with the volume. The mechanism, volume-tiering, is the same one that drives the volume, so the two are not independent. The 0.5% base is the model's single largest sensitivity, applying to the entire roughly $5 billion futures-and-options fee base: a 1-point change swings FY2032 operating income by about $0.5 billion and EPS by about $1.00. The bear rate of −1.5% a year is the more internally consistent center when paired with the high-volume scenarios.

The NEX deal's headline rationale — transform the cash and FX business — under-delivered, even though the deal overall was sound. The cash-markets business (BrokerTec plus EBS) that was NEX's pitch generated $283.7M in FY2025, about 4.4% of revenue, and has been roughly flat for three years against direct price competition from Tradeweb and MarketAxess; listed FX was the only product line down year over year in FY2025. Scored on the cash-markets pitch, a roughly $5.6 billion deal bought a $284M flat revenue line. Scored on what it actually delivered, namely a deeper Treasury and repo footprint, the recurring market-data add, and the Term SOFR benchmark business, it was sound. The variant perception is that the part management sold the deal on is the part that disappointed.

The buyback is mislabeled and, so far, mistimed. Management calls the repurchase program "opportunistic." The record is the opposite: CME bought hardest as the price rose, with the pace doubling from $266M in FY2025 to $536M in Q1 2026 and the heaviest month, March 2026, coinciding with the all-time-high stock price of $326, after which the stock declined about 29% to $233. The CFO confirmed the size is governed by a schedule to deploy the $1.55 billion OSTTRA sale proceeds, not by price. The mistiming is small in dollars (about $100M of unrealized markdown on one quarter's repurchases) but the program should be modeled as mechanical proceeds-recycling with no timing skill, not as an opportunistic value lever. The model's net-share-reduction assumption rests on weak ground on the same basis: CME has never sustained net share shrinkage for a full year, and the large dividend competes for the same cash.

CME is the highest-margin exchange, but not by a wide margin everywhere. Consensus framing treats CME as having the highest margins in the industry by a wide margin. Cboe earns a 60.4% FY2025 operating margin on its own SPX/VIX network and was roughly level with CME in the most recent quarter, so the wide premium is true versus ICE (+15.2 points) and Nasdaq (+20.5 points) but only +4.5 points versus Cboe. This does not change the moat conclusion: the same kind of moat can be built in a different niche.

10. Management, incentives & capital allocation

Leadership. Terrence Duffy, 67, has been CEO since 2016, on the board since 1995, and a CME exchange member since 1981 — a career floor-trader and exchange insider, not a professional-manager import. Lynne Fitzpatrick, 47, became President and CFO in November 2024 (CFO since April 2023), an internal promotion over 18 years and the most visible succession signal. The executive ranks are overwhelmingly internal-promote and long-tenured. The one structural risk is key-person concentration on Duffy, which the 2026 proxy and 2025 10-K both flag as a board focus with no named successor disclosed.

Compensation design. CEO total compensation was $23.4M in FY2025, about 88% variable, roughly 5x the next executive. The annual bonus runs on a single metric, Cash Earnings — a near-EBITDA distributable-cash measure that is also the number setting the dividend — so management is paid on the same cash pool it distributes. Long-term equity is 50% time-vested and 50% performance shares, measured against relative total shareholder return versus the S&P 500. The metric has real range (it paid 44% of target once and 200% most recently), but because it measures return versus the broad S&P 500 rather than exchange peers, it paid 200% in the very years CME's total return lagged ICE, Nasdaq and Cboe. From the Sept 2025 grant the metric adds an absolute net-income-margin component and caps the relative-TSR portion if absolute return is negative, both made in response to investor feedback. The bonus rewards an absolute cash pool with no per-share or cost-of-capital adjustment, but three facts pull the realized outcome toward per-share alignment: the share count is near-flat, the same number sets the dividend, and CME is barely acquisitive. Duffy's directly held shares declined from about 99,000 in 2018 to about 56,000 in 2026 despite more than $130 million of cumulative pay, so alignment rests on annual equity grants rather than a large permanent stake.

The 10-year capital-allocation record. Over FY2016 to FY2025 CME generated $28.2 billion of operating cash flow, spent just $1.2 billion (4.3%) on capex, paid out $25.3 billion in dividends (89.8% of operating cash flow), and bought back nothing until FY2025. CME pays out nearly all of its cash flow as dividends; per-share value has compounded through operating growth, not capital deployment.

11. M&A, divestitures & deal track record

Assessment. CME is a disciplined, deliberate, low-velocity acquirer, closer to "rarely does big deals, and the ones it does are absorbed cleanly" than to a serial roll-up. The cleanest deal-quality signal is zero goodwill impairment across the entire history. The one real criticism is that NEX's headline rationale under-delivered. This is the variant perception, not the headline grade.

12. Capitalization & balance sheet

The pass-through balance sheet. Dilution. Stock-based compensation is small and stable, $66M in FY2016 to $95M in FY2025, about 1.5% of revenue. On a like-for-like adjusted basis the model and consensus are close, so the gap is not a variant view. Estimates rose about 5% over the year while the stock declined 14.7% year to date, so the forward multiple compressed from about 27x to 19x.

17. Forward model summary

The model is a driver-based, bottom-up operating model: revenue is built per product line as average daily volume × trading days × rate per contract for the six futures-and-options lines, plus separately driven recurring and notional lines (cash markets, IRS clearing, market data, other), down to free cash flow. It contains no valuation, no multiples, and no discounting. Year-0 is FY2025, tied directly to the financial history. Bear, base, and bull are defined as deltas on drivers, not as haircuts to revenue or margin. The model fades the Q1 2026 volume spike rather than holding it: the per-line FY2026 steps de-rate the most cyclical lines hardest (metals from +130% to +28%, energy from +37% to +13%, rates from +24% to +13%), landing base FY2026 revenue at $7,122M, +9.2%, just above the BBG consensus of +8.2%.

The five most consequential drivers, by terminal-year swing in FY2032 operating income and EPS: the rate per contract (±$478M / ±$1.00, because it applies to the whole roughly $5 billion futures-and-options base), core operating expense growth (±$320M / ±$0.70), interest-rate ADV (±$300M / ±$0.66), market-data revenue growth (±$290M / ±$0.64), and equity-index ADV (±$150M / ±$0.33). The rate per contract is the largest and most fragile assumption because it is coupled to the volume steps — the volume-tiering that drives volume is the same mechanism that lowers the rate — so the model's high-volume scenarios are internally optimistic if they keep the rate decline shallow. The net collateral-reinvestment line, despite its $5 billion gross size, swings FY2032 operating income by $0 (it sits below the line) and EPS by only about $0.23, so the warning not to treat the gross figure as CME income is borne out: it is a small, rate-sensitive tail, not a profit driver. The weak assumptions cluster on the small or below-the-line items (cash markets, IRS clearing, intangible run-off, equity-method base, share count) plus the rate-per-contract drift; where the model output depends on the rate-per-contract drift, the bear of −1.5% is the more internally consistent center, and where it depends on net share reduction, the bear of flat shares is the history-consistent center.

18. Risks

The 10-K risk-factor year-over-year changes are the best signal, computed by hand from the archived filings (10K-redlines does not cover CME). The FY2025 additions are concrete: the CyrusOne data-center cooling failure that forced a one-day market halt (disclosed twice); prediction and event-contract legal-status risk; a dedicated AI risk factor; and a reworded competitor set naming "crypto and prediction market platforms" for the first time.

  1. Volume reverts when volatility declines. The whole revenue base is volume × rate, and Q1 2026's record volume was driven by Fed-policy, geopolitical, and safe-haven volatility that CME's own risk language says is transient. A 10% volume decline across the derivatives complex is roughly $480-520M of revenue, nearly all of it operating income. The bear case (calm markets) produces a −0.8% EPS CAGR to FY2032. Leading indicator: monthly ADV by product line, especially energy and metals; management already flagged a softer April 2026.

  2. The rate per contract declines faster than modeled as micro mix grows. The model's largest sensitivity. Q1 2026 already showed the blended rate down 6.3% as micro contracts surged with the volume. Exposure: the entire roughly $5 billion futures-and-options fee base; ±$1.00 in FY2032 EPS for the modeled range. Leading indicator: the disclosed blended rate per contract each quarter against ADV.

  3. A credible new rates-futures entrant. The bank-backed FMX Futures Exchange targets CME's most valuable line (interest rates, 26.4% of revenue) directly. The network-effect moat has repelled it so far — FMX's SOFR-futures ADV is about 1% of CME's after 18 months — but it is the one to watch. Leading indicator: open interest and ADV migration in Treasury and SOFR futures.

  4. Erosion of the cross-margining capital-efficiency advantage. The roughly $72-85 billion a day of margin savings depends partly on CME cross-margining with FICC, which CME's own CEO calls "critically important" and locates the value "with DTCC and the FICC clearing organization." As the SEC's expanded Treasury-clearing mandate brings FICC, DTCC, ICE and LCH alternatives online, the offset exclusivity could narrow. Exposure: the durability of the interest-rate moat. Leading indicator: SEC rulemaking on cross-margin access for competing clearers; FICC/DTCC Treasury-clearing volumes; CMESC adoption.

  5. The collateral-reinvestment line fades as the Fed cuts. Net retained income of about $411M is balance × short-rate spread; deeper cuts compress it. It sits below operating income, so it moves EPS, not operating income, by roughly ±$0.23 in FY2032. Leading indicator: the short-rate path and the disclosed cash-collateral balance and basis-point spread each quarter.

  6. Operational and cyber resilience is now a realized risk. The November 27, 2025 CyrusOne cooling failure (human error) forced CME to halt its markets and reopen the next day delayed — the first disclosed market halt of this kind in the 10-K window. The Google Cloud migration adds a new third-party dependency the 10-K says "may increase our risk of a cyber attack." A multi-day or recurring outage damages the liquidity network that is the moat. Leading indicator: any further halts; Google Cloud migration milestones and slippage; cyber-incident 8-Ks.

  7. Customer and channel concentration. One clearing firm is 12% of clearing and transaction-fee revenue (up from 10%), two resellers are about 30% of market-data revenue, and FanDuel filed for its own FCM, raising a channel-conflict question. Mitigation: customer flow re-routes to another firm because the end user wants CME's liquidity, so the economic exposure is smaller than the headline. Leading indicator: the 10-K concentration disclosures year over year.

  8. The cash-markets and FX lines are price-competitive and not protected by the liquidity moat. Cash markets (4.4% of revenue) have been flat for three years against Tradeweb and MarketAxess; FX futures (3.0%) declined in FY2025. Combined ~7% of revenue, so even outright erosion is not thesis-breaking. Leading indicator: cash-markets notional value and FX-futures ADV versus competitors.

  9. The vertical-integration model under slow-burn regulatory pressure. CME's largest clearing firms have lobbied to make clearing a not-for-profit utility separable from the exchange and to move open positions between clearing houses; a financial-transaction tax surfaces periodically. Open-access or fungible-clearing rules would break the margin model the business rests on. No such rule has been adopted in 15-plus years, and none is in force now.

These risks correlate. The single most important correlated pair is rates volume and collateral income: both are driven by Fed policy and rates volatility, so a benign-rate, low-volatility regime simultaneously pulls volume down, lets the rate per contract keep falling, and fades the collateral line. The bear case models that combination. Layered on a record FY2026 comp base, that is the bad-quarter scenario, not a single idiosyncratic event.

20. What we still don't know / monitoring KPIs

These material gaps remain unfilled and would change or strengthen the conclusions named beside each. The first three would most affect a section's conclusion.

  1. No per-product-line cost or profit-pool split exists (Sections 1, 3, 5). CME is a single reportable segment with no cost by line, so line-level margins are not computable; this leaves the dossier unable to confirm that the moat-protected rates line earns a higher margin than the price-competitive cash-markets line, or whether BrokerTec and EBS even cover their fully-loaded cost. Resolve: segment-level disclosure CME does not provide. Monitor: cash-markets revenue against its competitors as the proxy.

  2. The S&P Dow Jones Indices JV's standalone earnings contribution to CME is undisclosed (Sections 5, 17). Only the 27% ownership and the all-JV $371.7M equity-method total are given, so the net economics of the equity-index line (17.9% of revenue, the second-largest) cannot be closed and the model's $340M ongoing equity-method base rests on an estimate. Monitor: the equity-method earnings line each year now that OSTTRA has dropped out.

  3. The cyclical-versus-secular split of the Q1 2026 surge is not closed by history (Sections 6, 17). The 2020 COVID and 2022-23 rates episodes were narrated qualitatively but not reconstructed as before/after natural experiments, so the bear case's calm-markets downside has no fully quantified base rate. Monitor: aggregate ADV by product line versus the FY2025 average; the persistence of energy and metals volume after the catalysts fade; open interest holding while volatility normalizes.

  4. The FMX threat and the cross-margining durability are unsized in dollars (Sections 3, 4, 18). FMX's open interest and revenue are private; no disclosure bounds CME's exclusive cross-margin-offset share if the SEC mandate makes FICC offsets portable. Monitor: open interest and ADV in Treasury and SOFR futures; SEC rulemaking on cross-margin access; FICC/DTCC Treasury-clearing volumes.

  5. CMESC's committed capital and first-year operational-loss tail are unsized (Sections 2, 18). A new securities-clearing line into the SEC Treasury-clearing regime is a new tail risk no workpaper sizes. Monitor: CMESC launch timing, committed guaranty fund, and adoption versus FICC.

  6. The cloud-migration cutover risk is unsized (Sections 2, 18). The ten-year Google Cloud migration is the largest strategic shift and concentrates a systemically important market on a single cloud provider; no workpaper sizes the cutover-window operational or dependency risk or the rollback plan. Monitor: migration milestones (first ag products end-2026) and core operating-expense growth against the ~5% base assumption.

  7. Market-data ARPU cannot be decomposed (Sections 1, 13). Device and subscriber counts are undisclosed, so the 12.3%-of-revenue line is a single growth number with no price-versus-units split, and the ~30% two-reseller concentration cannot be expressed as a unit dependency. Monitor: market-data revenue growth and reseller concentration year over year.

  8. No within-name consensus dispersion and no curated sell-side bear case (Sections 14, 18). The Bloomberg snapshot is mean-only, and OC-Sellside has no CME coverage, so the §14 external range is cross-sectional across companies rather than a contributor band, and a real short report could surface a risk the inventory under-weights. Monitor: a sell-side digest refresh that includes CME.

  9. Insider buy/sell timing is unverified (Section 10). CME Form-4 history is not in the local store, so the management-ownership conclusion is a successive-proxy trend (net selling) only. Monitor: Form-4 filings against the stock price.
