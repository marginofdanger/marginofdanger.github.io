D1 (2026-07-02): SPGI Ratings vs Moody's (MIS): how the two franchises differ — fixed-income category mix, transaction vs recurring, and 10-year growth and margins

Question. How different are the ratings businesses of SPGI (S&P Global Ratings) and MCO (Moody's Investors Service)? How do their revenues break out by the different categories of fixed income (corporate/financial/structured/public finance/sovereign, and transaction vs recurring)? How have their growth and margins compared over the last 10 years? What explains these differences?

S&P Global Ratings and Moody's Investors Service (MIS) rate largely the same universe of debt under the same issuer-pays model, and at the aggregate level they behave like close analogues: both run low-60s operating margins, both compounded revenue near 10% a year through 2016-2021, and both are geared to the same corporate-issuance cycle. The differences are real but second-order, and three of them do most of the work — what each segment actually contains, how issuance-cyclical each book is, and how each discloses its revenue.

Segment scope and size. SPGI's Ratings segment reported $4,724M of revenue in FY2025 (about $4,549M external), against MIS's $4,317M segment and $4,119M external (SPGI 10-K FY2025; MCO 10-K FY2025). The headline 1.10x size gap overstates the ratings-only difference, because roughly $299M of SPGI's segment is Crisil's global research and analytics business, which is not ratings and which Moody's keeps out of MIS entirely (its analytics sit in a separate Moody's Analytics segment). On a pure-ratings basis SPGI's ~$4,250M is only about 1.04x Moody's $4,084M [estimate: SPGI external revenue less ~$299M Crisil research; low confidence, Crisil research is an INR-conversion figure], so the two franchises are far closer in size than the segment totals suggest.

Revenue by fixed-income category. Only Moody's discloses this; SPGI reports nothing below transaction versus non-transaction, so a like-for-like category comparison is impossible from filings. Moody's FY2025 external ratings revenue of $4,084M splits as follows (MCO 10-K FY2025):

SPGI's asset-class mix has to be triangulated from Moody's category shares, adjusted for the two firms' disclosed differences. The estimate: corporate ~49-53% ($2,100-2,250M), financial institutions ~17-19% ($720-810M), public/project/infrastructure ~17-20% ($720-850M, of which public finance and sovereign ~8-11%), and structured ~11-14% ($470-590M) [estimate: Moody's FY2025 shares applied to SPGI's ~$4,250M ratings revenue, tilted higher for S&P's larger government franchise and lower for its structured-share losses; low confidence, no SPGI asset-class revenue is disclosed]. The two mixes rhyme on corporate (about half each) and diverge at the tails: S&P is heavier in public finance and sovereign, lighter in the fastest-growing structured categories.

Transaction versus recurring, and why the headline misleads. SPGI booked 52% transaction / 48% non-transaction in FY2025 ($2,470M / $2,254M); MIS booked 67% / 33% ($2,741M / $1,378M) (SPGI 10-K FY2025; MCO 10-K FY2025). Taken at face value this makes Moody's look 15pp more issuance-sensitive, but the two firms draw the line differently and about 6pp of the gap is definitional. SPGI's non-transaction includes the ~$299M of Crisil research and a $172M royalty from Market Intelligence, both of which sit outside Moody's MIS transaction/recurring base. Strip them and SPGI runs roughly 58% / 42% against Moody's 67% / 33%, a genuine ~9pp gap rather than 15pp [estimate: SPGI non-transaction less Crisil research and the royalty]. The residual is real, because S&P monetizes frequent, large issuers more through flat annual relationship fees (booked non-transaction) than Moody's does, and its own billed-issuance measure explicitly excludes frequent-issuer programs (SPGI 10-K FY2025). Both splits are cyclically elevated: each was far more recurring in the weak-issuance year 2023 (SPGI 43% / 57%, MIS 57% / 43%).

Ten-year growth and margins. Over the full decade SPGI Ratings compounded revenue 7.2% a year against MIS's 6.3% external, but that gap is entirely post-2021 — through 2016-2021 the two grew within 0.1pp of each other (~10% each) and SPGI's size lead sat at a stable 3-4% (MCO 10-Ks FY2016-FY2025; SPGI series). SPGI's whole decade premium and its widened ~10% size lead were produced in a single year, 2022, by mix resilience rather than superior secular demand.

The margin ranking reversed over the decade: in 2016 MIS out-margined SPGI, by 2025 the order flipped. The bases differ and must not be mixed — SPGI reports Ratings on GAAP (63.8% FY2025), Moody's reports MIS on an adjusted basis (63.6%). On a common basis SPGI leads by about 1.4pp (both adjusted, ~65% versus 63.6%) to 2.9pp (both GAAP, 63.8% versus ~60.9%), the GAAP wedge reflecting MIS's far larger depreciation and amortization ($87M versus ~$38M) (MCO 10-K FY2025; SPGI Q4 2025 release).

Why MIS is the more cyclical of the two. MIS is 12-15pp more transaction-weighted in every year of the decade, so the 2022 issuance collapse hit it harder: external revenue declined 29% (against SPGI's segment 26%) and adjusted margin declined 10.4pp to a 51.8% trough (against SPGI's GAAP 9.4pp to 54.8%), even though the two firms' transaction-revenue declines were nearly identical (MIS −43%, SPGI −45%) (MCO 10-K FY2022; SPGI series). The difference is pure mix — transaction was 69% of MIS revenue entering 2022 but only 55% of SPGI's. The same asymmetry explains Moody's faster 2025 margin expansion (+350bp versus SPGI's ~+200bp): Moody's had ~11.8pp of trough-to-2025 recovery to make against SPGI's ~9.0pp, so this is mean-reversion off a deeper hole, not a quality inflection.

What explains the differences. Three drivers account for most of the gap. First, the Crisil-versus-ICRA subsidiary asymmetry: SPGI consolidates ~$400M of Crisil at ~26.5% margin while Moody's consolidates only ~$59M of ICRA (MCO 10-K FY2025; Crisil and ICRA results [web]). Crisil contributes roughly half the ~0.9pp/yr growth gap and drags SPGI's reported margin down ~3.4pp; strip both subsidiaries and SPGI's core public-ratings margin (~67.2%) sits about 3pp above Moody's (~64.1%), consistent with S&P's ~10% revenue-scale edge [estimate: ex-subsidiary margins, low confidence; the exact segment placement of Crisil's research business is uncertain]. So the apparent margin parity is manufactured by a subsidiary S&P chose to own, and the scale advantage is there once it is removed. Second, S&P's larger government and public-finance ratings stock — it holds 49.6% of all NRSRO ratings outstanding but only 31.4% excluding government, against Moody's 31.4% / 25.1% — builds a bigger surveillance annuity that cushions its cyclicality (SEC OCR 2025 Staff Report). Third, structured finance cuts the other way: S&P is losing share in the fastest-growing categories (CLO 60.5% to 47.0%, CMBS 38.1% to 13.0% between 2023 and 2025) while Moody's holds or gains, though at ~13-14% of each book this is a growth-vector risk rather than a current base-revenue one.

Two things are not drivers. Pricing is symmetric — both run identical list-price escalation and both face the same margin-diluting jumbo investment-grade mix from 2024-2025 AI and M&A megadeals. And the intersegment royalty flatters Moody's, not S&P: MIS receives $198M (4.6% of segment revenue) versus SPGI Ratings' $172M (3.6%), so the near-100%-margin royalty lifts MIS's reported margin slightly more, the opposite of the common assumption. The genuine adjacency difference is at the parent, not the segment — Moody's has built a nearly co-equal ~$3.6B Moody's Analytics franchise, making it a two-engine credit-and-analytics company, whereas Ratings is one of five SPGI segments at ~30% of revenue.

Current run-rate. Q1 2026 confirms the pattern without inflecting it: SPGI Ratings grew 13% at a 67.8% adjusted margin against MIS's +8% (organic constant-currency +6%) at 66.7% (SPGI 10-Q Q1 2026; MCO 10-Q Q1 2026), with about 2pp of SPGI's lead from favorable currency. Growth leadership flips year to year — MIS grew marginally faster than SPGI in FY2025 (+8.6% versus +8.1% external) — which underscores that within this duopoly the remaining differences are compositional, not competitive.

Moody's line-of-business, transaction/recurring, and margin figures verified against the FY2025 10-K, and the decade series against each year's 10-K; SPGI's asset-class mix, the like-for-like transaction split, and the ex-subsidiary margins are triangulated estimates stated as such, since SPGI discloses no ratings revenue below the transaction/non-transaction line.

D2 (2026-07-02): Capital IQ Pro vs Bloomberg, FactSet and LSEG/Refinitiv: relative size (revenue, seats, headcount), growth, and exposure to AI-driven disruption

Question. How does S&P Global's Capital IQ Pro (Market Intelligence) compare to its competitors Bloomberg, FactSet, and Thomson Reuters (Refinitiv/LSEG)? What is each competitor's size in revenue, subscribers/seats, and headcount? How has their growth compared over the years? How at risk are these desktop/data-terminal businesses from AI-driven disruption?

Capital IQ Pro and its three rivals are differentiated by job, not ranked on one axis, so framing the question as "Capital IQ Pro versus Bloomberg" mistakes complements for substitutes on most banking desks. Bloomberg Terminal is the front-office standard: real-time market data, the deepest fixed-income and FX coverage, and Instant Bloomberg messaging, a closed trader-to-trader network that none of the other three replicate and a primary reason desks pay the seat (costbench, accessed 2026-07-02) [web]. Capital IQ Pro is the fundamentals-and-deals platform: the deepest public and private company data (S&P markets 60 million-plus private companies and 110,000-plus PE/VC funds, a vendor figure third-party comparison sites cut to roughly 24 million tracked entities), M&A precedent screening, SNL sector datasets, and bundled S&P Ratings content no rival can source, though it is "not optimal for real-time market data" (wallstreetprep, accessed 2026-07-02) [web]. FactSet Workstation is the buy-side portfolio-analytics and pitchbook platform, roughly 84% buy-side by revenue, with the best Excel and PowerPoint plug-ins. LSEG Workspace, the Eikon successor whose migration completed in 2025, leads FX and rates, Reuters news, and Datastream macro history back to 1973, positioned as the mid-priced modern alternative.

Bloomberg is far the largest and permanently private, so its revenue is a triangulated range and its headcount an estimate (Revelio Labs, accessed 2026-07-02) [web]. LSEG Data & Analytics ($5,131M [estimate: £3,978M × 1.29 average 2025 GBP/USD]) is slightly larger than SPGI Market Intelligence ($4,902M external), so the two mid-tier players are neck-and-neck, each roughly a third of Bloomberg and about 2.2x FactSet (LSEG 2025 preliminary results, 2026-02-26; SPGI FY2025 10-K; FactSet FY2025 10-K). FactSet is the only pure-play with fully disclosed headcount of 12,800 as of August 2025; SPGI's group of 44,500 and LSEG's 26,000-plus do not break out the desktop division.

The seats cluster far tighter than the revenue, which is the sharpest structural fact: all four reach the same order of magnitude of desks (237,000 to 400,000), yet Bloomberg monetizes each at roughly $37,000 per user against about $14,000 at SPGI, $12,800 at LSEG, and $9,800 and declining at FactSet. The revenue spread is a pricing spread, not a reach spread. The $14,000 SPGI figure overstates the true Capital IQ Pro desktop yield because the Market Intelligence numerator includes Enterprise Solutions and Credit & Risk Solutions revenue unrelated to the desktop, so the real per-seat gap to Bloomberg is wider than $37,000 versus $14,000. Revenue per employee follows the same logic: Bloomberg earns about $667,000 per head against FactSet's $181,000 (a 3.7x gap), with SPGI group at $345,000 and LSEG at $446,000 in between, because FactSet (69% of staff in Asia-Pacific) and SPGI (59% in Asia) run large offshore content-operations workforces while Bloomberg monetizes a software and distribution annuity with fewer, higher-value heads (FactSet FY2025 10-K; SPGI FY2025 10-K). That offshore content base is the cost pool most exposed to AI automation.

Growth over the years: the scaled set is a 5–7% organic-growth oligopoly with no visible share shifts among the incumbents. Two directional signals stand out. FactSet's organic ASV re-accelerated across four quarters, from +5.7% at August 2025 to +7.1% ($2,486M) at May 2026, now above Market Intelligence's roughly 6% pace (FactSet Q3 FY2026, accessed 2026-07-02) [web]; but its user count grew 31% over three years against only 26% revenue, so ASV per user drifted down to about $9,800 as it added cheaper wealth and enterprise seats, which means its seat lead overstates its monetization momentum (FactSet FY2022–FY2025 10-Ks). Bloomberg's terminal count has grown about 1% a year, so its growth is price and enterprise data, not seats. LSEG Data & Analytics turned from flat-to-declining before 2021 to about +5% after the Refinitiv integration, and Moody's Analytics (+9%) is the only scaled analog materially out-growing the pack.

AI-driven disruption risk: the market has priced a specific thesis, that generative AI collapses the terminal's UI layer to a chatbot while LLM platforms aggregate the query habit and squeeze the data vendor from above. Information-services multiples derated about 40% on next-twelve-months earnings since mid-2025, and SPGI declined 21.9% year-to-date to 2026-06-26. SPGI's own 10-K names the mechanism: "Third parties could also use our data with AI tools to create their own insights and potentially supplant our products and services" (SPGI FY2025 10-K, Item 1A), a risk block first added in FY2023 and roughly tripled by FY2025.

The 2026 disclosed data runs the other way. At every disclosing incumbent, customers who adopt AI features grow spend faster, not slower: SPGI's AI-cohort ACV growth is 30% above non-adopters in Market Intelligence (and double in Energy), and FactSet's AI-solution clients grew subscription value 50% faster quarter-over-quarter than the rest of the book (SPGI Q1 2026 call; FactSet Q3 FY2026 [web]). Seats kept rising through the exact derating window, FactSet from 237,324 to more than 247,000. The profit pool sat in the licensed data layer, not the UI, and every incumbent re-exposes that layer through the same architecture, an MCP server or LLM-ready API feeding an LLM copilot: SPGI's Kensho, Bloomberg's ASKB, FactSet's Mercury, and LSEG's Microsoft-managed MCP server (Microsoft, accessed 2026-07-02) [web], differing only in whether the vendor builds the model layer in-house or partners for it. SPGI has already converted AI value into price rather than seats: two clients paid 35–45% renewal uplifts for AI-ready data, and one buy-side client switched to S&P data at a roughly 20% premium after testing the plug-in (SPGI Q1 2026 call).

SPGI's enterprise, non-per-seat pricing insulates its ACV from the mechanical seat channel that most threatens Bloomberg, the only pure per-seat model of the four and thus the most exposed if AI reduces analyst headcount, but only partially. It relocates the exposure to whole-contract value repricing at renewal, where the roughly 12% of Market Intelligence revenue management calls undifferentiated (about $590M) sits, and MI ACV has already softened to about 6% from 6.5–7% across the prior five quarters (SPGI Q1 2026 call). FactSet, historically per-user, is migrating toward the same enterprise model (the majority of its Q3 FY2026 ASV renewed as enterprise agreements or three-year-plus terms, average term extending about 30%), which is revealed preference that the structure is the industry's chosen defense [web]. The quantified AI-native threat, AlphaSense, scaled ARR from $500 million in October 2025 to more than $600 million by Q1 2026 at a $7.5 billion valuation, roughly 40% annualized and about 3x incumbent growth (AlphaSense, accessed 2026-07-02) [web]; but at $600 million ARR it is about 1/22 of Bloomberg and 1/8 of SPGI Market Intelligence, it attacks the UI and workflow layer only, and it still licenses the proprietary, benchmark, and regulated data it does not own. At 40% a year against MI's 6%, reaching MI's current scale would take about nine years. The desktop UI is genuinely exposed to AI; the licensed-data layer where the profit sits is being defended and, in places, repriced upward, and no disclosed KPI at any incumbent yet shows terminal cancellation at scale.

D3 (2026-07-02): S&P Global Ratings growth: new issuance vs refinancing, how predictive the debt maturity walls are, and how the recurring vs non-recurring customer bases differ

Question. How much of S&P Global Ratings' growth is driven by new issuance vs. refinancing of existing debt? How predictive are the debt maturity walls management talks about of actual future ratings growth? How different is the customer base that drives recurring (non-transaction) vs. non-recurring (transaction) revenue in Ratings?

Refinancing versus new money: S&P Global publishes no use-of-proceeds split of billed issuance in any period; it reports the number only by asset class, so the decomposition rests on qualitative attribution plus a single management quantification. The FY2025 10-K maps the drivers by class: tightening spreads drive refinancing in high yield and bank loans, while new money drives investment grade — M&A in 2024, AI and hyperscaler infrastructure debt in 2025 — and new CLOs drive structured finance (SPGI 10-K FY2025, Ratings MD&A). The one hard figure came in Q1 2024, a record issuance quarter: "about 2/3 overall the issuance activity that we saw was related to refinancing," a mix of 2024 maturities plus some 2025 and 2026 pulled forward (SPGI Q1 2024 call). External data corroborates the refinancing-heavy skew — US high-yield refinancing was roughly 75% of high-yield issuance in the first nine months of 2024 and European high yield 63% (debtexplorer.whitecase.com, accessed 2026-07-02) [web]. The pattern across the cycle is consistent: refinancing is the counter-cyclical ballast that dominates the mix in recovery years, while genuinely new money is the swing that produces both booms and busts. Management described the entire 2021 leveraged-finance surge as "entirely due to M&A, LBOs, buybacks and dividends... additive to future financing needs," not a pull-forward of later maturities (SPGI Q3 2021 call). A refinancing-heavy base is durable over a full cycle, because maturing debt must be dealt with and each rolled instrument feeds the surveillance annuity, but it is a low-single-digit-growth floor rather than a growth engine: transaction revenue compounded only 2.3% a year peak-to-peak from 2021 to 2025 ($2,253M to $2,470M) despite two record issuance years, because refinancing volume tracks the stock of outstanding debt rather than outrunning it, and the fee rate per dollar is mix-diluted. New-money investment grade is the lowest fee-rate bucket, so a shift toward it compresses conversion: Q4 2025 billed issuance grew 28% while transaction revenue grew 12%, and the realized rate declined from 5.95bp in 2024 to 5.71bp in 2025 (SPGI Q4 2025 call).

Predictive power of the maturity wall: the wall reliably predicts the multi-year cumulative refinancing need and poorly predicts the annual print. Its year-over-year change moved inside a roughly ±12% band across the 2023-2026 vintages (−1% to +12%), while realized billed issuance swung from about −45% in 2022 to +54% in 2024. For 2025 the wall signaled +4% on next-twelve-month maturities and −1% on three-year maturities; billed issuance printed +11%, the gap filled by M&A and hyperscaler AI-infrastructure debt tied to no maturity (SPGI Q4 2024 call; 10-K FY2025). Three mechanisms break the link from wall to issuance. Pull-forward empties a year's wall into the prior year — full-year 2019 reduced 2020 maturities 15%, and the first nine months of 2020 reduced 2021 maturities 18% (SPGI Q3 2020 call). Rate-driven deferral pushes maturities out to later years when spreads widen. And private or unrated refinancing removes rated volume entirely. The 2022 experiment is decisive: a rolling roughly $12 trillion multi-year wall stood intact, yet transaction revenue declined 45%, because a 425bp rate shock deferred refinancing and 2020-2021 pull-forward had already depleted the 2022 wall. S&P's own studies show the wall is a rolling treadmill, not a draining reservoir — rated corporate debt due through 2024 was near $12 trillion in the 2019 study and due through 2026 near $12 trillion in the 2021 study, essentially unchanged (spglobal.com/ratings, accessed 2026-07-02 [web]). Management itself downgraded the wall from an annuity floor ("$10.2 trillion... not going away," 2018 Investor Day) to "just one of the factors we consider when providing an outlook on billed issuance" (SPGI Q4 2024 call). The live 2026 test already shows the pattern: on a 2026 wall cited as 12% higher than the 2025 wall a year earlier, management guided low-to-mid single-digit billed issuance with a pre-announced negative fourth quarter, yet Q1 2026 billed issuance printed +14%, front-loaded on hyperscaler AI and M&A the wall does not contain (SPGI Q4 2025 and Q1 2026 calls).

Two customer bases: recurring and non-recurring revenue in Ratings are not two customer populations but largely one issuer base billed at different points in the debt lifecycle. Transaction revenue is the issuer at the point of sale of a new rated instrument, recognized at a point in time; non-transaction revenue re-bills that same issuer for surveillance, annual relationship programs, and entity ratings over the instrument's and the entity's life, recognized ratably (SPGI 10-K FY2025, revenue-recognition note). The transaction payer set is effectively a subset of the non-transaction set, which is broader for two reasons: it also bills every issuer with outstanding rated debt or an entity rating that did not come to market this year, and it holds two genuinely different non-issuer payer groups — Crisil's financial-institution research clients (about $299M) and the investor-side RatingsDirect and RatingsXpress subscription base traced through the $172M intersegment royalty Market Intelligence pays Ratings. Those non-issuer legs are only about $471M of the $2,254M non-transaction line, roughly 21% [estimate: residual from disclosed royalty and Crisil-segment revenue]; the other roughly 79% is the same issuers who pay transaction fees. The largest, most active issuers sit mostly outside the transaction bucket, because billed issuance excludes frequent-issuer programs whose fees book in non-transaction, so transaction skews toward first-time and one-off deals (SPGI 10-K FY2025, Ratings MD&A). Non-transaction is less cyclical because it is a function of the stock of outstanding ratings, which turns over slowly, while transaction is a function of the flow of new issuance: in 2022 transaction revenue declined 45% while non-transaction declined 2% (+1% in constant currency), and non-transaction compounded about 5.8% a year from 2016 to 2025. The flywheel links them, because each new rated issue enters the surveillance base and pays annually until maturity, which is why management guides transaction and non-transaction to grow at similar rates in 2026 even as billed issuance decelerates (SPGI Q4 2025 call). The separation is not clean, though: Ratings Evaluation Services sits in non-transaction but is M&A-driven and, in management's words, "a precursor to additional issuance," and the 52/48 split is a booking convention rather than an economic law, because Moody's classifies more of the same activity as transaction, at 67/33 (SPGI Q1 2026 call).
