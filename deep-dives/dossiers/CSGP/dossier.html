1. Business description, model & unit economics

CoStar sells information, workflow software, and advertising inside real estate. The company began in 1987 as a commercial real estate database and now spans commercial data and lease software, the leading CRE listing marketplace, apartment advertising, residential portals in three countries, land listings, hotel benchmarking, 3D digital twins, a business-for-sale marketplace, and a CRE auction platform. The repeated mechanism is the same across all of it: collect or organize scarce property data, draw professional or consumer demand to a product built on that data, and charge the business participant who needs the demand or the data.

CoStar reported $3,247 million of FY2025 revenue across four disclosed buckets: CoStar information and analytics was $1,259 million (38.8%), LoopNet $312 million (9.6%), Other Commercial Real Estate $216 million (6.7%), and Residential Real Estate $1,460 million (45.0%)▪ (FY2025 10-K, Note 3). The Commercial reportable segment was $1,787 million of revenue earning $672 million of Adjusted EBITDA, a 37.6% margin; Residential was nearly as large in revenue but lost $230 million on an Adjusted EBITDA basis▪ (FY2025 10-K, Note 13). The two halves work differently. Commercial is a profitable vertical-information business. Residential is a portfolio holding one proven marketplace (Apartments.com), one large unproven bet (Homes.com), and three smaller portals (Domain, OnTheMarket, Land.com).

Who pays, for what, and why this vendor

Commercial customers — brokers, owners, tenants, appraisers, lenders, investors, REIT managers, hotel operators — pay a fixed monthly subscription because the data is costly to recreate and they treat it as the industry standard.▪ The buy decision is "I cannot do my job without CoStar," not a discretionary ad spend. CoStar's value is the accumulated 38-year history, the standardization, the in-house research organization, and the customer habit behind the screen, not the screen itself. No single customer is more than 5% of revenue (FY2025 10-K), so no buyer can squeeze price.

LoopNet customers pay for distribution. A broker or landlord with vacant space buys LoopNet because tenants, buyers, and brokers search there. That creates a marketplace loop: more inventory improves search usefulness, more search demand improves advertiser return, and advertiser return supports price and renewal. Residential customers pay for demand and visibility. Apartments.com sells renter demand to property managers; Homes.com sells agent exposure and promoted listings under a model where the listing agent keeps the lead; Domain and OnTheMarket sell portal exposure in Australia and the U.K.; Matterport sells or enables visual proof of a space.

Pricing, contract structure, and revenue recognition

The dominant model is recurring subscription or subscription-like advertising. Subscription-based contracts were 93% of revenue in FY2025, down from 96% in FY2024, and roughly 90% in Q1 2026▪ (FY2025 10-K; Q1 2026 10-Q). The decline is mix, not churn: Domain sells premium listings transactionally and Matterport sells hardware and capture services, so management now puts roughly 15% of revenue as non-subscription (Q1 2026 transcript). Contracts generally auto-renew; subscription revenue is recognized straight-line over the contract life, premium listings over ad life, auctions at successful closing, and product sales at shipment (FY2025 10-K). FY2025 annualized net new bookings were $308 million, up from $250 million in FY2024 and $286 million in FY2023▪; management uses bookings as a forward indicator but cautions explicitly that they are not comparable to revenue and lead it by roughly a year (FY2025 10-K, MD&A). Remaining performance obligations were $527 million at year-end 2025, but the figure excludes contracts of one year or less, so it understates the recurring base.

What drives price, volume, and cost

Price on the commercial side rises with inflation-indexed annual escalators on auto-renewing contracts plus module cross-sell; CoStar revenue grew 9% in Q1 2026 "driven by both volume and price" (Q1 2026 transcript). Volume on the commercial side is users (up 22% year-over-year to 317,000) and new modules sold into the existing base — Debt Solutions crossed $100 million of revenue, STR profitability benchmarking added 750 hotel subscribers, and international launches (U.K., Canada, France, Australia) are building the database de novo. On the residential side, volume is paying rooftops for Apartments.com (89,275, a record 14,000 added in 2025) and agent subscribers for Homes.com (35,175, up 205% year-over-year), while price is the swing variable: Homes.com is raising new-member prices in May 2026 because management calls the product underpriced against an asserted 11x agent return.

The cost curve is dominated not by cost of revenue, which is low and stable near 21–22% of revenue, but by sales and marketing. FY2025 selling and marketing was roughly $1,560 million, near 48% of revenue, of which Residential marketing was $771 million versus $77 million for Commercial▪ (FY2025 10-K, Note 13). That gap is the Homes.com and residential-portal build. If Residential revenue keeps growing while that marketing falls, incremental profit is large; the path to consolidated margin recovery runs entirely through residential spend discipline, not through cost of revenue. The Q1 2026 quarter showed the leverage: Residential Adjusted EBITDA improved by roughly $56 million year-over-year on a $102 million revenue increase, a high incremental margin off a negative base.

Growth comes from seats × ACV × retention in the CRE-data franchise, and from whether roughly $771 million a year of residential marketing converts into durable Homes.com membership revenue. It does not come from the "$300 trillion global asset class / $100 billion+ revenue opportunity" or the per-vertical TAM slides ($4B CRE, $9-10B apartments). Today the proven subscription franchise (CoStar suite + LoopNet + Apartments.com) is ~87% of revenue and ~60% of the FY2023→FY2025 growth, ~32% of that growth was bought, and only ~10% is the unproven residential land grab (growth attributed on the recast-segment window, since segment splits are disclosed only from FY2023; Homes.com is estimated, because CoStar does not report it as a clean line).

2. Key technologies & technical differentiators

CoStar's durable edge is mostly data, scale, original content, and workflow distribution, not a single hard-to-copy software primitive. Matterport is the closest thing to proprietary technology in the narrow sense, and even there the strategic value is the spatial-data archive and marketplace integration more than the camera. The AI products — Homes AI, Apartments AI — matter because they turn the data into a new interface and appear to lift engagement, but the underlying model stack is rented (Microsoft Azure OpenAI, AWS models, plus a proprietary model), so the differentiator is the data corpus and distribution, not exclusive model IP (Q4 2025 transcript; FY2025 10-K).

The proprietary database and research platform. The core system is a standardized real-estate data platform feeding every CoStar product. It is fed not just by scraped public data but by phone calls, emails, field inspections, public records, news monitoring, third-party feeds, and direct feeds from larger apartment sites, owners, brokers, and homebuilders; field researchers inspect properties, verify availability and construction, capture images and Matterport tours, and gather tenant information (FY2025 10-K, Item 1). A competitor can license MLS data, public records, cloud compute, and LLMs; it cannot quickly recreate 38 years of standardized CRE history, confidential hotel benchmarking data, lender loan data, proprietary images, and a research organization that the industry voluntarily updates.▪ The scale claims are concrete: management cites 2.4 trillion data fields, 150 million properties, nearly 700,000 Matterports, 94,000 hotels contributing confidential performance data, and more than 500 financial institutions contributing detailed loan data (Q4 2025 transcript). The differentiation shows up as usage and retention rather than a benchmarked performance delta: 317,000 users (up 22%), NPS 69, and 92% quarterly renewal in Q1 2026.

Matterport spatial data and digital twins. Matterport digitizes physical spaces into dimensionally accurate 3D tours, monetizing through subscriptions, capture services, cameras, and add-ons. It is the hardest-to-copy pure technology in the stack because it combines capture hardware, spatial processing, patents, and an installed spatial-data corpus of more than 12 million spaces and 38 billion square feet under management at the deal date (Q1 2024 transcript). The differentiation evidence is unusually specific: Apartments.com listings with a Matterport generated far higher engagement — management cites 56x more tour requests per listing and renters spending 46% more time — and Homes.com subscribers with a Matterport on a listing renewed at a 37% higher rate▪ (Q1 2026 transcript; Q3 2025 transcript). The substitution threat is that 3D capture becomes cheap and commodity through phone cameras and Gaussian-splat tools; Matterport itself supports capture "from most smartphones," which broadens adoption but weakens camera exclusivity.

Homes AI and Apartments AI. These replace filter-driven search with conversational and voice search grounded in CoStar data, Matterport, school data, and neighborhood content. Early engagement is strong: in the first week after Homes AI's release, users who entered AI mode spent 16:50 on site versus 4:24 for non-users and submitted 7x as many leads; by Q1 2026 AI users still ran nearly 4x as many searches and submitted 7x as many leads, with April time-on-site of 18 minutes versus 4:32 (Q4 2025 and Q1 2026 transcripts). Apartments.com's Smart Search, the first AI voice search in multifamily, lifted time on site 94% and listings viewed 63%. The unresolved question is whether early engagement converts into renewal, ARPU, lower paid-search spend, and paid listings at scale; the same AI wave is a threat if consumers begin searching in ChatGPT or Google's AI experiences rather than the portals.

Direct data feeds as a differentiator over MLS parity. Residential listings come primarily from MLS feeds, which competitors also access, so the MLS layer itself is not differentiated. CoStar's distinct position is its direct-feed overlay: 663 homebuilders had signed data-feed agreements covering roughly 75% of U.S. production new-home activity in Q1 2026, plus 110 million residential parcels added to CoStar and hundreds of thousands of brokerages feeding millions of listings, many not on the open internet▪ (Q1 2026 and Q4 2025 transcripts). These feeds are the foundation for a new CoStar New Homes product, but they are new and must prove persistence and exclusivity.

Infrastructure and traffic. Cloud hosting (AWS, Akamai Connected Cloud) and SEO and paid-search traffic are central to scale but are not moats — they are table-stakes infrastructure and distribution that competitors with capital can buy. Traffic acquisition matters most for residential economics: Apartments.com ranked first 82% of the time for the core 10,000 multifamily SEO keywords, and Homes.com had roughly 108 million average monthly unique visitors in 2025. The 10-K is blunt that search-engine rankings are outside CoStar's control and that search engines could enter real-estate marketplaces directly, and that the GA4 measurement change in June 2024 breaks pre/post traffic comparability.

What differentiates CoStar, then, is the proprietary data stock and the research organization that maintains it, reinforced by Matterport's spatial archive and the brand and distribution scale of the marketplaces. The AI and cloud layers are products and infrastructure built on that data, not independent technical moats.

3. Competitive advantages & moat assessment

CoStar holds one structural, durable advantage and a set of real-but-contestable ones, and the two reportable segments must be assessed separately because their moats differ in kind.

CoStar information & analytics: a structural advantage close to the great franchises

The CoStar information and analytics business holds a durable competitive advantage of the structural kind — a proprietary-data near-monopoly built over 38 years and more than $5 billion of cumulative content spend, defended by the largest research department in the CRE industry and validated by the financial evidence a real moat must leave.▪ It is the clearest moat in the company, and management's claims, the external record, and the industrial structure all support it. Management calls itself "the most comprehensive commercial real estate database available" with "the largest commercial real estate research department in the industry" (FY2025 10-K, Item 1). The independent corroboration is unusually strong because litigation forced a court to find facts on the moat: the Ninth Circuit held in June 2025 that a competitor (CREXi) "plausibly" alleged CoStar holds monopoly power over CRE-information markets through de facto exclusive broker deals and technological barriers to entry, and the Supreme Court declined review in March 2026, letting the antitrust counterclaims proceed (law.justia.com, accessed 2026-06-21; bisnow.com, accessed 2026-06-21) [web]. The industrial structure points the same way: a decades-deep, field-collected dataset whose re-creation cost is the barrier — not a patent, since the 10-K concedes the raw data is not copyrightable — is the asset that earns durable excess returns without heavy reinvestment to defend it.

Each claimed source of advantage leaves the financial mark it should. Pricing power shows up as revenue growth "driven by both volume and price" while customers who publicly complain about "aggressive pricing" renew anyway. Switching costs show up as a contract renewal rate that has held at 89% company-wide for seven straight quarters, with 95% renewal for customers subscribed five years or more and a 92% CoStar quarterly renewal rate▪ (Q1 2026 transcript). Scale shows up because the industry proactively reports listings and transactions to the standard, so coverage compounds with the installed base. The one weak leg is legal protection: the raw data is not copyrightable, which is why CoStar layers contracts and anti-piracy enforcement on top, and why those layers draw antitrust scrutiny.

The standalone financial proof is partly inferred because CoStar's segment margin is buried inside the $1,787 million Commercial segment, which earned $672 million of Adjusted EBITDA (37.6%). That segment margin is dragged down by low-margin Matterport hardware inside Other CRE and by LoopNet marketing; stripping those out implies the CoStar suite alone runs a mid-40s percent EBITDA margin▪ [estimate: subtraction — strip Other CRE ($216M) and LoopNet ($312M) and their lower margins from the $1,787M/$672M segment, leaving CoStar ~$1,259M at mid-40s%; bounds mid-40s to high-40s%], medium confidence. On an asset-light subscription base with immaterial capex, that margin implies a return on invested capital far above any reasonable cost of capital, though a precise segment ROIC is not computable because the company does not allocate invested capital by segment.

Residential: one durable franchise, several contestable positions, and one unproven bet

The Residential segment is not one business. Apartments.com is a durable franchise — the #1 U.S. multifamily marketplace with 67% brand awareness versus 36% for the #2 competitor, a 99% monthly renewal rate, an NPS near 90, and fifteen consecutive quarters of double-digit revenue growth.▪ The financial evidence of a moat is present: it grew rooftops to 89,275 (a record 14,000 added in 2025) while the closest competitor lost share, and it did so while improving marketing efficiency (Q4 2025 and Q1 2026 transcripts). The best public proxy for the steady-state economics of a dominant residential portal is Rightmove at a 70% operating margin and REA Group at a 58% EBITDA margin; CoStar's own "core network" (which includes Apartments.com) ran at a 47% margin in Q3 2025, up 400 basis points year-over-year▪ (fool.com, accessed 2026-06-21) [web]. The mechanism is a two-sided network effect with winner-take-most dynamics: renters go to the site with the most listings, listers pay the site with the most renters, and the brand and SEO scale (first for 82% of 10,000 core keywords) is the barrier a sub-scale entrant cannot match.

Homes.com has no demonstrated moat yet — it is a marketing-funded attempt to manufacture one, having consumed roughly $3 billion of cumulative investment for only about $106 million of annual run-rate revenue, with management not expecting breakeven until 2030.▪ The "your-listing, your-lead" model is a real product difference from Zillow's buyer-lead model, and early engagement and a claimed 11x agent return are encouraging, but the only durable moat in residential portals is consumer-traffic aggregation, and on that axis Homes.com is #2-#3 and still spending Super-Bowl-scale money to hold position. Whether this becomes a Rightmove-style annuity or a multi-billion-dollar write-off is the single biggest open question in the company, and the evidence today does not resolve it. Domain is a contestable #2 in an REA-dominated duopoly (REA's realestate.com.au draws 4.0x the visits of its nearest competitor and earns a 58% EBITDA margin), Land.com is a real but small #1 in rural, and OnTheMarket is a U.K. #2-#3 challenger to Rightmove. The segment's reported $230 million FY2025 Adjusted EBITDA loss is a capital-allocation choice, essentially all Homes.com, not a judgment on Apartments.com's quality.

4. Competitive landscape & market sizing

CoStar competes in distinct markets with very different structures and share positions.

CoStar CRE data and analytics is fragmented by use case rather than dominated by one rival: LightBox owns parcel and geospatial workflow, MSCI/RCA owns transactions and capital markets, Moody's/Reis owns lending forecasts, CompStak owns crowdsourced lease comps, and VTS/Dealpath own workflow.▪ CoStar is the broad horizontal utility, and its revenue scale dwarfs the data comparables — MSCI paid $950 million for RCA at roughly $70 million of ARR (13.6x), and Moody's paid $278 million for Reis. The risk is not a single competitor but data modularization by these specialists plus AI experiences that reduce the need for one large interface; the counter-evidence is CoStar's scale, rising users, and renewal behavior. Against the 2023 deck's management-estimated $4 billion U.S. TAM, CoStar revenue implies roughly 31% penetration, a figure that is a CoStar estimate and should be treated as such.

LoopNet is the clear CRE-marketplace traffic leader — the 2023 deck claimed 14 million monthly unique visitors, 50x the average competitor and 7x the nearest by SEMrush — but its revenue is a small share of the market it claims. FY2025 LoopNet revenue of $312 million is about 6% of the deck's $5 billion North American TAM and 2% of its $15 billion global TAM, both management estimates. The rising adjacent competitor is CREXi, which is the named LoopNet and Ten-X rival and reported $815.6 billion of active for-sale listings as of November 2025, up 16.7% year-over-year, and more than $1 trillion of cumulative transactions▪ (prnewswire.com, accessed 2026-06-21) [web]. CREXi's separately reported auction figures (more than $4.5 billion of property supported and a 96% close rate) are cumulative since its 2019 launch, not 2025 annual figures, so the cleanest comparison is the listings and transactions data: CREXi growing while Ten-X declines.

Apartments.com competes in a U.S. rental advertising market concentrated around itself, Zillow Rentals, and the Redfin/Rent.com/ApartmentGuide lineage. Apartments.com's Q1 2026 revenue of $312 million annualizes to roughly $1,250 million, about twice Zillow's FY2025 Rentals revenue of $630 million and far above the Redfin/Rent. lineage's $204 million. The market shifted when Zillow became the exclusive multifamily listing provider on Redfin's properties — the FTC has sued Zillow and Redfin alleging that agreement suppressed competition, while Zillow frames it as distribution. Apartments.com captured roughly 1,200 rooftops from Rent.com organically after that sale, "without buying anything." Against the deck's $9 billion TAM, Apartments.com's annualized revenue implies roughly 14% penetration, with the underpenetrated white space being small (1-49 unit) properties at 1% penetration. The contested point is that Zillow Rentals grew revenue 39% off a smaller base, so the rentals battleground is more contested than management's traffic-decline narrative implies.

Homes.com has real consumer audience but early monetization. Zillow remains the entrenched #1 with roughly 220 million average monthly users and $514 million of quarterly For Sale revenue, versus Homes.com's $26 million quarterly revenue and $106 million ARR.▪ The better monetization-share proxy than total visitors is promoted listings: Homes.com subscribers promoted 260,000 active listings in Q1 2026, 8.7% of nearly 3 million U.S. homes for sale. Realtor.com/Move (News Corp, 80%; REA, 20%) generated $552 million of FY2025 revenue with roughly 72 million average monthly users and near-100% MLS coverage, and Redfin is now inside Rocket's ecosystem after its July 2025 acquisition.

Domain and OnTheMarket give CoStar credible #2/#3 international challengers but not incumbent economics. Domain reaches roughly 6.6 million Australians monthly, about 55% of REA's 12.1 million, but REA's 4.0x visit-frequency advantage implies lower engagement and pricing power▪; the A$3.0 billion implied EV was about 6.9x FY25 H1 annualized revenue and 19x EBITDA. OnTheMarket's roughly £35 million of TTM revenue at acquisition was about 8% of Rightmove's £425 million, in a profit pool Rightmove controls at a 70% margin.

Matterport, BizBuySell, and Ten-X (the Other CRE bucket) are run as funnel inputs to the flagship marketplaces, not standalone profit centers. Matterport is the category leader in automated 3D capture (1.2 million subscribers, 14.1 million spaces, 50.7 billion square feet) against a fragmented set of cheaper competitors (Zillow 3D Home, CubiCasa, iGuide, CloudPano), but it has no measured unit share and contributed a $146 million net loss in its first ten months under CoStar.▪ The widely cited "$24 billion digital-twin market growing ~41% to $400 billion-plus" TAM is dominated by industrial, manufacturing, and automotive predictive-maintenance use cases, with real estate a minor slice, so any framing that maps Matterport onto that TAM overstates the relevant opportunity by one to two orders of magnitude▪ (gminsights.com, accessed 2026-06-21) [web]. When CoStar bought Matterport, Zillow de-platformed Matterport 3D tours from its listings — the event is real and confirmed across trade press, but it occurred on October 20, 2025 (driven by a CoStar/Zillow API and terms-of-service dispute), not in late 2024; either way it removed Matterport's largest non-CoStar distribution surface (housingwire.com, accessed 2026-06-21) [web, unverified]. BizBuySell is a small, profitable, slow-growing #1 in Main-Street business-for-sale ($36 million revenue, 37% EBITDA margin). Ten-X is the leader of a tiny, shrinking niche: online auction was about 1% of CRE transactions when CoStar paid $190 million in 2020, Ten-X had roughly 90% of digital CRE auctions on $24 billion of cumulative sales, but auction revenue has declined for several years to roughly $33 million implied▪ [estimate: $216M Other CRE − $147M Matterport − $36M BizBuySell], and Ten-X was folded into LoopNet in March 2025, so the option has decayed rather than appreciated, and the growing adjacent player is CREXi, not Ten-X.

5. Value chain & profit pools

CoStar is not a physical-goods company with a supplier-distributor-OEM chain. It is a two-sided information and marketplace business that captures almost the entire category dollar at one layer — the CoStar platform — because there is essentially no distributor or integrator layer; it sells direct through a 2,090-person sales force. The relevant chain is data suppliers (MLS feeds, public records, the in-house research department, 663 homebuilder feeds, Matterport content) feeding CoStar, which aggregates consumer and professional demand and monetizes it through subscriptions and advertising sold to CRE professionals, residential agents, apartment owners, and advertisers.

The dollar decomposition that matters is therefore internal, between the two segments, because the whole platform is one layer of the chain. The profit pool sits entirely in Commercial.

Consolidated gross margin held near 79%, dipping slightly in FY2025 because Matterport hardware is the one low-margin input ($28 million of equipment and capture costs) in an otherwise 79%-gross-margin business, which is why management is deliberately pulling Matterport hardware down and pushing the SaaS subscription up.

The binding constraint sits on the residential side, not the commercial one. The scarce resource is not data or capacity — CoStar can manufacture data at will through its research department — it is converting traffic into paid agent subscriptions at rising ARPU, and the marketing required to generate that traffic.▪ Homes.com has only 35,175 subscribers against a target of hundreds of thousands, an ARPU of roughly $287, and a ~$550 million 2026 net investment; management concedes the constraint is sales-force productivity ("a very rookie sales force... unprecedented to have that many salespeople with that little tenure"), with company net new bookings declining sequentially for three straight quarters even as headcount was added, though the year-over-year figures rose (FY2025 net new bookings of $308 million against $250 million in FY2024, and up 16% in Q1 2026). The secondary constraint is upstream MLS feeds, which the 10-K warns "may be terminated with limited notice or cause" and whose rules can be "targeted... in response to our operations". Losing feeds would degrade the residential products. The CRE data, which CoStar collects itself, is unaffected.

The customer-side return is strongest for Homes.com, where management now discloses a cohort ROI: the first 11,400 members earned $36,400 more in commissions in year one against a $3,400 average subscription cost, an 11x return, which management frames as evidence the product is underpriced.▪ The study is company-run and selection-prone — it cannot prove the membership caused the lift versus better agents self-selecting in — but even discounted heavily the implied payback is fast, which is the basis for the May 2026 price increase. Bargaining power sits firmly with CoStar at most interfaces and is increasing: no supplier has power on the CRE side, and on the customer side CoStar sets price with strong renewal at CoStar (92% quarterly), Apartments.com (99% monthly), and a strengthening LoopNet (asset-based pricing). The two weak interfaces are Homes.com, where pricing power is an emerging bet not yet demonstrated on a large base, and Domain, whose transactional model is exposed to a cyclical Australian housing market CoStar does not control.

There is almost no circularity or vendor financing. Demand is funded by independent third-party customers, not by CoStar's own capital recycled through the chain. The only "stake-in-a-chain-participant" flows are outright acquisitions (Matterport, Domain, Visual Lease, OnTheMarket) that internalized content suppliers and downstream marketplaces; CRE profits pay for the residential build, which is a concentration risk if CRE ever stumbles but not a circular-demand risk.

6. Secular & industry trends

CoStar sits in two end-markets that move on different clocks, and lumping them obscures the picture. The whole thesis is share gain, not market growth: the end-markets (CRE data, U.S. apartment advertising, residential portals) are low-single-digit-growth or cyclically flat, while CoStar has compounded mid-to-high teens for years.▪ That gap is share capture.

On the commercial side, the CRE market is mid-recovery from a deep trough. Management characterized 2022-2024 as "the worst commercial real estate crisis in recent memory," with office net absorption turning positive only in the last two quarters and CRE sales volumes climbing 30% year-over-year to above long-term averages (Q4 2025 transcript; these are management figures, not independently pulled this run, so medium confidence). CoStar grew through the trough — roughly 10.5% in FY2023, 9% in FY2024, 7% in FY2025 — which implies it gained share every year of the downturn, consistent with non-discretionary subscription data. The clearest case is the U.K., where a primary competitor shut down in December 2025 and CoStar onboarded roughly 166 of its clients. The flip side is that some of the FY2026 commercial guidance (commercial revenue up roughly 10% at midpoint) is cyclical recovery, not pure secular share gain, and CoStar growth decelerates when the cycle turns down.

On the apartment-advertising side the share shift is explicit. Apartments.com grew 11% in FY2025 and 10% in Q1 2026 into a soft rental-search market by taking competitor rooftops — Apartments.com network visits were up 14% year-over-year in January 2026 while Zillow's rental traffic was down 48% and the broader Zillow-Redfin network down 29%▪ (Q4 2025 transcript). Management called the Rent.com rooftop grab "a once-in-a-decade opportunity to do a share shift," which is durable only as long as the competitor keeps shrinking and caps out once share is held; Apartments.com growth has decelerated toward 10-11%, and the durable run-rate may be high-single-digit. Homes.com is buying share with marketing rather than organic pull: revenue grew 63% in FY2025 and 58% in Q1 2026 to a roughly $106 million run-rate, funded by ~$550 million of 2026 net investment, with paid listings at 8.7% of homes for sale.

The technology shift that could change the slope in either direction is generative AI. CoStar argues its proprietary dataset is the fuel LLMs cannot replicate, positioning it "as a clear winner in the AI era." But its own 10-K names the opposite risk directly and the disclosure is escalating: generative-AI and "artificial intelligence" mentions in the 10-K jumped from 2 in FY2024 to 18 in FY2025, and the FY2025 filing added an explicit new mechanism — competitors building "AI-powered platforms that provide access to real estate agents, real estate data, and related content without directing users to our marketplaces," and "rapid advancement of generative AI Technologies may reduce the barrier to entry for new competitors."▪ This year-over-year escalation is itself the leading indicator that the residential marketplaces' cheap-traffic, lead-generation model faces a new structural headwind management did not flag a year earlier. The single biggest slope risk to the residential thesis is zero-click AI search eroding the SEO and paid-search traffic that Homes.com depends on. The secondary, durable risk is MLS-feed termination.

The secular component (CoStar cross-sell and international, Apartments.com share, Homes.com if it works) is real and is the dominant explanation for 60 consecutive quarters of double-digit revenue growth through the COVID shock and the CRE crisis. But the composition shifted — pre-2021 growth was CRE-data and Apartments.com organic, while 2021-2025 growth leaned increasingly on acquisitions and Homes.com marketing — so organic, pre-M&A growth has decelerated toward low-double-digit even as reported growth stayed high-teens. Reported growth held up, but organic growth slowed, and part of what remains now comes from a Homes.com that does not yet make money.▪ The management TAMs frame the runway but should be read critically: the "$300 trillion global asset class / $100 billion+ revenue opportunity" is asset value, not addressable revenue, and the "$10 billion Apartments.com TAM" treats 22 million sub-five-unit landlords at full ARPU, so the credible near-term ceiling sits well below the cited figures.

7. Critical points

  1. The consolidated loss is the cost of the residential build, not a sign of a broken business. CoStar reported a FY2025 operating loss of $72 million and $7 million of net income, yet the Commercial segment earned $672 million of Adjusted EBITDA at a 37.6% margin while Residential lost $230 million, essentially all Homes.com. A reader who anchors on the consolidated P&L will misjudge the quality of the CoStar franchise (cross-referenced in Section 8).

  2. CoStar information and analytics is one of the highest-margin data franchises in public markets, hidden inside a loss-making consolidated entity. The suite runs an estimated mid-40s percent EBITDA margin [estimate], between FactSet (32%) and MSCI (55%), on 92% quarterly renewal, 95% five-year renewal, and 22% user growth. A federal appeals court found a rival "plausibly" alleged it holds monopoly power — you do not get antitrust liability for mere brand.

  3. Homes.com is the single biggest swing factor and the central unresolved question. Roughly $3 billion of cumulative investment has produced about $106 million of run-rate revenue, with breakeven not promised until 2030 and a ~$550 million net investment in 2026 alone.▪ The bull case is convergence toward Rightmove/REA portal economics (70%/58% margins); the bear case is that the U.S. has Zillow and a different commission structure and Homes.com never reaches those economics. The evidence today does not resolve it (cross-referenced in Section 8).

  4. Apartments.com is a durable, high-margin annuity that funds the rest of the segment. It is the #1 multifamily marketplace (67% brand awareness versus 36% for #2), with 99% monthly renewal and a record 14,000 rooftops added in 2025; the "core network" that contains it runs a 47% margin. The contested point is that Zillow Rentals grew revenue 39% off a smaller base while Apartments.com grew ~11% (cross-referenced in Section 8).

  5. Roughly 40% of FY2026 revenue growth is acquisition-driven and non-subscription, diluting revenue quality. Three acquisitions in 14 months (Visual Lease, Matterport, Domain) lifted reported FY2025 growth to 19% against ~11% organic and Q1 2026 to 23% reported versus ~10% organic. Non-subscription revenue rose to ~15% of total, and Domain adds Australian housing-cycle exposure CoStar did not previously have (cross-referenced in Section 8).

  6. The medium-term targets ride on an organic-growth rate the company has not recently delivered. Management commits to a 15% revenue CAGR to 2028 and 2030, but organic growth ran 11-13% in 2022-2024; the 15%-plus prints came either off a smaller pre-2021 base or, in 2025-26, with acquisitions.▪ Management itself concedes the leg may miss ("we can rationalize costs if revenue growth is less than 15%").

  7. Pay rewarded size over per-share value, and the redesign was forced. CEO pay tripled to $37.4 million as FY2025 net income collapsed to $6.5 million; the 2022-2024 long-term plan paid 172.8% on a revenue metric despite a negative, 33rd-percentile three-year shareholder return. Say-on-pay support declined to ~53% from ~92%, and the 2026 plan switched to adjusted EPS and weighted relative TSR only after an activist campaign and that vote.

  8. AI cuts both ways, and the company's own disclosure says so louder each year. CoStar frames its data as the fuel AI cannot replicate, but the FY2025 10-K added an explicit warning that AI platforms could bypass its marketplaces and lower entry barriers, with AI risk-factor mentions rising from 2 to 18 year-over-year. Zero-click search eroding residential traffic is the biggest slope risk to the segment management is spending billions to build.

8. Variant perceptions

The consolidated loss says CoStar is barely profitable. The −$72 million operating loss (FY2025) is entirely the cost of the Homes.com and Residential build: Residential lost $230 million of Adjusted EBITDA on $771 million of residential marketing. The CoStar information and analytics suite is one of the highest-margin software franchises in public markets, in a mid-40s percent EBITDA tier between FactSet (32%) and MSCI (55%). The Commercial segment earned $672 million of Adjusted EBITDA on $1,787 million of revenue, a 37.6% margin (FY2025 10-K, Note 13), and the CoStar-suite mid-40s figure is a flagged estimate.

CoStar's moat is just incumbency and brand that AI will erode. The moat is the dataset itself: decades of field-collected records whose re-creation cost is the barrier, not the brand. A court found a rival "plausibly" alleged it constitutes monopoly power, which brand alone does not generate. The lock-in (92% quarterly, 95% five-year renewal) has held while raising price, and users grew 22% year-over-year in the AI era.

The Residential segment is a loss-making, value-destroying distraction. The bear case conflates a deliberate capital-allocation bet (Homes.com) with the quality of the Apartments.com business that funds it. Apartments.com inside the segment is a ~$1.25 billion, 99%-renewal annuity that backs the 47% core-network margin and would, on the Rightmove/REA template, earn 50-70% margins as a standalone #1 portal. Of the segment's −$230 million FY2025 Adjusted EBITDA, essentially all is Homes.com.

Apartments.com is unassailable and its moat is widening everywhere. Its single closest competitor, Zillow Rentals, grew revenue 39% to $630 million and multifamily listings 44% in FY2025 — faster than Apartments.com's ~11% — while Apartments.com's own ARPU per rooftop softened on a lower-end mix. The moat is intact on share and overall pricing power, but the rentals battleground is more contested than management's traffic-decline narrative implies. Quantification: +39% (Zillow Rentals) versus ~11% (Apartments.com), FY2025.

The 11x agent ROI proves Homes.com's moat. An 11x customer return is a reason agents should subscribe, not proof of a defensible toll. The moat in portals is consumer traffic, where Homes.com is still #2-#3 and spending ~$550 million per year net to hold position; ~$3 billion is already sunk with breakeven not expected until 2030. Demand still requires roughly $550 million a year of marketing; nothing yet shows it persists if the spend stops. Quantification: ~$3 billion invested, ~$106 million run-rate revenue, breakeven 2030 [web].

CoStar is a ~95% subscription, recurring-revenue compounder. After Matterport (hardware) and Domain (transactional listings), management states ~15% of revenue is now non-subscription, and ~40% of 2026 revenue growth is acquisition or non-subscription. Q1 2026 organic growth was ~10% versus ~23% reported. The recurring-revenue quality has quietly diluted, and Domain adds cyclicality CoStar did not have. Quantification: ~13 points of Q1 2026 growth was inorganic/non-subscription.

Matterport gives CoStar a $400 billion digital-twin growth platform. The $400 billion-plus digital-twin TAM is industrial, manufacturing, and predictive-maintenance, with real estate a minor slice; Matterport's real addressable market is the much smaller real-estate/AEC virtual-tour niche, and it contributed a $146 million net loss in its first ten months under CoStar. The mapped TAM overstates the relevant opportunity by one to two orders of magnitude. The precise "38% predictive maintenance / automotive largest" figures could not be reproduced on the cited page, and the 2025 base sits closer to $19-26 billion than $24 billion, but the directional point holds.

Ten-X is a leading online CRE transaction platform and a growth option. Online auction is still about 1% of CRE transactions, Ten-X revenue has declined for roughly three consecutive years to ~$33 million implied, it was folded into LoopNet in March 2025, and the growing adjacent player is CREXi. The 2020-deal facts are firm (90% of digital auctions, $24 billion cumulative, $190 million price, $20-30 billion TAM), but the FY2023-FY2025 auction-revenue decline is an inference, so the ~$33 million is a subtraction estimate, not a reported number.

10. Management, incentives & capital allocation

CoStar is run by a long-tenured, internally grown operating bench under a 38-year founder-CEO. Andrew Florance founded the company in 1987 and took it public in 1998; the operating heads — Lisa Ruggles (global operations, since 1999), Frank Simuro (CTO, since 1999), Frederick Saint (marketplaces, since the LoopNet acquisition) — each have 13-26 years of tenure. The one arms-length, external seat is the CFO, refreshed in July 2024 with Christian Lown (formerly CFO of Freddie Mac and Navient). Executive turnover is low. The defining governance fact is that Florance owns less than 1% of the company (3,044,568 shares, of which 1,393,133 are unexercised options, against 408 million shares outstanding), with no dual-class structure and no super-voting shares. His control is reputational and tenure-based, not structural, which is why the 2024-2026 activist campaign (Third Point's Daniel Loeb and D.E. Shaw) had real leverage: a 1%-owning founder-CEO is fully exposed to a shareholder vote. The April 2025 settlement added three independent directors and installed an independent chair; after Third Point's standstill expired, Loeb escalated in January 2026, calling Homes.com "a cautionary tale of management hubris" and demanding the board align pay to total shareholder return.

The pre-2026 incentive architecture rewarded enterprise scale, not per-share value. The 2025 cash bonus was 100% Adjusted EBITDA, a management-defined non-GAAP metric, and paid the CEO at the 200% cap ($3 million) in a year net income collapsed to $6.5 million. The long-term plan keyed to three-year revenue, the metric a serial acquirer most easily clears, on a slope so shallow that 102% of target paid 200%, with targets explicitly neutralized for acquisitions. The evidence is in the realized payouts: the 2022-2024 performance plan settled at 172.8% of target — a 200% revenue payout modified down only to 86.4% by a TSR component — even though three-year total shareholder return was negative 1.5%, in the 33rd percentile of the S&P 500.▪ Executives earned 73% above target while shareholders lost money. CEO pay tripled to a $37.4 million peak (a 303:1 ratio) as net income declined and the stock lagged peers, and the board's defense blamed "broader market dynamics" and "the perceived threat of emerging AI technology," external attribution for a year the company chose to spend net income to near-zero.

Say-on-pay support declined to roughly 53% from roughly 92% the prior year, and the 2026 redesign followed: a 50% organic-revenue weight added to the cash bonus, adjusted EPS (75%) and weighted relative TSR (25%, capped at target if absolute TSR is negative) replacing revenue in the long-term plan, stock options and one-year performance RSAs eliminated, symmetrical performance slopes, the CEO ownership requirement raised from 6x to 10x salary, the legacy 280G excise-tax gross-up removed, and a supplemental clawback added. The redesign is materially better aligned on per-share value — but every element was extracted after a 53% vote and an activist threat, so it is evidence the prior plan was gameable rather than evidence of proactive governance.

On capital allocation, the 10-year record reframes the per-share story. Operating cash flow rose steadily from $196 million (2016) to a peak of $490 million (2023) before dipping on the Homes.com build, and the company ran net cash throughout (roughly $4-5 billion through 2021-2023). Free cash flow turned negative in 2024 (−$186 million) on a capex spike to $579 million, 21.2% of revenue, for the Richmond campus build; ex-campus capex runs ~2.5% of revenue. The capital-allocation shift is the recent pivot to buybacks: $500 million completed in 2025, a $1.5 billion program authorized in January 2026 with ~$700 million planned for 2026, targeting retirement of ~5% of shares. This is a change of habit — historically CoStar issued large share blocks to fund M&A (11.7 million shares for Matterport alone), so the diluted share count rose from 324 million (2016) to 421 million (2025). The buyback is sensible if executed at attractive prices, but it raises the bar on acquisition returns because the company is using both balance-sheet cash and equity capacity while also asking shareholders to fund ~$550 million of annual Homes.com investment. The binding stewardship question is whether enterprise growth reaches the per-share line: revenue compounded ~16% over the decade, but diluted EPS went from $0.26 (2016) to $0.02 (2025) as the residential build consumed earnings▪. Whether that reverses depends on Homes.com.

11. M&A, divestitures & deal track record

CoStar is a disciplined serial acquirer in its older, now-proven core, and an as-yet-unproven one in its newest, largest bets. The judgment rests on the evidence: the apartment and LoopNet roll-ups demonstrably increased the size and strategic quality of the business, STR likely deepened the data moat, and the company has recorded no goodwill or intangible impairments across 2023-2025.▪ But the accounting record shows scale, not yet discipline — goodwill rose from $2.4 billion (2023) to $4.9 billion (2025), driven by Matterport and Domain, and the auditor flagged the Matterport and Domain acquisition accounting as a critical audit matter because $1.5 billion of acquired intangibles rests on forward-looking assumptions. No impairment is reassuring but not conclusive, because CoStar integrates acquired businesses into unified products, which makes standalone underperformance hard to see.

The synergy track record is real where it is old. The newest bets are the ones still being underwritten. The capital-allocation tension is rising: Matterport used 11.7 million CoStar shares while the company simultaneously runs a $1.5 billion buyback and funds ~$550 million of organic Homes.com investment, which raises the required return on every deal.

The weighted-average cost of debt is effectively the 2.80% Senior Notes coupon, since the revolver is undrawn; on a cash basis it is the same because there is no floating-rate draw. At year-end 2025 the company held $1,733 million of cash and restricted cash against $1,012 million of gross debt, for net cash of roughly $721 million (the mid-2026 xBBG snapshot shows net cash of $449 million after buybacks). There is no maturity wall — a single $1 billion note due 2030 — and refinancing at current rates would lift the embedded coupon, but the absolute amount is immaterial against $430 million of operating cash flow and a $1.1 billion undrawn revolver. Net debt to EBITDA is negative (net cash) throughout the history.

Weighted-average cost of debt is the principal-weighted coupon across drawn instruments. With only the $1.0 billion note drawn, it equals the 2.80% note coupon; the floating revolver is undrawn, so the cash interest rate does not differ materially.

The more relevant balance-sheet feature is share dilution. Stock-based compensation rose from $36 million (2016) to $194 million (2025), reaching ~6% of revenue in FY2025, and the diluted share count rose from 324 million to 421 million over the decade — a roughly 30% increase, driven by SBC plus equity issued for M&A (notably 11.7 million shares for Matterport).▪ The new $1.5 billion buyback ($700 million planned for 2026, ~5% retirement targeted) is the first sustained offset to that dilution; whether it nets to share-count reduction depends on price and pace. The structural balance-sheet risks are minor: there is no refinancing wall, no covenant proximity (the revolver is undrawn), no meaningful floating-rate exposure, and only modest FX exposure introduced by Domain (Australia) and OnTheMarket (U.K.). The capex line was temporarily lifted by the Richmond campus build: gross capex was $579 million in FY2024 (21.2% of revenue) and $307 million in FY2025 (9.5%), against $45 million in Q1 2026. Ex-campus capex runs ~2.5% of revenue, consistent with an asset-light subscription model.

17. Forward model summary

The forward model is built bottom-up across five revenue lines with different drivers, summed to segment and consolidated, with FY2025 actuals tied line-for-line to the reconciled financial history. It is an operating model only, running from revenue through adjusted EBITDA, GAAP operating income, and free cash flow. No multiples are applied and no per-share value is derived, and the assumptions table below gives each driver a provenance tag and a strength assessment.

The five lines and their FY2025 anchors are CoStar information and analytics ($1,259 million, modeled as firm subscriptions times seats times inflation-indexed price, plus the CRE-cycle and international legs), LoopNet ($312 million, paid listings times ARPU, lifted by the asset-based-pricing re-rate), Other CRE ($216 million, Matterport SaaS plus a one-time 2026 Matterport-full-year lap step and Ten-X decline), Apartments.com ($1,250 million, paying rooftops times ARPU), and Other Residential ($210 million, with Homes.com, Domain, and OnTheMarket/Land modeled separately because they move on different clocks).

The base case has one internal-consistency flag. The FY2026 Residential segment incremental adjusted-EBITDA margin computes to 112%, above 100%, because the segment goes from a $230 million loss to a roughly $108 million profit on about $300 million of incremental revenue: the segment swings from loss to profit because marketing falls while revenue grows, not because incremental margins exceed 100%. It is mechanically valid but depends entirely on management cutting Homes.com spend on schedule, which is why the residential margin is the model's largest sensitivity. No base-case revenue-driver growth rate in the model exceeds the company's own demonstrated rates, and the Commercial-segment incremental margin (49-57%) sits well below the 92% clean-year drop-through the history shows, so the data core is modeled conservatively.

18. Risks

The consolidated, severity-ranked risk inventory below draws on a dedicated risks workpaper, the dimension workpapers, and the management record. Each risk is given a mechanism, a quantified exposure, and a leading indicator.

  1. Homes.com fails to convert traffic into a durable, profitable subscriber base. Mechanism: ~$3 billion is sunk and ~$550 million more is committed for 2026 against ~$106 million of run-rate revenue, with breakeven not promised until 2030; if consumer demand requires permanent Super-Bowl-scale marketing, the segment never reaches Rightmove-style economics. Exposure: the entire residential build plus the ~$2.9 billion of residential goodwill. Leading indicators: organic-traffic growth, agent subscriber adds (was +4,300 in Q1 2026), ARPU after the May 2026 price increase, and Residential Adjusted EBITDA versus the Q2 2026 breakeven guide.

  2. AI and zero-click search erode residential traffic. Mechanism: if consumers begin searches in ChatGPT or Google's AI experiences, the SEM/SEO lead-generation model that Homes.com and Apartments.com depend on degrades; the FY2025 10-K added this exact warning and AI risk mentions rose from 2 to 18 year-over-year. Exposure: the 45% of revenue in Residential, most directly Homes.com. Leading indicators: organic versus SEM traffic mix, unique-visitor trends despite continued marketing spend, Google referral share.

  3. The CREXi antitrust case and class actions force open the CRE data moat. Mechanism: the Ninth Circuit let monopolization counterclaims proceed; an adverse outcome could force CoStar to drop broker exclusivity or open its listing pipes, weakening the contractual reinforcement of the CRE moat. Exposure: the highest-margin CoStar suite (~39% of revenue, ~mid-40s margin). Leading indicators: case docket and any settlement, CoStar quarterly renewal slipping below ~90%.

  4. MLS-feed termination degrades residential content. Mechanism: residential listings come from voluntary, terminable MLS feeds whose rules can be "targeted in response to our operations"; loss of feeds degrades the Homes.com and Apartments.com product. Exposure: residential content quality and traffic. Leading indicators: MLS rule changes, Clear Cooperation debates, disclosed feed disputes.

  5. The medium-term 15% growth target misses, exposing the margin path. Mechanism: organic growth ran 11-13% and the 15% target needs acceleration or further M&A; if revenue undershoots, the 2028 ~$1.25 billion Adjusted EBITDA depends on an unproven cost-rationalization lever. Exposure: the entire 2028 forward case. Leading indicators: organic revenue growth ex-acquisitions, net new bookings, the cadence of new M&A.

  6. Governance and capital-allocation tension with an active billionaire activist. Mechanism: a 1%-owning founder-CEO with low structural control faces an activist (Loeb) demanding strategic alternatives for Homes.com and TSR-aligned pay; the 2025 say-on-pay collapse to ~53% and the forced 2026 pay redesign signal continued pressure. Exposure: strategic direction and management stability. Leading indicators: 2026 say-on-pay vote, board composition, any Homes.com strategic-review announcement.

  7. Acquisition integration and impairment risk on Matterport and Domain. Mechanism: $1.5 billion of acquired intangibles rest on forward assumptions the auditor flagged as a critical audit matter; if the platform-conversion thesis disappoints, impairment would appear in goodwill or accelerated amortization. Exposure: ~$2.3 billion of Matterport+Domain goodwill. Leading indicators: Matterport subscription growth and renewal, Domain margin and non-core divestments, any change in amortization or restructuring charges.

  8. CRE cyclicality and Domain housing-cycle exposure. Mechanism: CoStar growth dampens but does not eliminate the CRE cycle (it slowed to ~7% in the 2022-2024 trough), and Domain adds transactional Australian housing-cycle and FX exposure CoStar did not previously have. Exposure: the cyclical portion of Commercial plus Domain's transactional revenue. Leading indicators: CRE sales-volume and absorption data, Australian listing volumes, net new bookings turning negative year-over-year.

These risks arrive together in the bad-quarter case: a CRE-cycle rollover would slow CoStar bookings while a soft housing market pressures Domain and Apartments.com, with continued Homes.com spend holding consolidated margins down.

20. What we still don't know / monitoring KPIs

The following material gaps were identified and remain open; any gap that bears on a section's conclusion is flagged at the top of that section or in the front-matter caveats.

  1. Product-level gross margins and brand-level residential splits. CoStar discloses revenue by four buckets and profit by two segments only. Apartments.com, Homes.com, Domain, and Matterport standalone gross margins and full FY revenue are not public, so the unit-economics table in Section 1 stops at ASP proxies and the CoStar-suite margin is an estimate. Monitor: any move to product-level cost disclosure; quarterly brand revenue and ARR.

  2. CoStar standalone segment ROIC. Invested capital is not allocated by segment, so the cleanest moat test — a durable ROIC-above-WACC series for the CoStar suite — is inferred from margin and asset-lightness, not computed. Monitor: any segment-level asset disclosure.

  3. Homes.com breakeven path and unit economics on a large base. The renewal, cancellation, and cohort-ROI data on a mature base do not yet exist; management guides Q2 2026 Residential breakeven while a web source cited Homes.com breakeven "not before 2030." Monitor: subscriber adds, ARPU after the May 2026 price increase, organic-traffic growth, Residential Adjusted EBITDA.

  4. Acquisition returns and impairment exposure. True acquisition-adjusted ROICs for Matterport, Domain, and the residential assets are not auditable because they integrate into segment reporting; ~$2.3 billion of recent goodwill rests on forward assumptions. Monitor: Matterport subscription growth and renewal, Domain margin and divestments, amortization and restructuring charges.

  5. The Homes.com-versus-Apartments.com profit split inside Residential. The split is triangulated from a "core network 47% margin" web proxy and subtraction, never disclosed, yet the entire "one franchise funding an unproven bet" reading and the residential margin path turn on it. Monitor: any brand-level segment-profit disclosure; the residential margin trajectory as Homes.com losses roll off.

  6. The quantified guidance hit-rate scorecard. The internal/external/base-rate diff by driver was built (Section 14), but a quantified guided-range-versus-actual hit-rate scorecard was not, because the OC-Call-extraction guidance pipeline was blocked by a broken external config; the internal-expectations read was reconstructed from the 24 archived transcripts and EDGAR releases directly. Monitor: guided-range-versus-actual by metric over coming quarters.

  7. The two live post-10-K regulatory actions are flagged but not sized. The June 2026 CRE antitrust suit against CoStar and the brokerages and the FTC's Zillow/Redfin rental case are named in Sections 4, 6, and 18, but their potential remedies, financial exposure, and timelines are not underwritten. Monitor: case dockets, any settlement terms, CoStar quarterly renewal.

  8. The 2026 international investment build is not isolated from the Homes.com loss. The roughly 1,031-headcount investment list (CoStar Australia and France, Europe LoopNet, Australia and U.K. Homes.com, lender origination) is a named 2026 margin drag, but a reader cannot separate how much of the guided 2026 margin trough is Homes.com rolling off versus international just ramping, because the post-recast reporting no longer splits geography. Monitor: any geographic revenue or margin disclosure under the new segment basis.

  9. The pre-2023 product-segment margin history and Apartments.com retention series. Prior periods were recast to the two product segments only back to FY2023, so the high-30s Commercial margin record rests on three years, not a decade; and no net-revenue-retention or cohort series exists for Apartments.com, so whether the cited 99% monthly renewal masks ARPU erosion under the lower-end Rent.com mix is unresolved. Monitor: recast segment history if extended; Apartments.com ARPU and renewal disclosure.

  10. Granular debt-covenant and dilution-sensitivity detail. Dedicated capitalization and risk workpapers were produced (Sections 12 and 18). The structure is simple — one $1.0 billion note due 2030, an undrawn $1.1 billion revolver, net cash — so the residual gap is small: the covenant-headroom math and a maturity-by-maturity refinancing-cost sensitivity are not separately tabulated beyond the single-note stack. Monitor: SBC and net-share-count trajectory against the buyback, any new debt issuance.

The single highest-leverage monitoring KPI across the whole company is Residential Adjusted EBITDA versus the Q2 2026 breakeven guide, because it tests at once the Homes.com investment glide path, the path to positive segment EBITDA, and the credibility of the medium-term margin targets.
