1. Business description, model & unit economics

Copart runs a two-sided vehicle remarketing network.▪ Sellers are led by insurers, which supplied 81% of FY2025 processed vehicles; other sellers include rental, fleet, finance, dealer, charity, municipal, and individual channels.▪ Buyers include dismantlers, rebuilders, dealers, exporters, repair licensees, and the public. No customer represented more than 10% of FY2023-FY2025 consolidated revenue (CPRT FY2025 10-K, Item 1).

2. Key technologies & technical differentiators

No single Copart product is unavailable elsewhere; the advantage is that assignment, imaging, title, bidding, settlement and seller status run through one connected system.▪ VB3 aggregates global auction demand; G2 and APIs manage assignment, imaging, inventory, title, bidding, settlement, and seller status; Copart Access exposes the workflow to insurers; Title Express coordinates title and loan-payoff work; Co.ai and Total Loss Express support pre-auction total-loss decisions; IntelliSeller supports reserve, counterbid, and rerun decisions; C360 improves remote condition inspection (CPRT FY2025 10-K, Item 1).

3. Competitive advantages & moat assessment

Conclusion: Copart has a strong and durable—but not absolute—competitive advantage because insurer workflow, difficult-to-site physical capacity, and global buyer liquidity reinforce one another and have sustained high returns through multiple cycles.▪

Management claims: Copart attributes advantage to insurer relationships, owned/controlled yard capacity, title expertise, technology, and buyer breadth. The company reports 81% insurer-supplied vehicles, more than 250 locations, and buyers across more than 160 countries. Sellers are not universally bound by long-term commitments, one account was lost, and management does not disclose retention, assignment share, or matched proceeds (CPRT FY2025 10-K; July 2026 special call).

External sources: RBA independently identifies zoning/use-permit scarcity, storage capacity, insurer service levels, auction proceeds, machine vision, pricing models, and international buyers as competitive variables. That shows a scaled rival competes on the same variables. It does not show that another operator can assemble them at Copart's density. RBA/IAA is evidence for industry barriers and credible rivalry, not proof of Copart exclusivity (RBA FY2025 10-K).

Ground-up view: The reinforcing loop is insurer supply → network density and title throughput → buyer participation and proceeds → seller recovery/service → more supply.▪ Each component alone is reproducible; the coordination cost and local density of the system create the barrier. The advantage would weaken if assignment share, seller retention, relative proceeds, operating margin, and ROIC deteriorated together while capital intensity remained high.

The broad peer transaction totals above therefore test scale, not concentration.

5.2 Binding constraint, customer economics, bargaining, and circularity

7. Critical points

  1. U.S. insurance assignments are the binding near-term variable.▪ FY2026 weakness includes lower insured exposure/claims and at least one account loss; treating it as purely cyclical would omit company-specific risk.
  2. Auction proceeds and fee yield have offset volume. Q3 FY2026 revenue increased 2.1% while units declined 2.4% and ASP increased 4.6%. The durability of fee/mix if used-car values normalize is unproven.
  3. The physical network is both barrier and burden.▪ FY2025 PP&E was 77.4% of revenue and capex was 12.2%; Hurricane Helene/Milton costs totaled $56M and capacity can still bind.
  4. International has different accounting and economics. FY2025 reported operating margin was 27.3%, versus 38.1% on a net-marketplace approximation; purchase-to-consignment migration can change reported revenue and margin mechanically.
  5. Balance-sheet deployment now matters to EPS. FY2026 repurchases through April were $1,632.5M, yet future cash deployment, repurchase price, and share issuance are not governed by a public rule.

The underlying evidence comes from the CPRT Q3 FY2026 call, lines 33-93; FY2025 10-K; Q3 FY2026 10-Q; July 2026 special call, lines 241-271; and filing-based financial_history.csv. The conclusions are based on those disclosed facts rather than management characterizations.

Operating EPS growth and cash-yield effects are separate. Interest income reaches a material share of operating income because modeled liquidity accumulates. Higher yields coincide with larger bull cash balances and lower yields with smaller bear balances, widening EPS dispersion. A cash-deployment ceiling and yield-duration schedule are unavailable.

Repurchase dollars and diluted-share shrinkage are independent assumptions.▪ The model subtracts buyback cash from liquidity but does not generate shares from repurchase price, SBC, options, RSUs/PSUs, ESPP, acquisition issuance, or other activity. 17.5 Organic/acquired ambiguity and capital-return limits

Future acquisition cash has no acquired revenue, operating profit, goodwill, amortization, or return schedule.▪ Higher operating growth may be organic, acquired, or mixed; the model cannot separate it. Capex and D&A are not connected through PP&E, depreciation lives, yard capacity, or invested capital. No forward ROIC or ROIIC is calculated, and the scenarios cannot establish future return durability.
