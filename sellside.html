JPM argues the oil market is clearing via forced demand loss and heavy inventory draws after a Hormuz-related supply shock, with further price upside likely needed to balance the market.

J.P. Morgan frames this as a macro commodities flash note rather than single-stock research, arguing that a roughly 14 mbd supply disruption tied to the Strait of Hormuz closure has overwhelmed normal balancing mechanisms because Gulf spare capacity is effectively inaccessible. The note says the market has instead adjusted through massive inventory draws and forced demand loss, especially in the Middle East, Asia, and Africa, with Europe and potentially the US still needing to absorb more of the rebalancing. JPM’s key conclusion is that current prices have not yet done enough work to clear the market globally, implying further tightening and higher prices may be required.

Important Observations

  • What looks like demand destruction is, in JPM's view, largely forced demand loss caused by missing physical supply rather than normal price elasticity.
  • The usual first shock absorber, Gulf spare capacity, is effectively unavailable, making this disruption structurally harder to offset than a typical supply outage.
  • The remaining balancing gap appears too large for EM demand weakness alone, implying Europe and the US may still need to absorb more rationing via higher prices.
  • Petchem feedstocks appear to be the earliest and largest point of adjustment, suggesting downstream industrial weakness could deepen before broader transport fuels fully roll over.
  • The Americas are relatively insulated near term, but JPM flags early signs that higher pump prices and airfares are beginning to dent US driving and jet demand.

New Data

  • JPM ests global supply disruption at 9.1 mbd in March, widening to 13.7 mbd in April.
  • Observable commercial and strategic inventories drew 4.0 mbd in March and 7.1 mbd in April.
  • Observed global oil demand fell 2.8 mbd in March and is tracking down 4.3 mbd in April.
  • Middle East, Asia, and Africa account for 87% of the 4.3 mbd April demand hit.
  • Feedstock-driven weakness represents about 55% of April demand loss; jet fuel is about 11% of the loss.
  • Russian supply is said to be down 350 kbd in recent weeks, while Russia's spare capacity is only about 300 kbd.
  • US shale response is limited near term, with only 0.3-0.7 mbd likely over 3-6 months and up to 1 mbd over 6-12 months.
  • India March LPG consumption fell 13% YoY, cited as evidence of feedstock/fuel stress.

Mgmt Insights

No strong hits found.

Risks / Watch Items

  • Further price upside may be needed if Europe and the US must participate more fully in demand rationing.
  • Inventory visibility is incomplete, especially for product stocks, creating risk that actual draws are either larger or less sustainable than observed.
  • Jet demand could weaken further in May as airlines across Asia and Europe cut activity.
  • Diesel and jet markets in Europe already appear tight; additional refinery constraints could broaden product stress into gasoline during the US summer driving season.
  • A slower-than-expected non-Gulf supply response, especially from US shale, would keep the market tighter for longer.
  • Regional dislocations in Asia and Africa could intensify if cargoes continue to be diverted toward higher-bidding markets.
