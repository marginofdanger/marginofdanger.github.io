1Q26 Earnings Themes

A theme-based synthesis of recent earnings-call summaries, normalized to the calendar quarter they inform. The focus is on business signals that repeat across companies, accelerate or decelerate meaningfully, or change the mechanism behind demand, margins, or capital allocation.

Earnings themes Earnings reactions

What Looks Most Incremental

  • The consumer is bifurcated, but not simply weak. Value retail is still gaining households, Hilton and Marriott described lower/mid-market lodging as improving, and several negative reactions point to households demanding a sharper value proposition in connectivity, used cars, restaurants, and rural retail.
  • AI infrastructure is no longer a single-bottleneck story. Memory, storage, power, and server manufacturing remain the strongest bottom-up scarcity signals, but ARM and AMD add a compute-architecture layer: CPU orchestration, accelerator competition, memory bandwidth, interconnect, rack efficiency, and power availability are all becoming part of the same investment debate.
  • Software AI is bifurcating too. Workflow/data control layers are finding monetization paths; services firms are seeing procurement pressure and productivity sharing.
  • Credit improved faster than the macro narrative. Card and auto credit loss metrics got better, but lenders still sound cautious about lower-income and rate-sensitive borrowers.
  • Healthcare split into payer relief and product/volume quality risk. Centene and UnitedHealth showed cost/risk-adjustment clarity, while Zimmer, Thermo, and HCA show why beat quality, utilization, and guide bridges matter.
  • Guidance tells a cleaner story than revenue alone. Lodging, parcel, power, telecom, semi equipment, and Centene saw real raises or positive payer repricing; airlines, Nike, housing, Domino's, Spotify, and some healthcare tools/hospitals carried more caveats.

Portfolio-manager overlay: what the external context says

The outside data do not overturn the bottom-up read; they make it more investable. The macro is not a simple recession/boom call. It is a nominally resilient economy with persistent inflation, softer rate-sensitive demand, and a capital cycle increasingly pulled toward AI, power, and data infrastructure. That argues for separating exceptional businesses with pricing power and duration from businesses merely showing cyclical rebound or price-funded demand.

Macro contextBEA reported Q1 real GDP +2.0% SAAR and real final sales to private domestic purchasers +2.5%, but also said consumer spending decelerated while investment accelerated. March PCE looked hot in dollars, with PCE +0.9% nominal, but real PCE only +0.2% and the saving rate 3.6%. The portfolio read is: demand is alive, but pricing and income mix matter more than unit volume.
ConsumerCensus retail sales were +1.7% m/m and +4.0% y/y in March, with nonstore retail +10.1% y/y, while Conference Board expectations stayed weak at 70.9 and NFIB optimism fell to 95.8. That supports the company-level split: Walmart/Dollar Tree/value convenience can win share, Marriott/Hilton can see selective travel normalization, but undifferentiated discretionary and financing-sensitive categories need a sharper value proposition.
CreditThe Fed's April SLOOS said banks left credit-card and auto standards basically unchanged, but demand weakened for credit-card, auto, and other consumer loans; C&I standards tightened modestly and NDFI standards tightened over the past year. That squares with Ally/Capital One/Synchrony showing improving loss metrics while Wells Fargo, Tractor Supply, CarMax, and Charter still describe a consumer sensitive to rates, fuel, and monthly payments.
AI capexThe external AI evidence is unusually strong. Microsoft said its AI business exceeded a $37B annual run rate, up +123% y/y; Amazon reported AWS +28% to $37.6B and an AWS AI run rate above $15B; Alphabet reported Google Cloud +63% to $20.0B with backlog above $460B. This makes the GEV/STX/SNDK/MU/CLS/VRT evidence look less like one-off supplier exuberance and more like a real capital-cycle pull-through.
ScarcityIEA expects global data-center electricity consumption to reach around 945 TWh by 2030, with accelerated-server electricity demand growing about 30% annually. Deloitte's 2026 semiconductor outlook frames the same bottleneck from the chip side: global semi sales near $975B, generative-AI chips approaching $500B, and memory near $200B, with consumer memory shortages and major price spikes as AI pulls capacity into high-value workloads. The investable read is scarcity pricing in memory, power, cooling, and interconnect.
Quality filterThe Buffett/Hohn part says prefer businesses that can compound because they control scarce assets, customer relationships, data, workflow, or mission-critical infrastructure. The Druckenmiller part says the biggest changes happen when investors begin underwriting a new capital cycle. The best candidates in this report are where both are true: pricing power plus reinvestment runway, not merely a low multiple or a one-quarter beat.
External triangulation: BEA GDP; BEA PCE; Census retail sales; Conference Board confidence; NFIB; Fed SLOOS; Microsoft IR; Amazon IR; AWS AI commentary; Alphabet SEC filing; IEA Energy and AI; Deloitte semiconductor outlook.

How I am ranking what is interesting

I am using two lenses. The first is extract-first: let the transcripts surface repeated or unusually large business changes. The second is reaction-first: use the earnings move as a prompt, then go back to the extract and ask what investors were actually reassessing.

Bottom-upHighest weight goes to things that change the mechanism, not just the level: new pricing power, allocation instead of normal selling, explicit capacity constraints, surprising cohort commentary, guidance changes, margin step-changes, deferrals, or a TAM expansion that management can quantify.
Top-downLarge stock moves are treated as a search prompt. If a stock moves sharply on a small beat, a miss, or mixed headline numbers, I look for the non-obvious driver: guide quality, order book, margin bridge, credit trend, management tone, backlog duration, or a risk the headline metrics hide.
SynthesisThe strongest ideas are where both lenses agree. The most useful questions often come when they disagree, such as a strong text narrative with a negative stock reaction, or a big positive stock reaction despite an EPS miss.
Practical rule: a datapoint gets promoted when it is large, repeated, surprising versus category expectations, or confirmed by the earnings reaction. A company-specific anecdote gets demoted when it cannot be tied to a broader mechanism or when the extract is not the actual earnings report.

Earnings reaction cross-check: where large moves deserve more work

The Bloomberg/XBBG appendix now covers 144 companies from the local extract universe and requested stock-reaction-only checks, with 143 Bloomberg reported-earnings records returned. The median EPS surprise was about +4.1%, while the median first-close reaction was roughly flat at +0.1%, so investors were not simply rewarding beats. Before-market releases use that day's close; after-market releases use the next trading-day close. That makes the largest moves useful: they identify where the call probably contained a more important signal than the headline EPS and revenue surprise.

GEV1d: +13.8%

Bloomberg EPS missed consensus by about -56.8%.

The driver was not the quarter's EPS. It was orders +71%, backlog rising to $163B, higher power-equipment pricing, a large FCF guide raise, and data-center orders already exceeding all of FY25. The reaction confirms that investors were repricing power as an AI capacity bottleneck.

GNRC / CNC1d: +16.5% / +14.0%

These two large positive moves added themes I would have underweighted from keywords alone.

Generac points to backup/distributed power as an AI/grid bottleneck; Centene points to payer upside from medical-cost execution plus Marketplace risk-adjustment mechanics.

ARM / AMD1d: +2.1% / +23.4%

ARM reported 4Q26 EPS +2.7%, revenue +1.3%, and FY EPS revision +2.2%; AMD reported 1Q26 EPS +6.8%, revenue +3.7%, and FY EPS revision +8.0%.

AMD's reaction was one of the largest positive moves in the set, which makes AI compute a confirmed market signal rather than just a thematic extrapolation. ARM's reaction was smaller, but the combination of reported-results data and its local AGI CPU event extract moves the read-through away from a GPU-only lens and toward heterogeneous compute: CPUs coordinating inference, accelerators doing dense math, and the full rack determining delivered performance.

CHWY1d: +13.3%

The move came despite a small EPS miss and roughly in-line revenue.

That points to margin and productivity as the real story. The extract points to adjusted EBITDA growing roughly 3x revenue growth and AI savings moving from low tens of millions in 2026 to more than $50M annualized in 2027.

ALLY1d: +8.1%

EPS was +19.2% versus consensus, even as revenue missed.

The read is credit and funding relief: retail auto net charge-offs improved to 1.97%, NIM guidance was held, and management's credit guide did not deteriorate despite a cautious consumer backdrop.

UNH1d: +7.0%

EPS was +10.0% and revenue +2.3% versus consensus.

Investors rewarded clarity that Medicare Advantage pricing and membership discipline are working: pricing near 10% against 7-8% trend, deliberate membership trade-down, and OptumHealth operational improvement from clinical management and lower admissions/SNF use.

ZBH1d: -10.6%

EPS was +12.4% and revenue +0.8% versus consensus.

This is a classic quality-of-beat signal. Organic constant-currency sales growth was only +2.9%, full-year organic growth guidance stayed +1% to +3%, EPS was raised only about $0.10 despite a $0.20 tariff benefit, and Q2 operating margin was guided down about 200 bps.

TMO1d: -9.2%

EPS was +3.8% and revenue +1.5% versus consensus.

The negative reaction points to the organic-growth quality of the raise: Q1 organic growth was only +1%, the full-year organic guide remained +3% to +4%, and the headline revenue raise was helped by Clario. China, academic/government, and the second-half ramp remain the debate.

BABA1d: -7.1%

EPS was -43.4% and revenue -1.7% versus consensus.

The text contains a real AI/cloud acceleration story: cloud revenue +35% and AI revenue triple-digit for the tenth straight quarter. The reaction says investors cared more about the cost of that strategy: adjusted EBITDA down -57%, free cash flow compression, and core commerce pressure.

CHTR / KMX1d: -25.5% / -15.1%

These were among the largest negative moves in the set.

The common thread is affordability/substitution rather than a clean recession call. Charter's issue was top-of-funnel broadband consideration, not churn; CarMax stabilized units by giving up GPU and pausing buybacks. Both reactions point to customers demanding sharper value propositions when financing, competition, or replacement cycles are unfavorable.

SPOT / DPZ / TSCO1d: -12.4% / -8.8% / -11.7%

All three had plausible strategic stories, but the stock reactions shifted the focus toward current margin translation and guidance.

Spotify's ad gross margin and AI/cloud reinvestment, Domino's lowered U.S. same-store-sales guide and value-promo intensity, and Tractor Supply's trip-frequency/pet drag against a 2% comp breakeven threshold all mattered more than the broad strategy narrative.

W / SGI1d: -12.8% / -11.8%

These are useful process checks.

The available extracts skew toward fireside or strategy-day style commentary rather than the actual earnings report, so a positive long-term narrative should not override a negative earnings reaction without finding the company-specific driver.

Consumer: value-seeking broadened, but lower-end travel improved

The interesting consumer read is not one-dimensional. Dollar stores and Walmart still show households managing budgets, but lodging companies gave some of the cleanest commentary that lower and middle income travel demand improved. That matters because it is a different signal from the old "luxury is resilient" frame: experiences may be recovering further down the income ladder even while rate-sensitive goods and big-ticket categories remain stuck.

HLT1d: -2.7%

System-wide RevPAR was +3.6%, above the high end of guide; business transient RevPAR improved 4 points versus Q4, group was +4.3%, and leisure was +3.5%.

The texture was more useful than the beat: management described a "C-shaped economy" in which demand was moving from luxury and upper-upscale into mid and lower chain scales as the middle class got back into the game.

MAR1d: +1.3%

U.S./Canada RevPAR was +4.0%; select-service RevPAR inflected to +3.5% from a weak Q4.

Marriott called the strength broad across leisure, group, business transient ex-government, and chain scales. In Q&A, the explanation was concrete: domestic/drive-to travel, higher tax refunds, low supply, and travel-over-goods behavior, including among lower-income consumers.

DG1d: -6.1%

Same-store sales were +4.3% and Value Valley grew +17.6%.

This is still the budget-pressure side of the consumer: traffic is healthy when the format gives shoppers a very explicit value proposition.

DLTR1d: +6.4%

Comp was +5%, households rose +6.5M in Q4, and discretionary comp +6.2% outpaced consumables +3.6%.

That is more interesting than a defensive grocery-like read. It says consumers are not only trading down for staples; they are also willing to spend on small-ticket discretionary when the price architecture works.

WMT1d: -1.4%

Ecommerce grew +20%, marketplace grew +20%, marketplace SKUs reached roughly 500M, and Spark baskets were about 35% larger.

The read-through is value plus convenience, with ads, marketplace, and services improving the profit mix. Walmart is not just a defensive consumer share-taker; it is becoming a scale platform for budget-conscious demand.

FIVE1d: +9.9%

All districts and income cohorts were positive.

This fits the same pattern as Dollar Tree: affordability is a draw, but the demand is not purely distress. Novelty and low absolute ticket size still pull traffic.

ULTA1d: -17.9%

Comp was +5.4%, loyalty members grew +5%, and app users rose +15%.

Beauty remains a resilient small luxury, but the more important mechanism is retention: loyalty and mobile engagement make the category less exposed to generic discretionary weakness.

AXP1d: -4.3%

Spend grew +10%, revenue +11%, and more than 70% of new accounts were on fee-paying products.

Premium spending remains structurally different from the mass-rate-sensitive consumer, and AmEx is still finding customers willing to pay for membership value.

WFC1d: -5.7%

Wells Fargo said upper-income consumers remained resilient while lower-income consumers were more exposed to rates and energy.

The gas-price comment was specific: gasoline moved to about 7% of debit-card spend and 5% of credit-card spend, up from 6% and 4%. That creates a natural offset against other categories in the back half.

SBUX1d: +7.8%

Comp was +7% and transactions improved by 4 points.

The read is brand-specific frequency recovery, not generic restaurant traffic. It is a useful reminder that execution can overwhelm category averages in consumer services.

AAL / DAL1d: +2.4% / +3.8%

American revenue grew +10.8% and premium paid load factor was roughly 10 points above 2019; Delta revenue grew +9.4%.

Travel preference remains intact, especially at the premium end, but airlines are more exposed than lodging to fuel and international disruption.

Watch: the confirmation would be continued lower-chain lodging strength without a credit deterioration. The break would be a reversal in Dollar Tree discretionary, Marriott/Hilton select-service, or card-loss trends.

Affordability and substitution: investors punished price-funded demand

The reaction pass added a harsher read to the otherwise resilient consumer section. The weak spots were not random: they were businesses where customers have alternatives, financing matters, or management is trying to preserve demand by giving something back through price, margin, promotions, or capital intensity. That is why the large negative moves in Charter, CarMax, Tractor Supply, Domino's, and Spotify matter even though the headline macro data does not say "consumer collapse."

CHTR1d: -25.5%

Internet net adds were a 120K loss, revenue fell -1%, adjusted EBITDA fell -2.2%, and CapEx rose to $2.9B, while mobile still added 370K lines and exceeded 12M total lines.

The important management texture was that churn and point-of-sale yield were not the core problem; the problem was getting households to consider Charter in the first place. FWA, fiber overbuild, housing, satellite, and mobile substitution are turning home connectivity into a much more active price/value market.

CMCSA / RCI1d: +7.7% / +13.6%

Comcast and Rogers were a striking contrast to Charter.

Comcast still lost 65K broadband customers, but that was 117K better YoY, wireless net adds were a record 435K, and about 40% of the residential broadband base was on simpler pricing. Rogers showed wireless net adds of 33K, retail Internet net adds of 7K, wireless margin of 65%, and cable margin of 58%. The split says convergence and pricing architecture matter as much as raw broadband category growth.

KMX1d: -15.1%

Used unit comps improved to -1.9% from -9.0% in Q3, but adjusted EPS was $0.34 versus $0.60, used retail GPU fell $207, Q1 GPU was guided down about $300 YoY, and buybacks were paused.

CarMax is one of the clearest affordability anecdotes: every FICO tier except 800+ is feeling affordability/inflation pressure, and ValuMax is now roughly 50% of inventory versus 15-20% a decade-plus ago. Unit stabilization is being bought with lower margin and broader credit reach.

TSCO1d: -11.7%

Comp was only +0.5%, below the roughly 2% comp threshold needed for fixed-cost leverage; transactions were -1%, and companion animal was a -100 bps comp drag.

This was not an all-out rural consumer break. It was narrower and more interesting: trips were consolidated, tax refunds were going to essentials/savings/debt, and dog population fell from 96M in 2023 to 94M in 2024 and roughly 92M in 2025, hitting Tractor Supply's dog-heavy pet mix.

DPZ1d: -8.8%

U.S. same-store sales were +0.9%, carryout was +2.4%, delivery was -0.3%, and 2026 U.S. SSS guidance moved from about 3% to positive low single digits.

Management's comment that competitors were copying the Domino's value playbook is useful macro texture. The company still thinks rivals cannot sustain the promos without hurting franchisee economics, but the near-term read is simple: value competition is intense enough to force guide resets even for category winners.

SPOT1d: -12.4%

Spotify had MAUs of 761M, subscribers of 293M, revenue growth of +14%, and gross margin of 33%.

Spotify's negative reaction reframes the call around ad translation and reinvestment: ad revenue grew only +3%, Q2 operating income guide was EUR630M versus EUR715M in Q1, and management is spending on marketing, cloud, and AI R&D.

Watch: this theme worsens if price/value actions stop producing traffic, if lower-credit cohorts weaken, or if ad/subscription businesses keep spending more to produce the same incremental growth. It improves if Charter-type connectivity names stabilize connects without ARPU give-up, CarMax recovers GPU, and restaurant value promotions convert to share gains rather than margin pressure.

Housing and big-ticket: turnover is frozen, so execution is the story

Housing remains one of the clearest weak macro areas. The nuance is that a frozen resale market does not mean every housing-adjacent company is equally weak. Builders are using incentives and inventory control, repair/pro remains steadier than resale-driven big-ticket, and a few furniture and contractor-platform names are taking share inside a bad category.

HD1d: +2.0%

Existing-home turnover stayed near 3% versus a normal 4-5%, and big-ticket PFRI decelerated for the eighth straight quarter.

The home-improvement read is still "locked housing," not broad recovery. The offset is that repair and pro demand can keep comps from breaking even when discretionary big-ticket remains pressured.

DHI1d: +5.8%

Orders rose +11% while completed unsold homes fell -35%. D.R. Horton lowered the top end of revenue guidance and trimmed closings by about 500.

The tone was not demand capitulation. It was a builder choosing pace, inventory, and incentives while rates remain the gating item.

PHM1d: +2.4%

Incentives rose 290 bps, and active-adult plus move-up buyers were roughly 60% of mix.

That mix matters: builders are leaning into buyers with equity and balance-sheet flexibility rather than waiting for first-time affordability to normalize.

RH1d: -14.5%

Revenue grew +8% while peers were down 8-30 points worse.

This is a brand and share-control story inside a weak furniture cycle. The macro category is not suddenly healthy; the exceptional company result is the point.

W1d: -12.8%

Wayfair moved from flat to +7% growth while the category contracted.

Online furniture can recover before the category if price, logistics, and assortment improve. Again, the read-through is share and execution more than end-market strength.

SGI1d: -11.8%

Somnigroup said mattress units are about 30% below 2021 and 11.4M below long-term trend.

That is one of the starkest normalization datapoints in the set: household formation and replacement demand have not translated into a normal durable-goods cycle.

SHW1d: -3.5%

Gross margin improved +90 bps despite weak residential demand.

Pricing, mix, and contractor/pro exposure can still defend profitability even when housing volumes are soft.

WSO1d: -4.0%

Watsco has more than 75K weekly contractor users, about $1.8B of GMV through OnCall Air, and roughly 40% of volume digital.

This is a quieter but useful read-through: contractor workflow digitization can be a margin and stickiness lever even in a rate-sensitive end market.

Watch: housing needs turnover to move first. Until then, the winners are likely to be builders with incentive flexibility, repair/pro channels, and companies taking share in weak categories.

AI infrastructure: bottlenecks are spreading beyond GPUs

The most novel read-through is not simply that AI demand is strong. It is that bottlenecks are spreading across the full system: memory and storage are moving toward allocation economics; power and backup generation are becoming priced constraints; CPU orchestration and interconnect are expanding the semiconductor opportunity beyond GPUs; and deployment capacity is becoming a gating item for revenue conversion. The external evidence raises conviction: BEA's GDP details point to information-processing equipment and software as investment drivers, while Microsoft, Amazon, and Alphabet all reported AI/cloud scale that is now large enough to change the supplier profit pool.

SNDK1d: +11.5%

Q3 FY26 / calendar 1Q26Revenue was $5.95B, up +251% YoY and +97% QoQ, while gross margin jumped from 51.1% to 78.4% in one quarter.

Management called out "extraordinary pricing acceleration", five multiyear NBM partnerships, more than one-third of FY27 BiCS supply under firm commitments, about $42B of minimum contractual revenue in three Q3 contracts, and more than $11B of guarantees across five deals. The key read is that NAND customers are accepting scarcity economics, not just paying spot price.

MU1d: -3.8%

Q2 FY26 / calendar 1Q26Revenue was $23.9B, up +196% YoY; DRAM grew +207%, NAND +169%, and gross margin reached a company-record 75%.

Micron said supply is meeting only 50-67% of key customer medium-term demand, and described memory as a "defining strategic asset" in the AI era. The HBM4 ramp for NVIDIA's Vera Rubin and 4x YoY LP DRAM content per data-center CPU make this more architectural than cyclical.

STX1d: +8.0%

Data-center revenue was $2.5B, up +55%, and nearline capacity is nearly fully allocated through CY27.

Storage is behaving like an allocation market. Seagate also lifted its long-term revenue target from low-to-mid teens growth to at least 20%, a striking change for what used to be a slower-cycle hardware category.

GEV1d: +13.8%

Orders were $18.3B, up +71%, with book-to-bill around 2x and backlog rising to $163B from $116B at spin.

Data-center orders were $2.4B in Q1, more than all of FY25. Pricing on first-half orders was 10-20 points higher per kW than Q4 2025, making power a priced bottleneck, not just a volume opportunity.

GNRC1d: +16.5%

Generac's investor-day extract framed data-center backup power as a $14B-$17B global TAM, with C&I TAM expanding from roughly $14B to $30B when data centers are included.

The underlying business texture is grid stress: management cited $1.4T of needed grid infrastructure investment over five years, load growth of +32% over the next five years versus +9% in the prior five, and a data-center backlog of about $700M booking into 2027-2028. The power theme is now centralized grid plus behind-the-meter resilience.

ARM / AMD1d: +2.1% / +23.4%

ARM's local event extract framed agentic AI as a CPU-intensity step-change: CPU cores per GW move from roughly 30M pre-agentic to about 120M, agentic queries can require 15x+ tokens per human query, CSS is already roughly 20% of royalties, and the AGI CPU step moves Arm from IP/CSS into direct silicon. AMD's reported-results check showed EPS +6.8%, revenue +3.7%, FY EPS revision +8.0%, and the strongest large-cap semi reaction in this report set.

The read-through is that AI compute is becoming a system-design market, not only an accelerator unit-volume market. The better question is which companies control a durable bottleneck: CPU orchestration, accelerator road maps, memory bandwidth, interconnect, software compatibility, or power and rack-level efficiency. ARM is architecture evidence; AMD is the earnings-reaction confirmation that investors are rewarding credible alternatives and capacity into the AI compute stack.

ALAB1d: +6.3%

Astera's conference extract points to interconnect as a new AI bottleneck: Aries was designed across 100% of NVIDIA platforms, Scorpio P-Series went from roughly zero to more than $125M of FY25 revenue, Taurus exceeded $100M, and PCIe switch TAM could move from roughly zero last year to more than $10B by 2030.

The read-through is that AI clusters are becoming networking systems. GPU count, lane speed, rack scale, signal distance, telemetry, and NVLink/Fusion-style translation bridges are becoming monetizable constraints.

VRT1d: -2.3%

Sales grew +30%, operating margin reached 20.8%, up 430 bps, and FY26 margin guide moved to 23.3%, up 290 bps YoY.

The Americas were up +44%; the second-half acceleration depends on capacity additions. That is an important pattern across AI infrastructure: demand is not the bottleneck, deliverability is.

CLS1d: -11.9%

Revenue was $4.05B, up +53%; enterprise revenue rose +130% from AI/ML compute and storage ramps.

Management said the backlog and pipeline were the strongest of the CEO's tenure and expected 2027 to add more revenue than the $6.5B layered on in 2026. That makes ODM/server manufacturing a direct AI capacity read-through.

LRCX1d: +0.1%

Lam raised its 2026 WFE outlook to roughly $140B with upside bias; NAND conversion spend was framed around $40B, mostly before the end of CY27.

The nuance is supply: NAND installed wafer capacity is still down more than 20% from prior highs. If customers need more bits, equipment has to come back.

ASML1d: -2.4%

ASML narrowed/raised 2026 revenue guidance to EUR36-40B; memory customers were sold out for the rest of the year.

Low-NA EUV capacity is planned to move from at least 60 systems in 2026 to at least 80 in 2027. This supports the memory-capex recovery rather than just a logic/foundry story.

NXP1d: +22.1%

Mixed-signal data-center revenue is expected to exceed $500M in 2026 versus roughly $200M in 2025.

That is a helpful reminder that AI infrastructure beneficiaries include power management, signal chain, and analog content, not only GPUs and memory.

Watch: the strongest confirmation would be more collateralized memory/storage agreements, power-equipment pricing holding, and WFE guide raises. The break would be hyperscaler digestion showing up first in server builds, then in memory commitments.

Software and cloud: AI is rewarding control layers and pressuring exposed services

The software read is more textured than "AI helps software." The companies with governed data, workflow ownership, identity, or distribution into enterprise systems sound advantaged. Services firms are seeing a messier version of the same trend: AI creates project demand but also automates delivery, pressures pricing, and gives procurement another lever. The long-duration question is whether AI becomes a true product expansion and pricing layer, or just another reason customers demand more output per dollar.

NOW1d: -15.3%

Subscription revenue grew +19% constant currency, above guide, and customers spending over $1M on Now Assist rose +130% YoY.

The important detail is monetization shape: roughly 50% of net-new business came from non-seat models such as tokens, infrastructure, hardware, and connectors. ServiceNow is trying to become the enterprise "AI control tower", not just a per-seat workflow vendor.

IBM1d: -9.6%

Revenue grew +8%, infrastructure grew +14%, GenAI is about 30% of backlog, and internal productivity benefit exceeds $4.5B.

IBM is a useful hybrid read: AI is both external demand and internal margin action. Mainframe/infrastructure strength also says enterprise AI budgets can land in old-stack modernization, not only cloud-native software.

BABA1d: -7.1%

Cloud revenue grew +35%, and AI revenue grew triple-digit for the tenth straight quarter.

Cloud EBITDA fell -57% because Alibaba is investing through the cycle. That is a useful contrast to U.S. software margins: cloud providers may accept near-term margin pressure to secure AI platform position.

SPOT1d: -12.4%

MAUs reached 761M, up +12% constant currency, subscribers were 293M, revenue grew +14%, gross margin was 33%, and AI DJ had 94M subscribers using it.

The negative stock reaction keeps the analysis honest: ad-supported revenue grew only +3%, ad gross margin declined YoY because engagement/content cost outran ad revenue, and Q2 operating income guidance stepped down as marketing, cloud, and AI R&D rise. AI product engagement is real, but investors still demanded proof of monetization and operating leverage.

ADBE1d: -8.9%

Adobe is monetizing AI through subscription, freemium, and outcome/consumption paths, with AEM, AEP, AgenticWeb, LLM Optimizer, and Brand Concierge broadening the surface area.

The read-through is that AI-mediated discovery may become a marketing budget category. Adobe is trying to sell the control layer for brand assets, agent traffic, and content workflows.

SPGI1d: -0.9%

S&P Global cited demand for AI-ready data distribution.

This is one of the cleaner "data is infrastructure" reads: proprietary, permissioned, enterprise-ready data becomes more valuable as model interfaces proliferate.

MCO1d: +1.6%

Moody's highlighted private-credit data and distribution into ChatGPT Enterprise.

The specific read-through is not only AI search. It is that high-value financial data providers can turn model distribution into a channel while keeping entitlement and governance controls.

EFX1d: -10.0%

Equifax beat on revenue and margin, framed mortgage as a 20%+ framework, and produced +75 bps of ex-FICO margin expansion.

Management held guidance despite the beat because of Iran uncertainty and said absent that uncertainty it would have raised. That makes the call a useful signal of underlying data demand with macro caution layered on top.

ACN1d: +4.3%

Bookings remained large, but the quarterly bookings and consulting tone were less clean.

Accenture is the offset to the control-layer winners: AI can create transformation demand while also compressing delivery economics and giving clients reason to scrutinize rates.

INFY1d: -4.0%

Infosys had a growing large-deal pipeline, but procurement pressure and AI productivity offsets weighed on pricing.

The read-through is that AI value is being negotiated. Services vendors may not keep all productivity gains if customers can see the automation benefits.

Watch: non-seat consumption, AI data distribution, and platform attach should confirm the advantaged software cohort. Consulting price pressure and slower discretionary projects would show AI acting as a deflationary force in services.

Credit and financials: losses improved, but lenders still respect the lower-end risk

Credit was better than feared across several consumer lenders, but the tone was not euphoric. The useful macro read is that delinquencies and charge-offs can improve while management still treats lower-income and rate-sensitive borrowers cautiously. The Fed's bank survey supports that nuance: standards were broadly stable in cards and autos, but demand weakened. Financial activity also broadened: trading, hedging, and deal-pipeline commentary improved even without a fully open capital-markets backdrop.

COF1d: -3.0%

Card charge-offs were 5.1%, down 109 bps YoY, and delinquencies were 3.7%, down 55 bps.

Capital One described domestic card delinquencies as better than seasonal, but was "not ready to declare divergence". That caution is exactly the texture: credit is improving, but management is not extrapolating aggressively.

ALLY1d: +8.1%

Retail auto net charge-offs improved to 1.97%; NIM guidance stayed 3.60-3.70% and NCO guidance stayed 1.80-2.00%.

Ally's guide implies an exit at or above the high end of NIM despite fewer cuts. That is a better liability/asset-pricing story than the headline auto-volume backdrop would suggest.

SYF1d: -1.2%

Purchase volume grew +5% with better delinquencies and net charge-offs.

Private-label credit is often a good stress signal. Synchrony improving on both volume and losses argues against a broad consumer-credit break.

AXP1d: -4.3%

AmEx credit remained below 2019 levels while spend and fee-card acquisition stayed strong.

This confirms the premium split: the consumer with balance-sheet flexibility is still spending and still low-loss.

BAC / JPM1d: BAC +1.8%JPM -0.8%

Bank of America revenue grew +7%, EPS grew +25%, and NII grew +9%; JPMorgan delivered 23% ROTCE.

Large-bank earnings power is still robust when deposit betas, fee activity, and credit costs are not all moving against them at once.

WFC / PNC1d: WFC -5.7%PNC +0.4%

Wells Fargo closed its final consent order and auto originations more than doubled; PNC still points toward a 3% NIM path.

The regional and money-center read is gradual profitability improvement rather than a sudden loan-growth boom.

CME1d: +0.5%

ADV rose +22% to 36.2M contracts, with commodities up +38%.

Hedging demand remains high. That is a macro uncertainty signal, but also a revenue tailwind for market infrastructure.

GS1d: -1.9%

Goldman cited $150B of lead M&A assignments.

Capital markets are not fully open everywhere, but the backlog of strategic activity is rebuilding.

Watch: consumer credit is most convincing if charge-offs keep falling while purchase volume holds. The financial-activity confirmation would be M&A assignments converting into announced deals and financing volumes.

Capacity discipline: operators are protecting scarcity rather than chasing volume

A recurring theme across transport, aerospace, and logistics was that management teams are not blindly adding capacity to chase demand. Some of this is forced by supply constraints, fuel, geopolitics, or network resets. But the broader macro read is margin discipline: scarce aircraft, constrained networks, and rational parcel capacity are being managed as profit levers.

AER1d: +0.7%

AerCap expects aircraft shortages through the end of the decade.

That supports lessor pricing and keeps airlines from returning to undisciplined capacity growth too quickly.

BA1d: +5.5%

Boeing is moving 737 output toward 47/month and later 52/month.

Supply recovery matters, but the aircraft shortage is not solved quickly. That gives the airline and lessor ecosystem a longer scarcity runway.

GE1d: -5.6%

Orders rose +87%, and commercial-services backlog was above $170B.

The aftermarket and installed-base cycle remain strong. Aviation profitability is not just new deliveries; it is service intensity on a constrained global fleet.

UAL1d: -7.3%

Capacity for 3Q/4Q was revised to flat to +2%, about 5 points below the prior plan, and FY EPS range widened to $7-$11.

United said there was "no demand destruction visible" in bookings, but fuel and geopolitics force discipline. Management is cutting marginal capacity such as off-peak and red-eye flights unless yields can carry the cost.

LUV1d: -3.8%

FY26 capacity moved to roughly 2%, the low end of the prior 2-3% range, and management did not update the $4 EPS path because of fuel volatility.

This was caution rather than a volume collapse. The industry is increasingly unwilling to fly uneconomic capacity just to protect share.

UPS1d: -4.0%

UPS is absorbing about 500K fewer Amazon pieces per day, closing 23 buildings, and using driver buyouts while healthcare revenue reached $3B.

Mix and network simplification matter more than headline package growth. The company is shrinking lower-quality volume and reallocating toward specialized logistics.

FDX1d: +2.6%

FedEx raised revenue growth guidance to +6-6.5% and adjusted EPS to $19.30-$20.10 from $17.80-$19.00.

Network 2.0 savings, B2B share gains, and better mix make FedEx the cleaner parcel execution story in the set.

UNP1d: +8.8%

Union Pacific cut trains 24% since 2019 while adding 113K carloads.

Rails are an operating-leverage story if volumes hold: fewer assets moving more freight is the simple version of the productivity thesis.

Watch: if fuel remains higher, airlines' ability to pass through price will matter more than headline demand. In logistics, the key is whether network savings can offset soft package mix.

Healthcare: payer relief, utilization friction, and guide quality split investor response

Healthcare was not one macro story either. Investors rewarded payer names where cost trend, risk adjustment, or benefit-cost repricing became clearer, but punished cases where beat quality, utilization timing, or organic growth looked less clean. Patient affordability is still visible in delayed care and exchange mechanics, while procedure growth, diagnostics automation, and HSA growth point to pockets of strong structural demand.

CNC1d: +14.0%

Adjusted diluted EPS was $3.37, about $0.50 above expectations, and FY26 adjusted EPS guidance moved to more than $3.40 from more than $3.00.

The texture was payer mechanics, not just a beat: Medicaid HBR improved 50 bps YoY to 93.1%, Medicare HBR was 84.9%, Marketplace membership was about 3.58M, and Wakely data suggested membership loss after APTC changes was less severe than expected in nearly every market. Management still carried a cautious Marketplace pretax margin guide around 3%, leaving risk-adjustment upside to be corroborated.

HQY1d: +1.1%

HealthEquity added more than 1M HSAs while U.S. jobs grew only 181K; HSA assets were above $36B, up +14%, across 17.8M accounts.

The unusually strong account growth makes healthcare savings behavior a clean affordability and benefits-design read-through.

CYH1d: -14.0%

Elective surgeries fell -2.2%; high-deductible commercial and exchange patients delayed care.

This is direct evidence that out-of-pocket burden still changes utilization, even when the broader economy is not in recession.

UNH1d: +7.0%

UnitedHealth priced Medicare Advantage against roughly 7-8% trend and expects MA membership down about 1.3M.

The macro read is benefit-cost repricing, not simple demand weakness. The company raised its full-year earnings view while choosing margin repair over membership growth.

HCA1d: -8.8%

Revenue grew +4.3%, adjusted EBITDA grew about +2%, and management reaffirmed FY26 guidance, but Q1 had a combined adjusted EBITDA drag of roughly $180M from seasonal volume/storm and supplemental-payment timing.

The call is a good example of guide-quality skepticism. Management described the Q1 drag as temporal, pointed to February/March volume rebound, kept the next three quarters' volume growth expectation at 2-3%, and said Florida DPP upside was not in the guide. The stock reaction suggests investors still wanted cleaner utilization and EBITDA conversion.

ISRG1d: +3.9%

Procedures grew +17%, and SP procedures grew +68%.

Procedure demand and robotic adoption remain strong even where patient affordability is uneven. Innovation can produce volume growth that macro affordability alone would not predict.

ZBH1d: -10.6%

Sales grew +9.3%, with tech alone up +30%.

Medtech acceleration is more innovation and penetration than macro volume alone.

DGX1d: +4.5%

Revenue grew +9.2%, AD-Detect more than doubled, and AI customer-service productivity improved +40%.

Diagnostics is a data and automation story as well as a utilization story.

DHR / TMO1d: -0.5% / -9.2%

Danaher biotech grew +7% and bioprocessing was high-single-digit, while Thermo Fisher was more mixed.

Life-science tools are improving, but not with the clarity of procedure-driven medtech or diagnostics automation.

Watch: affordability pressure should show up first in elective-care deferral and payer benefit design. The upside confirmation is procedure growth staying high while life-science tools broaden.

Profitability and capital allocation: productivity is bridging mixed demand to strong EPS

Several companies are not waiting for a perfect demand backdrop. They are using productivity, loyalty, lower acquisition cost, AI savings, and buybacks to convert uneven revenue into EPS growth. This is the corporate-profitability side of the macro read: management teams are still finding levers when volume is mixed.

PG1d: +1.7%

Organic sales grew +3%, with volume +2 and price +1; productivity offset 330 bps of pressure.

Staples are no longer getting the same easy pricing, so productivity is doing more of the margin work.

ACI1d: -3.0%

Albertsons loyalty members exceeded 51M, up +12%, and the productivity target rose to $2B over three years from $1.5B.

Food retail is using loyalty, data, and cost actions to offset pharmacy and inflation pressure.

VZ1d: +1.6%

Verizon delivered the first positive Q1 postpaid phone adds in 13 years, and March acquisition/retention cost was down 35% versus end-Q4.

Profitability came from commercial discipline as much as subscriber growth. The guide raise shows management is letting better cost discipline flow through.

CHWY1d: +13.3%

Chewy guided adjusted EBITDA growth at roughly 3x revenue growth, with AI savings scaling from low tens of millions in 2026 to $50M+ annualized in 2027.

This is one of the clearer examples of AI as a cost and productivity bridge, not just a product-revenue story.

C1d: +2.6%

Citigroup repurchased $6.3B.

Large-bank capital return remains aggressive where capital ratios allow it.

FICO1d: +2.9%

FICO's $605M buyback was its largest quarter.

Proprietary data and software economics are being converted directly into per-share growth.

MGM1d: -2.0%

MGM has reduced share count by more than 50% over time.

The casino read-through is as much capital allocation as Las Vegas demand.

SNDK1d: +11.5%

SanDisk authorized a $6B buyback in the same quarter it reached net cash.

The scale of capital return underscores how abruptly NAND profitability changed. It also makes memory one of the most dramatic capital-allocation stories in the set.

Watch: productivity is most valuable when revenue is only modestly positive. The risk is that companies over-harvest costs if volume softens further.
