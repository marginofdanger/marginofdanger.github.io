Key Takeaways

  • EMIB traction is real and accelerating now — driven by CoWoS shortage, not technical preference. Customers are setting up Intel as a second 2.5D supply line. Expert pegs Intel's near-term share at 10–20%, primarily filling the gap TSMC can't satisfy.
  • Silicon, not packaging, is the gating variable. "Who owns the silicon technology has the best position to offer advanced packaging." Intel/Samsung packaging only flourishes if their silicon (Intel 18A → next nodes) lands. Without silicon, "that'll be tough for them."
  • The competitive battle is on a roadmap that doesn't yet exist. Current production is 5.5X reticle; 9X demoed at symposium; customers calling for 14X from both Intel and TSMC. 14X exists only on roadmaps today.

Topics Discussed

EMIB vs CoWoS — Structure & Scaling

  • Structural difference: EMIB is a "two-story house" (silicon bridge embedded in substrate, no interposer); CoWoS is a "three-story house" with interposer. EMIB is structurally simpler → potentially cheaper and easier to scale package size.
  • EMIB's catch: the embedded silicon bridge is the substrate supplier's burden. More bridges per substrate = harder yield; warpage control comes into play. Intel owns integration but depends on suppliers delivering yieldable substrate, "otherwise it's going to be very expensive."
  • Scaling verdict: "By just looking at package structure and complexity, EMIB can scale better" — but both are "working their butts off" toward 14X via different paths.
  • 3D clarification: 3D = die-on-die stacking; after stacking it becomes one chiplet, which still must mount on an interposer (CoWoS) or EMIB. So EMIB/CoWoS remain relevant in the 3D era.
Key Insight 14X reticle "doesn't exist today — it's just on the roadmap." Both vendors are benchmarking each other on schedule, not on shipping product. The winner of next-gen large-package share is undetermined.

Yield & Pricing

  • CoWoS yield: "fairly high 90s%" for smaller body sizes. CoPoS still in development — no yield quotable.
  • EMIB yield: expert only saw an unverified article claiming 90% — "whether it's true or not, don't know."
  • CoWoS pricing (packaging only, on top of front-end wafer): close to front-end wafer pricing; simpler modules a few thousand $/wafer, complex ones ~$10K up to $20K, driven by dies-per-module.
  • Margin: packaging GM "comparable, maybe slightly lower" than front-end. No bundling ("that word is antitrust") — wafer and packaging priced separately but negotiated together; discounts case-by-case, "level playing field is very critical."
  • Pricing outlook: most advanced CoWoS commands premium (cost + R&D); competition will force reassessment; customers continually push for price drops as tech matures.

Intel's Go-to-Market & Customers

  • Customer base today: Intel's own products are its biggest EMIB customer. Rumored fabless interest: MediaTek, NVIDIA. SK hynix reportedly developing 2.5D EMIB for HBM. "All these Tier-one folks, more or less, are looking at Intel's EMIB right now."
  • The wedge play: Step one is assembling TSMC silicon in Intel EMIB — customers don't change two things at once. Intel uses packaging as "the low-hanging fruit to get into the door first," with the ultimate goal of selling silicon-through-packaging.
  • Front-end-at-TSMC / back-end-at-Intel feasibility depends on Intel silicon quality (cost/yield/reliability) as both race 2nm → 1.6 → 1.4 → 1.0nm.
Key Insight Intel's packaging entry is a Trojan horse for its foundry: "if Intel lands on the silicon business big time, then the packaging business will flourish — no doubt." Packaging share gains are leading indicators of foundry intent, not the end goal.

TSMC Capacity, Outsourcing & Panel-Level (CoPoS)

  • CoWoS capacity: roughly doubling from this year to next year (expert flagged he didn't want to mislead on exact numbers). A ~10K-wafer line takes ~1 to 1.5 years to stand up, "maybe quicker these days."
  • CoPoS (panel-level): 310mm × 310mm panels (~wafer size, slightly larger) → better utilization, more interposers per panel. Development line being built this year; volume production ~2028; initial scale est. 5,000–10,000 panels/month.
  • Outsourcing (ASE/OSATs): only the "outsourceable portion" of the process, never the whole flow. A make-vs-buy CapEx optimization. TSMC keeps the most advanced packaging in-house — "packaging is a prerequisite now, a must-have. Without packaging, no products."
  • Allocation: declined to give NVIDIA/Google/AMD splits; bigger customers take the bigger share under shortage, but TSMC tries to satisfy everyone on a "level playing field."

Demand Dynamics

  • Visibility: TSMC has booking visibility through 2026, 2027, and into 2028.
  • CPU surge / reallocation: "Just today" MediaTek and Qualcomm dropped some smartphone device demand; AMD picked it up immediately for CPU. Freed capacity finds buyers instantly — "no shortage of customers picking that up in today's environment."

Other Competitors

  • No genuinely novel competing architecture on the horizon beyond EMIB/CoWoS. OSATs and Samsung develop "lookalike" technologies — must tread carefully on patent infringement; minute differences serve the same purpose.
  • Samsung uses an interposer approach (CoWoS-like), but "not many customers" — because it lacks silicon traction.

Expert's Assessment

  • TSMC: 8–9/10, still leading. "If they continue to maintain that technology leadership, they will continue to hold that position."
  • Intel: 6–7/10, "a close second right now." EMIB is "a great idea" with real potential as a main 2.5D option, but Intel "didn't really have a chance to work with the fabless companies before" — limited prior traction outside its own products. Must still bring up, qualify, and scale larger packages into volume.
  • Samsung: low score, "not much visibility, not much going on."
  • Core thesis — silicon is the foundation: "Silicon will determine the development of the packaging business... Silicon determines what the behavior on the packaging is." Expert expects Intel and Samsung to catch up in packaging within a few years only if their silicon businesses are realized; if silicon doesn't catch up, "that'll be tough for them."
  • Net competitive read: the expert frames Intel's near-term EMIB momentum as opportunistic (shortage-driven second-sourcing, 10–20% share) rather than a structural displacement of TSMC — TSMC keeps the most advanced packaging in-house and retains the silicon-plus-packaging integration advantage that NVIDIA and AMD have leveraged "for a long time."
  • Noted Elon Musk is working with both Samsung (Texas) and Intel — a potential silicon-demand catalyst for the laggards.
