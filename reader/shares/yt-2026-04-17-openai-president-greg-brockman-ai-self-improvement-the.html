Brockman on AI Risk, Safety, and Takeoff↪ 31:12

Another way to think about takeoff is that progress in AI goes from incremental to gathering momentum to this unstoppable march to an intelligence smarter than humans. Do you worry that there are possibilities for things to go wrong in that process?

Absolutely, yes. The way to get benefits from this technology is to really think about the risks. We invest a lot in safety and security. A good example is prompt injections. If you have an AI that's very smart and capable and hooked up to lots of tools, you want to make sure it can't be subverted by someone giving it a weird instruction. That's something we've invested in quite a lot and have really incredible results.

It's interesting to think about some of these problems where you can make analogies to humans. Humans are also susceptible to phishing attacks, deception, not understanding full context. We bring those analogies into our development process. Whenever we release or develop a model, we think about how to ensure it's aligned with people and actually helpful.

There are bigger questions about the world and economy—how does everything change, how does everyone benefit from this technology—that aren't purely technical and aren't something OpenAI alone can solve. But yes, I think quite a lot about not just pushing forward the technology but ensuring we have the positive impact it has potential for.

The worry is that this is a race. What's being done within OpenAI is also being copied by many open-source players with much fewer boundaries and protections on the safety side. You said that it takes many people getting lots of things right to be creative, but one person with bad intent to be destructive. That's where the concern lies. This is clearly a race going fast. Many of your counterparts have said if everybody agreed to stop, they'd stop. It doesn't seem like it's going to slow. Is the reward worth the risk?

I think the reward is worth the risk, but that's too coarse-grained an answer. From the beginning of OpenAI, we've asked what does a great future look like? How can this technology uplift everyone? There are two different angles here.

One is the centralization view: the way to make this technology safe is to have only one actor building it so you don't have pressures and can really get it right. That's a tough pill, but there's a lot you can approach differently, which we call resilience. Think of it as an open system where lots of players develop the technology, but it's not just about the technology—it's about building societal infrastructure that helps it go well.

Look at electricity: lots of people produce it and it has dangers and risks, but we build safety infrastructure in diverse ways—safety standards, different ways of harnessing it, scaling it, regulations at massive scales, democratized access, inspectors. There's a whole system built around the needs and proclivities of that technology.

With AI, we need this broad conversation. If the technology is going to change everything for everyone, people need to participate. It can't be done in secret by one centralized group. This has been a very core question to how this technology should play out. We really believe in this resilience ecosystem that should emerge around AI development.
